## Key Concepts

### What Are Capital Gains?
When you buy something — a mutual fund unit, a stock, gold, or property — and later sell it for more than you paid, the profit is called a **capital gain**. It's that simple. Buy at ₹100, sell at ₹150, your capital gain is ₹50.

Your trust fund is full of capital gains right now. You invested ₹1,20,000 and it's worth ₹1,41,600. The ₹21,600 difference is your unrealized capital gain. "Unrealized" because you haven't sold yet. The moment you redeem those units, the gain becomes "realized" — and that's when the tax man comes knocking.

Capital gains aren't just about mutual funds. They apply to stocks, real estate, gold, bonds, and even cryptocurrency. The tax treatment varies based on two things: **what you sold** and **how long you held it.**

### Short-Term vs Long-Term: The Holding Period
The government distinguishes between gains from quick trading and gains from patient, long-term investing. The dividing line is called the **holding period** — the time between when you bought and when you sold.

**For equity mutual funds and stocks:**
- **Short-Term Capital Gains (STCG):** Held for less than 12 months. Taxed at **20%**.
- **Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG):** Held for 12 months or more. Taxed at **12.5%** (with ₹1.25 lakh annual exemption).

**For debt mutual funds:**
- Since April 2023, all gains from debt funds are taxed at your income slab rate regardless of holding period. There's no LTCG benefit for debt funds anymore.

**For gold and property:**
- **STCG:** Held for less than 24 months (gold) or 24 months (property). Taxed at your slab rate.
- **LTCG:** Held for 24+ months. Taxed at 12.5% without indexation.

The message is clear: the government rewards patience. Hold longer, pay less tax. This is deliberate policy — India wants citizens to invest for the long term, not speculate.

### Calculating Capital Gains: A Step-by-Step
Let's calculate the capital gain for one of your trust's funds:

**Parag Parikh Flexi Cap:**
- Amount invested: ₹30,000
- Current value: ₹38,400
- Capital gain: ₹38,400 - ₹30,000 = **₹8,400**
- Percentage gain: (₹8,400 / ₹30,000) × 100 = **28%**

Now, if you redeemed this today:
- Since the trust has been investing since January 2025, each SIP unit has a different purchase date
- Units purchased more than 12 months ago → LTCG at 12.5% (with ₹1.25L exemption)
- Units purchased within the last 12 months → STCG at 20%

This is called **FIFO (First In, First Out)** — the first units you bought are considered the first units you sell. Your oldest units (most likely LTCG) are redeemed first.

### Why This Matters for Your Trust
Your trust fund was created in January 2025. It's now early 2027 (2 years later). Most of your SIP units have been held for over 12 months, qualifying for LTCG treatment. When the trust matures in 2030 (when you turn 21), nearly all units will qualify for the lower 12.5% LTCG rate with the ₹1.25 lakh exemption.

If you had been actively trading — buying and selling within months — you'd face the 20% STCG rate on every trade. That's a significant difference. A ₹8,400 gain on Parag Parikh would cost ₹1,680 in STCG tax vs potentially ₹0 under LTCG (if within the exemption).

The trust structure forces you to stay invested. That's not a limitation — it's a superpower.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Let's calculate hypothetical taxes if Kabir's trust redeemed all units today:

**Total gain: ₹21,600**
- All gains qualify as LTCG (held over 12 months)
- LTCG exemption: ₹1,25,000
- Taxable gain: ₹21,600 < ₹1,25,000 = **₹0 tax**

That's right — if Kabir redeemed today, the entire ₹21,600 gain would be **tax-free** because it falls within the annual LTCG exemption. Compare this to STCG: at 20%, the tax would be ₹4,320. By holding for the long term, Kabir saves ₹4,320 in taxes. Now multiply that by 4 more years of growth — the savings get much bigger.

Even the ICICI Corp Bond Fund with its ₹500 loss has a silver lining — that loss can be set off against gains from other funds, reducing total taxable gains further.
