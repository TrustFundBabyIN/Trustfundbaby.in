## Key Concepts

### How India Taxes Income
India follows a **progressive tax system** — the more you earn, the higher percentage you pay. This is managed through **income tax slabs**. Think of your income as water filling buckets. The first bucket (up to ₹3 lakh) has no tax. The next bucket (₹3-7 lakh) is taxed at 5%. And so on. You don't pay the highest rate on your entire income — only on the portion that falls in that bracket.

For the financial year 2025-26, the **new tax regime** (which is now the default) has these slabs:

| Income Slab | Tax Rate |
|---|---|
| Up to ₹3,00,000 | 0% (No tax) |
| ₹3,00,001 – ₹7,00,000 | 5% |
| ₹7,00,001 – ₹10,00,000 | 10% |
| ₹10,00,001 – ₹12,00,000 | 15% |
| ₹12,00,001 – ₹15,00,000 | 20% |
| Above ₹15,00,000 | 30% |

So if someone earns ₹10 lakh, they don't pay 15% on the whole amount. They pay 0% on the first ₹3 lakh, 5% on the next ₹4 lakh, and 10% on the remaining ₹3 lakh. The total tax works out to much less than 15% of ₹10 lakh.

There's also a **standard deduction** of ₹75,000 in the new regime, and a **rebate under Section 87A** that makes income up to ₹7 lakh effectively tax-free for salaried individuals. That's a big deal — it means if you earn ₹7 lakh or less, you might pay zero tax.

### Old Regime vs New Regime
India has two tax systems running side by side. The **new regime** (introduced in 2020, made default in 2023) has lower rates but fewer deductions. The **old regime** has higher rates but allows many deductions — like ₹1.5 lakh under Section 80C (for investments in PPF, ELSS, life insurance), ₹50,000 for NPS, ₹25,000 for health insurance, and more.

Which is better? It depends. If you have lots of deductions (home loan, investments, insurance), the old regime might save you more. If you don't have many deductions, the new regime's lower rates are simpler and often better. You can choose each year.

For most young earners starting out with few deductions, the new regime is usually the way to go. It's simpler, and you don't need to scramble to find investment receipts at tax time.

### Filing Your Tax Return
Every person whose income exceeds the basic exemption limit (₹3 lakh in the new regime) must file an **Income Tax Return (ITR)**. This is a form you submit to the IT Department declaring your income, taxes paid, and claiming refunds if you've paid excess tax.

Even if your income is below the limit, filing is good practice. It creates a financial record that helps when you apply for loans, visas, or credit cards later in life. Filing is done online at the Income Tax portal (incometax.gov.in). There are different ITR forms depending on your income type — ITR-1 for simple salary income, ITR-2 for investments and capital gains, and so on.

The deadline for filing is usually July 31st for individuals (not requiring audit). Late filing is allowed until December 31st, but with a penalty of up to ₹5,000.

### Tax Deducted at Source (TDS)
Before you even see your money, the government often takes its cut. **TDS (Tax Deducted at Source)** means the person paying you — your employer, your bank, your mutual fund — deducts tax and sends it to the government on your behalf.

For example, your bank deducts 10% TDS on fixed deposit interest if it exceeds ₹40,000 per year (₹50,000 for senior citizens). Your employer deducts TDS from your salary every month based on your projected annual income. When you sell mutual fund units, the fund house deducts TDS on gains.

At the end of the year, you calculate your actual tax liability. If TDS deducted is more than what you owe, you get a **refund**. If it's less, you pay the balance as **self-assessment tax**. The ITR is where this reconciliation happens.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Right now, your trust fund's gains of ₹21,600 aren't taxable because trusts have their own tax rules and exemptions. But imagine you were investing on your own and earned ₹21,600 in capital gains. If these were long-term equity gains (held over 1 year), you'd get ₹1.25 lakh as exemption — so ₹21,600 would be completely tax-free. If they were short-term (held under 1 year), you'd pay 20% = ₹4,320 in tax. That's why understanding income tax and capital gains timing matters. Your trust fund is growing tax-efficiently right now — you'll learn why in Module 4.

## Quiz
1. In the new tax regime, what is the tax rate for income between ₹3-7 lakh? — A) 0% B) 5% C) 10% D) 20% (Answer: B)
2. If someone earns ₹10 lakh, do they pay 15% on the entire amount? — A) Yes, always B) No, only on the portion above ₹10 lakh C) No, each slab applies to its portion only D) No, they pay a flat 10% (Answer: C)
3. What is TDS? — A) Tax Deducted at Source B) Total Deducted Salary C) Tax Department Statement D) Temporary Deduction Scheme (Answer: A)
4. What is the standard deduction under the new tax regime? — A) ₹50,000 B) ₹75,000 C) ₹1,00,000 D) ₹1,50,000 (Answer: B)
5. What happens if more TDS is deducted than your actual tax liability? — A) You lose the money B) You pay a penalty C) You get a refund when you file ITR D) It's adjusted next year automatically (Answer: C)
