# Module 10: Government Schemes & Savings Bonds
**Year:** 5 (Age 17)
**Month:** 10
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Learn about key government savings schemes available to Indian citizens
- Understand how sovereign bonds and PPF work
- Compare government schemes with mutual fund investments
- Know when government schemes make sense as part of your portfolio

## Key Concepts

### Why the Government Wants You to Save
The Indian government offers several savings and investment schemes — not out of generosity, but because citizen savings fund government spending. When you invest in a government scheme, you're essentially lending money to the government. In return, you get guaranteed returns, tax benefits, and sovereign safety (the government won't default on your money — it can always print more, though that causes inflation).

These schemes serve a dual purpose: they help citizens build wealth systematically, and they provide the government with a stable source of funding. This is why many government schemes come with tax benefits — the government incentivizes you to lend it money.

### Key Government Schemes

**Public Provident Fund (PPF):**
- **Lock-in:** 15 years (but partial withdrawals after 7 years)
- **Interest rate:** ~7.1% (compounded annually, set by government each quarter)
- **Tax benefit:** Investment up to ₹1.5 lakh deductible under Section 80C. Interest and maturity are also tax-free (EEE status — Exempt-Exempt-Exempt)
- **Who should use it:** Long-term savers who want guaranteed, tax-free returns. Excellent for retirement or your child's education fund.

**Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana (SSY):**
- **For:** Girl children under 10 years old
- **Lock-in:** 21 years from opening (or until marriage after age 18)
- **Interest rate:** ~8.2% (highest among government schemes)
- **Tax benefit:** EEE status (same as PPF)
- **Who should use it:** Parents of girl children for long-term education or marriage expenses

**National Savings Certificate (NSC):**
- **Lock-in:** 5 years
- **Interest rate:** ~7.7% (compounded annually, paid at maturity)
- **Tax benefit:** Investment up to ₹1.5 lakh deductible under Section 80C
- **Who should use it:** Conservative investors looking for guaranteed 5-year returns

**Senior Citizens Savings Scheme (SCSS):**
- **For:** Individuals above 60 years
- **Lock-in:** 5 years (extendable by 3 years)
- **Interest rate:** ~8.2% (paid quarterly)
- **Tax benefit:** Investment up to ₹1.5 lakh deductible under Section 80C
- **Who should use it:** Retirees seeking regular, safe income

**Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs):**
- **Lock-in:** 8 years (exit option after 5 years)
- **Returns:** Linked to gold price + 2.5% annual interest
- **Tax benefit:** Capital gains at maturity are tax-free. Interest is taxable.
- **Who should use it:** Investors who want gold exposure without physical storage headaches

**RBI Floating Rate Bonds:**
- **Lock-in:** 7 years
- **Interest rate:** ~8.05% (floating, resets every 6 months)
- **No tax benefit** (interest fully taxable)
- **Who should use it:** Investors who want higher-than-FD returns with government safety

### Government Schemes vs Mutual Funds
This is the million-rupee question: should you invest in government schemes or mutual funds?

**Government schemes win on:**
- Safety: Zero risk of losing principal (sovereign guarantee)
- Predictability: Fixed interest rates (except floating rate bonds)
- Tax benefits: Many schemes offer EEE status

**Mutual funds win on:**
- Returns: Equity mutual funds have historically delivered 12-15% long-term returns vs 7-8% for government schemes
- Liquidity: Most mutual funds can be redeemed within 1-3 days (except ELSS with 3-year lock-in)
- Flexibility: You can invest any amount, switch funds, set up SIPs easily

The honest answer: **you need both.** Government schemes form the stable foundation — the money you absolutely cannot afford to lose. Mutual funds are the growth engine — the money you invest for long-term wealth creation. A balanced portfolio might be 70% equity mutual funds + 20% debt/bonds + 10% government schemes.

### Bonds: Lending to the Government
When you buy a government bond, you're lending money to the government for a fixed period. In return, the government pays you interest (called a "coupon") periodically and returns your principal at maturity.

For example, a 10-year government bond at 7% means: you lend ₹1,00,000 to the government. Every year, you receive ₹7,000 in interest. After 10 years, you get your ₹1,00,000 back. Total return: ₹70,000 in interest over 10 years.

Bond prices move inversely to interest rates. When interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall (because new bonds offer higher rates, making old ones less attractive). This is why your ICICI Corp Bond Fund is down 2.8% — interest rates rose in 2025-26, hurting existing bond values. But if you hold to maturity, you still get your expected return.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Your trust fund's ICICI Corporate Bond Fund invests in bonds issued by companies (not the government, but similar principles). It's currently down 2.8% because of rising interest rates. But this fund serves an important purpose: when equity markets crash (like during COVID-19), bond funds typically hold steady or even rise. They're your portfolio's safety net.

If Kabir's trust had allocated the entire ₹5,000/month to equity funds instead of splitting into 4 funds, the returns might have been higher (say 20% instead of 18%). But the risk would also be higher. The ICICI Corp Bond portion (₹17,500 of ₹1,41,600) provides stability — ensuring that even in bad equity markets, a portion of the portfolio is protected.

Consider this: if you also invested ₹1,000/month in PPF alongside your trust fund SIP, at 7.1% for 9 years, you'd have approximately ₹1,55,000 at maturity — completely tax-free. This creates a balanced approach: mutual funds for growth, PPF for safety and tax savings.

## Quiz
1. What is the approximate interest rate on PPF? — A) 5.0% B) 6.5% C) 7.1% D) 8.5% (Answer: C)
2. What does EEE status mean for tax purposes? — A) Exempt-Exempt-Exempt (investment, interest, and maturity are tax-free) B) Easy Electronic Entry C) Estimated Effective Earnings D) Equal Earnings Entry (Answer: A)
3. Which government scheme offers the highest interest rate? — A) PPF B) NSC C) Sukanya Samriddhi Yojana D) RBI Floating Rate Bonds (Answer: C)
4. Why is the ICICI Corp Bond Fund in your trust down 2.8%? — A) Poor fund management B) Rising interest rates hurting existing bond values C) Stock market crash D) Company defaults (Answer: B)
5. What happens to bond prices when interest rates rise? — A) They rise B) They fall C) They stay the same D) They become zero (Answer: B)
