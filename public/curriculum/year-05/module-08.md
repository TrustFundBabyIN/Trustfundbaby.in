# Module 8: When to Switch Funds
**Year:** 5 (Age 17)
**Month:** 8
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand when it makes sense to switch between mutual funds
- Learn the difference between fund switching and redemption
- Know the costs and tax implications of switching
- Develop criteria for evaluating whether a fund deserves to stay in your portfolio

## Key Concepts

### Switching Is Not the Same as Redeeming
When you **redeem** a mutual fund, you sell your units and get cash in your bank account. When you **switch**, you sell units from one fund and immediately buy units in another fund — often from the same fund house. The money never hits your bank; it moves directly from Fund A to Fund B.

Switching is treated as a redemption for tax purposes (you'll still owe capital gains tax on profits), but it's operationally smoother. No waiting for money to arrive in your bank, no separate transaction to buy the new fund. It's a one-step process.

Many people confuse switching with trying to "time the market." It's not. Switching is a strategic decision based on long-term fund performance, not short-term market predictions. You switch because a fund is consistently underperforming, not because the market had a bad month.

### When Should You Consider Switching?
Not every dip calls for a switch. Markets go up and down — that's normal. Here are legitimate reasons to consider switching:

**1. Consistent underperformance:** If a fund underperforms its benchmark for 2-3 consecutive years, the fund manager might not be doing a good job. One bad year is normal. Three bad years is a pattern.

**2. Change in fund strategy:** Sometimes a fund manager leaves, or the fund changes its investment style. If your mid-cap fund suddenly starts investing heavily in large-caps, it's not the fund you signed up for.

**3. Overlap between funds:** If two of your funds hold many of the same stocks, you're not as diversified as you think. Switching one to a different category improves diversification.

**4. Life stage changes:** As you approach your goal (like age 21 for your trust), you might want to shift from aggressive equity funds to more stable debt funds. This reduces risk as the deadline nears.

**5. Fund size issues:** If a fund grows too large (AUM over ₹50,000-₹1,00,000 crore), it may struggle to find enough good investments. Performance can stagnate. Conversely, very small funds may close or merge.

### When NOT to Switch
Equally important is knowing when to stay put:

**- One bad quarter or year:** Markets correct. Fund performance dips. This is normal. Don't switch based on 3-6 months of data.

**- Another fund did better recently:** "My friend's fund returned 35%, mine only returned 21%." Chasing last year's top performer is a classic mistake. Today's winner is often tomorrow's underperformer.

**- Market crash:** During a crash, everything looks bad. Switching during a crash locks in losses. Wait for recovery.

**- Tax savings:** Sometimes the tax cost of switching (STCG at 20% or LTCG at 12.5%) outweighs the benefit. Always calculate before switching.

### The Tax Cost of Switching
Switching triggers a taxable event. When you switch from Fund A to Fund B, it's treated as:
1. You sold Fund A (capital gains tax applies on profits)
2. You bought Fund B (fresh purchase, new holding period starts)

For example, if you switch ₹51,200 from HDFC Mid-Cap (invested ₹42,000):
- Capital gain: ₹9,200
- If held over 12 months (LTCG): Tax = 12.5% on gains above ₹1.25L exemption = potentially ₹0 if total gains are within limit
- If held under 12 months (STCG): Tax = 20% = ₹1,840

That ₹1,840 is money lost to tax that could have been compounding. So switching has a real cost. Make sure the benefit of switching to a better fund exceeds this cost over your remaining investment horizon.

### Systematic Transfer Plan (STP): The Smarter Switch
Instead of switching all at once, you can use an **STP (Systematic Transfer Plan)**. This moves a fixed amount from one fund to another regularly — like a reverse SIP. For example, moving ₹10,000/month from an equity fund to a debt fund as you approach your goal.

STP has two advantages:
1. **Rupee cost averaging:** You don't sell everything at one price. You sell in tranches, averaging out market ups and downs.
2. **Reduced tax impact:** Smaller redemptions may stay within exemption limits.

As Kabir's trust approaches maturity in 2030, an STP from equity funds to debt funds (starting maybe 1-2 years before) would be a smart de-risking strategy.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Let's evaluate Kabir's funds for potential switching:

**HDFC Mid-Cap (+21.9%):** Strong performer. No reason to switch. Keep.

**SBI Small Cap (+15.0%):** Decent for a small-cap fund. Small-caps are volatile but have higher long-term potential. Keep.

**Parag Parikh Flexi Cap (+28.0%):** Star performer. Definitely keep. Consider increasing allocation.

**ICICI Corp Bond (-2.8%):** Underwater, but context matters. Bond funds were hurt by rate hikes in 2025-26. If rates stabilize or fall, this fund should recover. However, if it continues to underperform for another year, consider switching to a shorter-duration bond fund or a gilt fund.

**Verdict:** No switches needed right now. All four funds are doing their jobs. Review again in 6-12 months.

## Quiz
1. What is the difference between switching and redeeming? — A) No difference B) Switching moves money between funds without going to your bank C) Redeeming is faster D) Switching is tax-free (Answer: B)
2. For how many consecutive years should a fund underperform before you consider switching? — A) 1 quarter B) 1 year C) 2-3 years D) 5 years (Answer: C)
3. What is STP? — A) Short-Term Profit B) Systematic Transfer Plan C) Switch Tax Plan D) Standard Transfer Protocol (Answer: B)
4. If you switch HDFC Mid-Cap units invested at ₹42,000 now worth ₹51,200, what is the capital gain? — A) ₹42,000 B) ₹51,200 C) ₹9,200 D) ₹93,200 (Answer: C)
5. When approaching your trust's maturity, what strategy reduces risk? — A) Invest more in small-caps B) Switch all to equity C) Use STP to gradually move to debt funds D) Stop SIPs immediately (Answer: C)
