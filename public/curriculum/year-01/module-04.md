# Module 4: Setting Money Goals
**Year:** 1 (Age 13)
**Month:** 4
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand why goals make saving purposeful
- Learn the difference between short-term, medium-term, and long-term goals
- Set your first real money goal with a plan to achieve it
- Use the SMART framework adapted for personal finance

## Key Concepts

### Why Goals Matter
Saving without a goal is like running without a destination. You'll get tired and quit. But when you save FOR something — a new book, a birthday gift, or a trip with friends — every rupee you set aside feels like progress. Goals give your money direction.

Imagine telling yourself: "I'm saving ₹3,000 for a cricket bat by December." Now every time you skip buying extra samosas at the canteen, you think: "That's ₹20 closer to my bat." The goal turns sacrifice into motivation. Without the goal, you're just... not buying samosas. Boring.

### Short-Term, Medium-Term, and Long-Term Goals
Not all goals take the same amount of time. Here's a simple way to think about it:

**Short-term goals** (1-3 months): Buy a new school bag, save for a friend's birthday gift, get a new video game. These are small, immediate targets that build your saving muscle.

**Medium-term goals** (3-12 months): Save for a bicycle, buy a tablet for studies, save for a school trip. These need more patience and planning.

**Long-term goals** (1+ years): Save for a laptop, start a fund for college, build an emergency cushion. Long-term goals are where the real magic happens — they teach you discipline and delayed gratification, the same skills that will make you wealthy someday.

### SMART Goals for Money
SMART stands for Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. Here's how it works for money:

Instead of "I want to save money" (vague), try: "I will save ₹200 per month for 5 months to buy a cricket bat worth ₹1,000 by August 15th." That's Specific (cricket bat), Measurable (₹200/month), Achievable (₹200 is reasonable from pocket money), Relevant (you actually want the bat), and Time-bound (by August 15th). Write your goals down. People who write their goals down are significantly more likely to achieve them.

## Real-World Example
Meera, 13, from Jaipur, set three goals at the start of her school year:
- **Short-term:** ₹500 for a new sketch set (2 months — saved ₹250/month)
- **Medium-term:** ₹2,400 for a school trip to Agra (8 months — saved ₹300/month)
- **Long-term:** ₹5,000 for a tablet for online courses (ongoing — saving ₹500/month)

She wrote them on a sticky note on her desk. Every month, she updated the amount saved. By December, she had the sketch set, the trip was booked, and her tablet fund was at ₹2,500. Writing goals down made them real. Tracking progress made them exciting.

## Quiz
1. Saving without a goal is compared to: — A) Swimming without water B) Running without a destination C) Reading without a book D) Eating without food (Answer: B)
2. A goal to save ₹1,500 for a school event happening in 2 months is a: — A) Long-term goal B) Medium-term goal C) Short-term goal D) Impossible goal (Answer: C)
3. In SMART goals, the "A" stands for: — A) Automatic B) Achievable C) Aggressive D) Absolute (Answer: B)
4. "I want to save money" is NOT a good goal because it's: — A) Too specific B) Not measurable or time-bound C) Too easy D) Written down (Answer: B)
5. Which is a SMART money goal? — A) I'll be rich someday B) I'll save ₹300/month for 4 months for new headphones C) I'll save a lot of money D) I'll stop spending (Answer: B)
