# Module 6: Budgeting Basics
**Year:** 1 (Age 13)
**Month:** 6
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what a budget is and why it matters
- Learn the 50-30-20 rule adapted for teenagers
- Create a simple monthly budget using pocket money
- Track expenses and identify spending patterns

## Key Concepts

### What Is a Budget?
A budget is simply a plan for your money. Before the month starts, you decide how much you'll spend on what, and how much you'll save. That's it. It's not about restricting yourself — it's about being in control. Without a budget, money just... disappears. You wonder where ₹500 went and all you have to show for it is a few canteen receipts and an auto ride you barely remember.

Think of a budget like a game plan. Before a cricket match, you decide who bats first, who bowls, and what the strategy is. You don't just walk onto the field and hope for the best. A budget is your financial game plan.

### The 50-30-20 Rule (Teenager Edition)
Adults use a popular budgeting rule: 50% for needs, 30% for wants, 20% for savings. As a teenager, your version might look different since your needs are mostly covered by parents. Here's a teen adaptation:

**20% Savings:** Always save at least 20% of whatever money you receive. Non-negotiable. This builds the habit that will make you wealthy.

**50% Planned Spending:** Things you've decided to spend on — school supplies, canteen food, transport, gifts for friends. This is money with a purpose.

**30% Fun Money:** Impulse buys, treats, movies, snacks. This is your guilt-free zone. Spend it however you want, but when it's gone, it's gone until next month.

Of course, these percentages aren't rigid. The point is: divide your money into categories BEFORE you start spending. Some months you might save 40%. Other months, a friend's birthday might eat into your fun money. That's fine. The habit of dividing and planning is what matters.

### Tracking: The Secret Ingredient
A budget without tracking is like a diet without weighing yourself — you think you're doing well but you have no idea. Track your spending for just one month. Write down every single expense, even the ₹10 chai. Use a notebook, a spreadsheet, or a simple app. At the end of the month, you'll be shocked at where your money actually went.

Most people discover they spend way more on small, random purchases than they thought. ₹20 here, ₹30 there — it adds up to hundreds without you noticing. Tracking exposes these money leaks. Once you see them, you can fix them.

## Real-World Example
Kabir gets ₹800 pocket money monthly. Here's his budget:
- **Savings (20%):** ₹160 → goes into his bank account
- **Planned Spending (50%):** ₹400 → ₹200 canteen, ₹100 transport, ₹100 stationery
- **Fun Money (30%):** ₹240 → movies, snacks, impulse buys

Last month, Kabir tracked everything. He found he was spending ₹120/month on random snacks from the school canteen — items he didn't even really enjoy. By cutting that to ₹60, he freed up ₹60 more for fun money or savings. Small tweak, meaningful result. Budgeting isn't about deprivation. It's about awareness.

## Quiz
1. A budget is best described as: — A) A restriction on spending B) A plan for your money C) Something only adults need D) A bank document (Answer: B)
2. In the 50-30-20 rule, the 20% represents: — A) Fun money B) Needs C) Savings D) Bills (Answer: C)
3. If you receive ₹1,000 pocket money, how much should you save using the 50-30-20 rule? — A) ₹100 B) ₹200 C) ₹300 D) ₹500 (Answer: B)
4. Why is tracking expenses important? — A) It's required by law B) It reveals where money actually goes C) Banks need it D) It's a waste of time (Answer: B)
5. You notice you spent ₹300 on random snacks last month without realising. This is called: — A) A good investment B) A money leak C) A necessary expense D) Smart spending (Answer: B)
