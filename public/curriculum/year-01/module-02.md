# Module 2: Needs vs Wants
**Year:** 1 (Age 13)
**Month:** 2
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Distinguish between needs and wants clearly
- Recognise how advertisers blur the line between needs and wants
- Practise categorising everyday expenses
- Build awareness of impulse buying and how to avoid it

## Key Concepts

### What Are Needs?
Needs are things you genuinely cannot live without. Food, water, shelter, basic clothing, education, and healthcare — these are needs. Without them, life gets really difficult or even dangerous. In India, a family's basic needs might include rice or roti, clean drinking water, a roof over their heads, school fees, and medicine when someone falls sick.

The key test for a need: "Can I survive and function well without this?" If the answer is no, it's a need. You need lunch to concentrate in school. You need a uniform to attend classes. You need books to study. These aren't optional.

### What Are Wants?
Wants are things that make life more enjoyable but aren't essential for survival. A new pair of Nike shoes (when your current shoes work fine), the latest iPhone (when your phone still calls and texts), a fancy pizza delivery (when there's food at home) — these are wants.

Wants aren't bad! There's nothing wrong with wanting nice things. The problem starts when wants pretend to be needs. "I NEED the new PlayStation" — no, you want it. And that's okay to admit. But calling a want a need is how people spend money they don't have.

### The Grey Zone
Here's where it gets interesting. Some things sit in a grey area. Is a smartphone a need or a want? For many Indian students today, a basic phone for online classes and homework is practically a need. But a ₹1,20,000 flagship phone? That's a want. The same object can be either, depending on the version and the reason.

Food is a need. But ordering biryani from Zomato three times a week when there's perfectly good home-cooked food? That's a want dressed up as a need. Learning to spot the difference is one of the most powerful money skills you'll ever build.

## Real-World Example
It's Diwali season. Your family has ₹10,000 set aside. Needs: new school shoes (₹1,500), textbooks for next term (₹800), electricity bill (₹2,000). Wants: new clothes for Diwali (₹3,000), fairy lights and decorations (₹500), sweets and firecrackers (₹1,500), a gift for your best friend (₹700). Total needs: ₹4,300. Total wants: ₹5,700. Budget: ₹10,000. Can you cover everything? Yes — but only because you know the difference. If you'd spent ₹5,000 on designer clothes first, you might not have enough left for school shoes and books. Needs first. Always.

## Quiz
1. Which of these is a need? — A) Netflix subscription B) School lunch C) Video game D) Branded sneakers (Answer: B)
2. An advertiser says "You NEED this new phone to be cool." What's really happening? — A) They're right B) They're disguising a want as a need C) The phone is essential D) All teenagers must have it (Answer: B)
3. You have ₹500 left for the week. Your school project needs supplies costing ₹300. You also want a movie ticket for ₹200. What should you do? — A) Buy the movie ticket first B) Spend on project supplies, save the rest C) Skip both D) Borrow money for both (Answer: B)
4. A basic smartphone for online classes is best described as: — A) Always a want B) A need in today's education context C) A luxury D) A waste of money (Answer: B)
5. "Impulse buying" means: — A) Buying things after careful thought B) Buying things suddenly without thinking C) Buying only needs D) Never shopping (Answer: B)
