## Key Concepts

### What Is Compounding?
Compounding is when your money earns returns, and then those returns also earn returns. It's growth on top of growth. Think of it like a snowball rolling down a hill. It starts small, but as it rolls, it picks up more snow and gets bigger. The bigger it gets, the more snow it picks up. Eventually, it becomes massive — not because you pushed harder, but because growth feeds on itself.

Here's a simple example. You invest ₹1,000 and earn 10% in Year 1. You now have ₹1,100. In Year 2, you earn 10% on ₹1,100 (not just the original ₹1,000). That's ₹110 earned, not ₹100. In Year 3, you earn 10% on ₹1,210 — that's ₹121 earned. Each year, the amount you earn grows, even though the percentage stays the same. That's compounding. Your money is making money, and that money is also making money.

### The Rule of 72
Here's a handy shortcut. To find out how long it takes to double your money, divide 72 by the annual return rate.

- At 6% return: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years to double
- At 12% return: 72 ÷ 12 = 6 years to double
- At 15% return: 72 ÷ 15 ≈ 4.8 years to double

So if your mutual fund averages 12% per year, your money doubles roughly every 6 years. ₹10,000 becomes ₹20,000 in 6 years, ₹40,000 in 12 years, ₹80,000 in 18 years. The same ₹10,000 — no additional investment, just time and compounding.

### Why Starting at 13 Is a Superpower
This is where your age becomes your greatest advantage. Consider two people:

**Priya** invests ₹5,000 per year from age 13 to 21 (9 years), then stops. Total invested: ₹45,000.
**Rahul** invests ₹5,000 per year from age 25 to 58 (34 years). Total invested: ₹1,70,000.

Both earn 12% per year. At age 58, who has more money? Priya does — despite investing far less. Her ₹45,000 had 45 years to compound. Rahul's ₹1,70,000 had only 33 years. Time beats amount. Every single time.

This isn't a trick. It's mathematics. And it's exactly why your Trust Fund Baby account is so powerful. You're starting at 13. The compounding engine is already running.

## Real-World Example
Let's make it real. Imagine your Trust Fund Baby SIP is ₹1,000 per month, invested in a mutual fund averaging 12% annual returns.

- After 1 year: You've invested ₹12,000. Your fund is worth approximately ₹12,800.
- After 3 years: Invested ₹36,000. Fund value: approximately ₹43,000.
- After 5 years: Invested ₹60,000. Fund value: approximately ₹82,000.
- After 8 years (when you turn 21): Invested ₹96,000. Fund value: approximately ₹1,55,000.

You invested ₹96,000 but received ₹1,55,000. The extra ₹59,000? That's compounding doing its work while you studied, played cricket, and hung out with friends. Your money was working even when you weren't.
