# Module 4: Types of Mutual Funds — Equity, Debt, Hybrid
**Year:** 3 (Age 15)
**Month:** 4
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the three main categories of mutual funds: Equity, Debt, and Hybrid
- Learn the sub-categories within each type and what makes them different
- Know real Indian fund examples from major AMCs
- Understand how SEBI's categorisation rules help investors make better choices

## Key Concepts

### Equity Funds — Investing in Stocks

**Equity funds** invest primarily in shares (stocks) of companies. When you put money in an equity fund, the fund manager uses it to buy stocks of listed Indian companies — or sometimes international companies too.

The goal of equity funds is **growth**. Stocks can go up significantly over time, but they can also be volatile in the short term. Think of equity funds like planting a mango tree — it takes time to grow, there will be storms (market crashes), but once it matures, it bears fruit for decades.

Equity funds come in several sub-categories, as defined by SEBI:

- **Large Cap Funds:** Invest in India's top 100 companies by market capitalisation. These are established giants like Reliance Industries, TCS, HDFC Bank, and Infosys. Lower risk among equity funds, steady growth. Example: **ICICI Prudential Bluechip Fund**.

- **Mid Cap Funds:** Invest in companies ranked 101–250 by market cap. These are growing companies with more room to expand but also more volatility. Example: **HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund**, one of India's most popular mid-cap funds with over ₹60,000 crore in AUM.

- **Small Cap Funds:** Invest in companies ranked 251 and beyond. These are the smallest listed companies — high risk, high potential reward. Example: **Nippon India Small Cap Fund**.

- **Flexi Cap Funds:** Can invest across large, mid, and small caps with no fixed allocation. The fund manager has flexibility to shift based on market conditions. Example: **Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund**, known for its value-investing approach and some international stock exposure.

- **ELSS (Equity Linked Savings Scheme):** An equity fund that also gives you tax benefits under Section 80C. You can claim up to ₹1.5 lakh as a deduction. It has a mandatory 3-year lock-in period. Example: **Quant ELSS Tax Saver Fund**.

SEBI mandates that equity funds must invest at least 65% of their assets in equity and equity-related instruments. This ensures you're actually getting an equity fund, not a disguised fixed deposit.

### Debt Funds — Investing in Bonds and Fixed Income

**Debt funds** invest in fixed-income instruments — things like government bonds, corporate bonds, treasury bills, money market instruments, and other debt securities. Instead of buying shares of companies, the fund is essentially lending money to the government or to companies, and earning interest in return.

The goal of debt funds is **stability and regular income**. They're less volatile than equity funds but also offer lower returns. Think of debt funds like keeping money in a very advanced savings account — it earns interest, it's relatively safe, and you can access it without wild ups and downs.

SEBI defines several debt fund sub-categories:

- **Overnight Funds:** Invest in securities that mature in 1 day. The safest debt fund category, but returns are low — similar to a savings account. Example: **HDFC Overnight Fund**.

- **Liquid Funds:** Invest in securities that mature in up to 91 days. Great for parking emergency money or short-term surplus. Very low risk, slightly better returns than a savings account. Example: **SBI Magnum Liquid Fund**.

- **Short Duration Funds:** Invest in bonds with 1–3 year maturities. A balance between safety and returns. Example: **ICICI Prudential Short Term Fund**.

- **Corporate Bond Funds:** Invest in high-rated (AA+ and above) corporate bonds. Higher returns than government bonds, but with some credit risk. Example: **Aditya Birla SL Corporate Bond Fund**.

- **Gilt Funds:** Invest primarily in government securities (G-Secs). These carry no credit risk (the government won't default) but are sensitive to interest rate changes. Example: **SBI Magnum Gilt Fund**.

Debt funds are particularly useful when you need money in the short to medium term (1–3 years) or when you want to reduce the overall risk of your portfolio.

### Hybrid Funds — The Best of Both Worlds

**Hybrid funds** invest in a mix of equity and debt. They're designed to give you growth from stocks and stability from bonds, all in one fund. Think of them as a thali — you get a bit of everything in one plate.

SEBI defines several hybrid fund sub-categories:

- **Aggressive Hybrid Funds:** Invest 65–80% in equity and the rest in debt. More growth-oriented, but with a debt cushion during market falls. Example: **ICICI Prudential Equity & Debt Fund**.

- **Conservative Hybrid Funds:** Invest 10–25% in equity and the rest in debt. More stable, with just a touch of equity growth. Example: **SBI Magnum Hybrid Bond Fund**.

- **Balanced Advantage Funds (Dynamic Asset Allocation):** These funds automatically adjust their equity-debt mix based on market conditions. When markets are expensive, they shift more to debt. When markets are cheap, they increase equity exposure. This is done using mathematical models. Example: **ICICI Prudential Balanced Advantage Fund**, one of India's largest balanced advantage funds with over ₹50,000 crore in AUM.

- **Multi Asset Allocation Funds:** Invest in at least three asset classes — typically equity, debt, and gold. Provides broad diversification in one fund. Example: **ICICI Prudential Multi-Asset Fund**.

Hybrid funds are excellent for beginners because you don't have to decide "how much in equity vs debt" — the fund does it for you. They're also great during uncertain times because the debt portion acts as a shock absorber.

### SEBI's Categorisation Rules — Why They Matter

Before 2017, the Indian mutual fund landscape was chaotic. Fund names were confusing — a fund called "XYZ Opportunities Fund" could be a large-cap fund in one AMC and a mid-cap fund in another. Investors had no idea what they were actually buying.

In 2017, SEBI introduced strict **categorisation and rationalisation rules**. Here's what changed:

1. **Standardised categories:** Every AMC must use the same category names (Large Cap, Mid Cap, Small Cap, etc.) with the same definitions.
2. **One fund per category per AMC:** An AMC can only have one fund in each sub-category. No more launching five different large-cap funds with slightly different names.
3. **Clear definitions:** Large cap = top 100 companies. Mid cap = 101–250. Small cap = 251 and beyond. No ambiguity.
4. **Minimum investment thresholds:** Equity funds must invest at least 65% in equity. Debt funds must follow maturity and credit quality rules.

This was a game-changer for Indian investors. Now, when you see "HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund" and "SBI Magnum Mid Cap Fund," you know they're both investing in the same universe of mid-cap companies (ranks 101–250). You can compare them more fairly.

## Real-World Example

Let's say you're 15 and you have ₹2,000/month to invest. How might you split it across fund types?

**Option 1: Simple — One Hybrid Fund**
Invest ₹2,000/month in **ICICI Prudential Balanced Advantage Fund**. This single fund gives you exposure to both equity and debt, automatically adjusted by the fund manager's model. Simple, diversified, beginner-friendly.

**Option 2: Moderate — Two Funds**
- ₹1,200/month in **HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund** (equity for growth)
- ₹800/month in **SBI Magnum Liquid Fund** (debt for stability)

This gives you more control. You tilt towards growth since you're young and have decades to ride out volatility, but keep some in debt for balance.

**Option 3: Aggressive — Pure Equity**
- ₹2,000/month in **Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund** (100% equity, diversified across sizes and geographies)

Maximum growth potential, but also maximum volatility. At 15, you have time on your side — but only if you can stomach the ups and downs without panic-selling.

All three options are valid. The "right" choice depends on your comfort with risk and your financial goals. The important thing is to start — and to understand what each fund type is doing with your money.

## Quiz

1. Equity funds primarily invest in:
   A) Gold and commodities
   B) Government bonds
   C) Stocks and shares of companies
   D) Real estate
   (Answer: C)

2. Which type of fund is best for parking emergency money for a few days?
   A) Small Cap Fund
   B) Liquid Fund
   C) ELSS Fund
   D) Aggressive Hybrid Fund
   (Answer: B)

3. A Balanced Advantage Fund adjusts its equity-debt mix based on:
   A) The investor's age
   B) Market conditions using mathematical models
   C) SEBI's monthly instructions
   D) Random allocation
   (Answer: B)

4. After SEBI's 2017 categorisation rules, how many funds in the same sub-category can an AMC have?
   A) As many as they want
   B) Up to five
   C) Only one
   D) Only two
   (Answer: C)

5. Which fund sub-category invests in India's top 100 companies by market cap?
   A) Mid Cap Fund
   B) Small Cap Fund
   C) Large Cap Fund
   D) Flexi Cap Fund
   (Answer: C)
