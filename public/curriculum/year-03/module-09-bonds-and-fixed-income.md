## Key Concepts

### What Are Bonds? (Lending Money, But Smarter)
Imagine your friend asks to borrow ₹1,000 from you. They promise to return ₹1,000 after one year, plus pay you ₹80 every 6 months as a "thank you" for lending. That's essentially what a bond is — except instead of lending to a friend, you're lending money to a government or a company.

A bond is like a formal IOU. When you buy a bond, you become a lender. The borrower (the government or a company) promises to pay you back on a specific date (called the maturity date) and pays you regular interest (called a coupon) along the way. The main difference from lending to your friend? These IOUs are legally binding, regulated by SEBI and the Reserve Bank of India, and you can even sell them to someone else before they mature.

Bonds are considered "fixed income" investments because you know roughly how much money you'll earn — the interest payments are fixed (or predictable). This is why conservative investors and institutions like pension funds love bonds. You won't get the explosive growth that stocks might offer, but you also won't wake up to a 30% drop in your portfolio.

### Government Bonds (G-Secs) vs Corporate Bonds
Government bonds are issued by the Government of India (Central Government) or state governments to fund public spending — building highways, running schools, defence, and so on. When the Government of India issues a bond, it's called a Government Security or G-Sec. These are considered the safest bonds in India because, well, the government can always print money to pay you back (though that's not ideal for the economy!).

Within government bonds, there are a few types you should know:
- **Treasury Bills (T-Bills):** Short-term bonds with maturities of 91 days, 182 days, or 364 days. These don't pay regular interest — you buy them at a discount and get the full face value at maturity.
- **Government Dated Securities:** Long-term bonds with maturities ranging from 5 to 40 years. These pay interest semi-annually.
- **State Development Loans (SDLs):** Bonds issued by state governments like Maharashtra, Karnataka, etc.
- **Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs):** Government bonds linked to gold prices — we'll cover these in the next module!

Corporate bonds work the same way, but the borrower is a company instead of the government. Companies like HDFC Ltd, Tata Motors, Reliance, and LIC Housing Finance issue bonds to raise money for expansion, new projects, or refinancing debt. Corporate bonds typically pay higher interest than government bonds because there's more risk — a company could face financial trouble and default on its payments. SEBI regulates the corporate bond market in India, and companies with higher credit ratings (like AAA, AA) are considered safer borrowers.

### How Bond Prices and Interest Rates Dance Together
Here's the trickiest concept in bonds, but also the most important. Bond prices and interest rates move in *opposite directions*. When interest rates go up, existing bond prices fall. When interest rates go down, existing bond prices rise. But why?

Imagine you bought a government bond that pays 7% interest per year. A year later, new bonds are being issued at 8% interest. Why would anyone buy your old 7% bond at full price when they can get 8% on a new bond? They wouldn't — so the price of your bond drops until the math works out and it becomes attractive again. The opposite happens when rates fall.

This matters a lot for long-term bonds. A 10-year G-Sec bond will swing in price much more than a 2-year bond when interest rates change. The RBI sets the repo rate (currently around 6.50%), which influences all bond yields in the economy. When the RBI raises rates to fight inflation, bond prices fall. When it cuts rates to boost growth, bond prices rise. This is why bond investors keep a close eye on RBI monetary policy announcements.

In India, the bond market is enormous — the government alone has over ₹150 lakh crore in outstanding bonds! But most retail investors haven't explored it yet. That's changing, especially with platforms like RBI Retail Direct making it easier for everyday people like you to invest.

## Real-World Example

### Investing in Bonds Through RBI Retail Direct
In November 2021, the RBI launched the **RBI Retail Direct** platform — a game-changer for individual investors in India. Before this, buying government bonds was complicated and usually required going through banks or brokers. Now, any Indian citizen can open a Retail Direct Gilt (RDG) account for free on the RBI's website.

Here's how it works in practice. Say you're 18 and have ₹10,000 to invest. You log into RBI Retail Direct and see a selection of G-Secs available in the primary auction (when the government first issues new bonds). You decide to buy a 10-year G-Sec currently yielding about 7.2%. That means you'll earn ₹720 per year on your ₹10,000 investment, paid semi-annually (₹360 every 6 months). At the end of 10 years, you get your ₹10,000 back.

For corporate bonds, you can invest through platforms like **BondsIndia**, **GoldenPi**, or through your demat account via the **BSE or NSE**. Companies like HDFC Ltd have issued bonds with yields of 8-9%, while AAA-rated companies like NABARD or IRFC offer yields around 7.5-8%. These come with higher returns but slightly more risk than government bonds.

The beauty of bonds is their predictability. While your equity mutual fund might be down 15% in a bad year, your bond will keep paying its interest like clockwork. That's why financial advisors recommend having a mix of both — bonds for stability, equities for growth. As a Trust Fund Baby, understanding bonds means you can build a portfolio that balances safety with returns.

## Quiz

1. **What is a bond?** — A) A share in a company B) A loan you give to a government or company C) A type of mutual fund D) A savings account at a bank (Answer: B)

3. **What is a G-Sec?** — A) A government security issued by SEBI B) A government security issued by RBI on behalf of the Government of India C) A gold bond D) A corporate bond (Answer: B)

4. **Why do corporate bonds typically pay higher interest than government bonds?** — A) Because companies are richer B) Because corporate bonds carry more default risk C) Because government bonds are longer-term D) Because SEBI forces them to (Answer: B)
