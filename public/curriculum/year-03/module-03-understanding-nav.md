# Module 3: Understanding NAV
**Year:** 3 (Age 15)
**Month:** 3
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what NAV (Net Asset Value) means and how it's calculated
- Learn why NAV changes every business day
- Clear up the common misconception that NAV works like a stock price
- Know where and how to check NAVs of Indian mutual funds

## Key Concepts

### What Is NAV?

NAV stands for **Net Asset Value**. Simply put, it is the price of one unit of a mutual fund. If you've been following along from Module 1, you know that when you invest in a mutual fund, you receive "units." The NAV tells you how much each of those units is worth.

Here's the formula:

**NAV = (Total Assets of the Fund − Total Liabilities) ÷ Total Number of Units Outstanding**

Let's break this down with real numbers. Imagine a mutual fund called "Growth India Fund" that has:

- Total assets (stocks, bonds, cash): ₹100 crore
- Total liabilities (expenses, fees owed): ₹2 crore
- Total units issued to investors: 10 crore units

**NAV = (₹100 crore − ₹2 crore) ÷ 10 crore = ₹9.80 per unit**

So each unit of Growth India Fund is worth ₹9.80. If you invest ₹9,800 today, you'd receive 1,000 units.

This calculation happens at the end of every business day — after the Indian stock markets (BSE and NSE) close at 3:30 PM. The AMC calculates the current market value of all stocks, bonds, and other assets in the portfolio, subtracts any liabilities, and divides by the total units. That number is the NAV you see published on the AMC's website, AMFI's website, and every investment app.

### How NAV Is Calculated — A Deeper Look

Let's walk through a real scenario with a fictional fund, "Bluechip India Fund":

**Step 1: Value all assets**
The fund owns shares of various companies. At market close:
- 50,000 shares of Reliance Industries × ₹2,800 = ₹14 crore
- 30,000 shares of Infosys × ₹1,500 = ₹4.5 crore
- 40,000 shares of HDFC Bank × ₹1,650 = ₹6.6 crore
- Government bonds worth = ₹10 crore
- Cash in bank = ₹0.9 crore

**Total assets = ₹36 crore**

**Step 2: Subtract liabilities**
- Pending expenses, fees, etc. = ₹0.2 crore

**Net assets = ₹35.8 crore**

**Step 3: Divide by total units**
- Total units outstanding = 2 crore

**NAV = ₹35.8 crore ÷ 2 crore = ₹17.90**

That's it. It's basic arithmetic — but done with crores of rupees across hundreds of stocks and bonds, recalculated every single day. Fund accountants at the AMC (supervised by SEBI-registered custodians and auditors) handle this computation.

The NAV is published on the AMFI website (amfiindia.com) by 9:00 PM every business day. You can also check it on apps like Groww, Zerodha Coin, Kuvera, or directly on the AMC's website (like hdfcfund.com or sbimf.com).

### NAV Is NOT Like a Stock Price — The Big Misconception

This is the most common mistake people make with mutual funds, so let's clear it up once and for all.

**A stock price** is determined by supply and demand on the stock exchange. If more people want to buy Reliance shares, the price goes up. If more people want to sell, it goes down. The price reflects what the market *thinks* the stock is worth.

**A NAV** is purely mathematical. It is the actual, calculated value of the underlying assets divided by units. Nobody is "bidding" on a NAV. It doesn't go up because more people are buying the fund. It goes up (or down) only because the value of the stocks and bonds inside the fund went up (or down).

This means:

- **A fund with ₹10 NAV is not "cheaper" than a fund with ₹500 NAV.** This is like saying a 250ml bottle of water is "cheaper" than a 1-litre bottle just because it costs less. The NAV reflects the per-unit value of the fund's portfolio. A higher NAV simply means the fund's investments have grown more (or it's been around longer).

- **Buying a fund at ₹10 NAV doesn't mean you'll make more money than buying at ₹500 NAV.** Returns are percentage-based. If both funds grow 15% in a year, ₹10 becomes ₹11.50 and ₹500 becomes ₹575. Your percentage gain is identical.

- **A "New Fund Offer" (NFO) at ₹10 is not a bargain.** Some people think buying a brand-new fund at ₹10 is like buying a stock during an IPO at a discount. It's not. The NAV is ₹10 simply because the fund is new and hasn't invested yet. There's nothing special about ₹10.

SEBI and AMFI have repeatedly warned investors about this NAV misconception. Always compare funds by their track record, expense ratio, and investment strategy — never by NAV.

### How NAV Changes Daily

Every business day, after markets close, the NAV updates. Here's what causes it to change:

1. **Stock prices move:** If Reliance goes up 2%, the fund's NAV rises because its Reliance holding is now worth more.
2. **Bond interest accrues:** Bonds generate interest daily, which adds to NAV.
3. **Dividends received:** When companies in the fund pay dividends, it adds to the fund's assets.
4. **Expenses deducted:** The AMC charges a small fee (expense ratio) daily, which slightly reduces NAV.
5. **Investor redemptions and purchases:** When investors buy or sell units, the total units outstanding change, which affects NAV calculation.

This is why you'll see NAV published with 4 decimal places (like ₹45.2376). Every paisa matters when you're managing hundreds or thousands of crores.

## Real-World Example

Let's look at **ICICI Prudential Bluechip Fund — Direct Plan (Growth)**, one of India's largest equity mutual funds. As of recent data:

- **AUM:** Over ₹55,000 crore
- **NAV:** Around ₹80–90 (varies daily)
- **Category:** Large Cap (invests predominantly in India's top 100 companies by market cap)

Now here's the key insight: this fund has been around since 2008. When it launched, its NAV was ₹10. Today, it's around ₹80+. That's not because people "bid it up" — it's because the stocks inside the fund (companies like TCS, Infosys, Reliance, HDFC Bank) have grown in value over 15+ years.

Meanwhile, a newer fund like **Nippon India Multi Cap Fund** might have a NAV of ₹25. That doesn't mean it's "worse" — it just means it's younger or started from a different point.

Compare funds by their 3-year, 5-year, and 10-year returns. Not by NAV. That's the professional way.

## Quiz

1. What does NAV stand for?
   A) National Asset Valuation
   B) Net Asset Value
   C) Net Average Volume
   D) New Asset Verification
   (Answer: B)

2. A mutual fund has ₹200 crore in assets, ₹5 crore in liabilities, and 15 crore units. What is the NAV?
   A) ₹13.00
   B) ₹13.33
   C) ₹12.50
   D) ₹15.00
   (Answer: A) — (₹200−₹5)÷15 = ₹13.00

3. Which of the following is TRUE about NAV?
   A) A fund with ₹10 NAV is cheaper and better to buy
   B) NAV changes based on supply and demand like stock prices
   C) NAV is the calculated per-unit value of a fund's net assets
   D) A higher NAV means the fund is overvalued
   (Answer: C)

4. When is the NAV of an Indian mutual fund calculated?
   A) At 9:15 AM when the market opens
   B) At 3:30 PM when the market closes
   C) Only on weekends
   D) Once every month
   (Answer: B)

5. A "New Fund Offer" at ₹10 NAV means:
   A) The fund is offering a special discount
   B) The fund will definitely give 10x returns
   C) The fund is simply new and hasn't invested yet — ₹10 is the starting NAV
   D) The fund is the cheapest in the market
   (Answer: C)
