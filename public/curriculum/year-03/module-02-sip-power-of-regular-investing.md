# Module 2: SIP — The Power of Regular Investing

**Month:** 2
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives

- Learn the concept of rupee cost averaging

## Key Concepts

### What Is a SIP?

A SIP, or Systematic Investment Plan, is simply a way of investing a fixed amount of money into a mutual fund at regular intervals — usually every month. Think of it like a subscription. Just like you pay ₹199/month for a streaming service, you set up a SIP of ₹500 or ₹1,000 per month into a mutual fund, and the money gets automatically deducted from your bank account.

You don't have to remember to invest. You don't have to time the market. You don't have to decide "is today a good day to buy?" The SIP handles all of that for you, consistently, month after month.

Here's how it works in practice: let's say you set up a SIP of ₹1,000/month in HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund. On the 5th of every month, ₹1,000 gets debited from your bank account and invested into the fund. In return, you receive units of the fund based on that day's NAV. Some months the NAV is high, so you get fewer units. Other months the NAV is low, so you get more units. Over time, this averages out — and that's exactly the magic of SIPs.

According to AMFI data, India had over 8.4 crore SIP accounts as of 2024, with monthly SIP contributions crossing ₹20,000 crore. That's crores of Indians choosing the disciplined, automatic route to wealth building.

### Rupee Cost Averaging — Your Built-In Advantage

This is one of the coolest concepts in investing, and SIPs make it effortless.

**Rupee cost averaging** means that by investing a fixed amount regularly, you automatically buy more units when prices are low and fewer units when prices are high. Over time, your average cost per unit tends to be lower than the average price.

Let's make this real. Imagine you invest ₹1,000 every month in a mutual fund:

| Month | NAV (₹) | Units You Get |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| Jan   | 20      | 50.00         |
| Feb   | 25      | 40.00         |
| Mar   | 16      | 62.50         |
| Apr   | 22      | 45.45         |
| May   | 18      | 55.56         |

Total invested: ₹5,000. Total units: 253.51. Your average cost per unit: ₹5,000 ÷ 253.51 = **₹19.72**

The average NAV across 5 months was ₹20.20. But because you bought more units when NAV was low (₹16 in March), your effective cost is ₹19.72 — lower than the average! That's rupee cost averaging working in your favour.

This is why SIPs are so powerful in volatile markets like India's. You don't need to predict the market. You just need to keep investing consistently.

### The Power of Starting Early

This is where things get mind-blowing. Let's compare two investors:

**Riya** starts investing ₹500/month at age 15. She invests for 15 years (age 15 to 30), then stops completely. Total invested: ₹90,000.

**Arjun** waits until age 30 and starts investing ₹5,000/month. He invests for 25 years (age 30 to 55). Total invested: ₹15,00,000.

Assuming both earn an average annual return of 12% (which is roughly what the Nifty 50 index has delivered over the long term in India):

- **Riya's corpus at age 55:** ₹26.5 lakh (from just ₹90,000 invested!)
- **Arjun's corpus at age 55:** ₹94.5 lakh (from ₹15 lakh invested)

Arjun invested **16 times more money** than Riya, but his final amount is only about 3.5 times more. Riya's tiny ₹500/month, started 15 years earlier, grew into a massive amount because compounding had 40 years to work.

The lesson? **Time in the market beats timing the market.** Starting with ₹500 today is more powerful than starting with ₹5,000 fifteen years from now. You have the greatest asset right now that no amount of money can buy later — time.

### SIP vs Lump Sum

A **lump sum** is when you invest a large amount all at once — say ₹1 lakh in one go. A SIP spreads that ₹1 lakh over several months.

Which is better? It depends:

**When lump sum works better:** If you have a large amount and the market is at a low point, investing it all at once means your money has more time to grow. Historically, lump sum investing has slightly outperformed SIPs in rising markets.

**When SIP works better:** Most of us don't have ₹1 lakh lying around, and even if we did, we'd worry about investing at the wrong time. SIPs remove that anxiety. They're also perfect for salaried individuals or students who get money in small, regular amounts (pocket money, part-time income, gifts).

For most people — especially young investors — SIP is the smarter choice. It builds discipline, removes emotion from investing, and leverages rupee cost averaging. The biggest risk in investing isn't choosing the wrong fund; it's not starting at all.

## Real-World Example

**SBI Small Cap Fund** is one of the most talked-about mutual funds in India. If you had started a SIP of ₹500/month in this fund 10 years ago, your total investment would be ₹60,000. But thanks to the fund's strong performance (it has delivered over 20% annualised returns in many periods), your investment could have grown to over ₹1.5–2 lakh.

Now, SBI Small Cap Fund has periodically stopped accepting fresh SIP investments when its AUM grew too large (SEBI rules require small-cap funds to manage within certain limits). This is actually a great example of how regulation protects investors — the fund manager wasn't greedy and chose to cap inflows rather than invest in lower-quality stocks just to deploy money.

HDFC Mutual Fund also offers easy SIP setup through their website and apps. You can set up a SIP in minutes, choose a date that works for you, and even set up a "step-up SIP" where your SIP amount increases by 10% every year as your income grows.

2. In rupee cost averaging, you buy _____ units when the NAV is low.
   A) Fewer
   B) More
   C) Zero
   D) The same number of
   (Answer: B)

3. Riya invests ₹500/month from age 15. Arjun invests ₹5,000/month from age 30. Who has more money at age 55?
   A) Always Arjun, because he invests more
   B) Always Riya, because she started earlier
   C) It depends on returns, but starting early gives Riya a huge compounding advantage

   A) Higher guaranteed returns
   B) Removes the need to time the market
   C) No taxes on SIP returns
