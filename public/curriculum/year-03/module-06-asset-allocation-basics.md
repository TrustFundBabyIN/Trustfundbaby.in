## Key Concepts

### Don't Put All Your Eggs in One Basket

You've probably heard this phrase before, and it's one of the smartest ideas in all of investing. Imagine you're carrying 12 eggs home from the market. If you put all 12 in one bag and the bag tears, you lose everything. But if you split them across 3 bags and one bag drops, you still have 8 eggs safe.

Investing works the same way. If you put all your money in one stock — say, Reliance Industries — and that company has a bad year, your entire portfolio takes a hit. But if you spread your money across different types of investments (equity, debt, gold), a bad year in one area won't destroy everything.

This spreading-out strategy has a fancy name: **diversification**. It doesn't eliminate risk, but it dramatically reduces the chance of a catastrophic loss. SEBI and AMFI consistently recommend diversification as a core principle for Indian investors.

### What Is Asset Allocation?

**Asset allocation** is simply the decision of how to divide your money across different types of investments. These types are called **asset classes**, and the main ones in India are:

- **Equity (Stocks/Equity Mutual Funds):** Ownership in companies. Higher growth potential, higher volatility. Think HDFC Equity Fund or a Nifty 50 index fund.
- **Debt (Bonds/FDs/Debt Mutual Funds):** Lending money to governments or companies. Lower returns but more stable. Think PPF, SBI FDs, or ICICI Prudential Bond Fund.
- **Gold:** A traditional store of value in India. Gold tends to shine when other markets fall, making it a good safety net. You can invest via Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) or gold ETFs.
- **Cash/Liquid Funds:** Money kept easily accessible for emergencies. Liquid mutual funds from AMCs like HDFC or Aditya Birla Sun Life offer slightly better returns than a savings account with instant access.

The idea is to choose a mix that matches your goals, time horizon, and risk appetite. A well-allocated portfolio doesn't just reduce risk — it actually tends to deliver better **risk-adjusted returns**, meaning you get more reward per unit of risk taken.

### The Age-Based Rule of Thumb

Here's a simple formula many financial planners in India recommend as a starting point:

**Equity allocation (%) = 100 − your age**

At age 15: 100 − 15 = **85% equity, 15% debt/other**
At age 25: 100 − 25 = **75% equity, 25% debt/other**
At age 40: 100 − 40 = **60% equity, 40% debt/other**
At age 60: 100 − 60 = **40% equity, 60% debt/other**

The logic is simple: when you're young, you have decades to recover from market dips, so you can afford more equity exposure. As you get older and closer to retirement, you shift toward safer, more stable investments to protect what you've built.

This is a **rule of thumb**, not a rigid law. Some aggressive investors use "110 minus age" to stay more in equity. Conservative investors might use "80 minus age." The point is to have a plan, not to guess randomly.

For a 15-year-old, an allocation of around 85% equity and 15% debt makes sense — but since you're likely investing small amounts from pocket money or gifts, even a simple 80-20 split across a good equity fund and a debt fund is a fantastic start.

### Diversification Across Asset Classes

Let's see how this works with a sample portfolio. Suppose you have ₹1,00,000 to invest and you're 15 years old:

| Asset Class | Allocation | Amount | Example Investment |
|-------------|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| Equity Mutual Funds | 60% | ₹60,000 | HDFC Nifty 50 Index Fund + SBI Small Cap Fund |
| Debt Mutual Funds | 30% | ₹30,000 | ICICI Prudential Short Term Fund |
| Gold | 10% | ₹10,000 | Sovereign Gold Bond (SGB) |

Why this works: In a good year for stocks (say equity goes up 15%), your ₹60,000 in equity grows to ₹69,000. Your debt portion grows steadily by 7% to ₹32,100. Gold might go up 8% to ₹10,800. Total: ₹1,11,900.

In a bad year (equity drops 10%), your equity falls to ₹54,000. But your debt still grows to ₹32,100, and gold might jump 12% during the market panic to ₹11,200. Total: ₹97,300 — a much smaller loss than if you'd been 100% in equity.

**Diversification doesn't prevent losses, but it cushions the fall.** Over time, the cushioning compounds into significantly better outcomes.

## Real-World Example

Let's look at a real scenario from the Indian markets. In 2020, when COVID-19 hit:

- The **Nifty 50** crashed 38% between January and March 2020
- **Gold** surged about 28% during 2020 as investors sought safety
- **Debt funds** (short duration) delivered around 7-8% with minimal volatility

An investor who was 100% in equity experienced the full 38% crash. But an investor with the 60-30-10 allocation saw a much milder impact because gold and debt cushioned the blow.

By 2021, the Nifty recovered and surged 24%. Gold gave up some gains. The diversified investor captured most of the upside while having survived the downside with far less stress.

**AMFI data** shows that investors who maintain consistent asset allocation across market cycles tend to outperform those who chase whichever asset class did well last year. The discipline of rebalancing — selling what went up and buying what went down — is what makes allocation truly powerful.
