# Module 8: Reading a Fund Factsheet
**Year:** 3 (Age 15)
**Month:** 8
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what a mutual fund factsheet is and why it matters
- Learn to read key numbers: returns, AUM, expense ratio, and risk metrics
- Compare a fund against its benchmark
- Gain confidence in evaluating mutual fund options

## Key Concepts

### What Is a Fund Factsheet?

Every mutual fund in India is required by **SEBI** and **AMFI** to publish a monthly factsheet. Think of it as a report card for the fund — a one or two-page document that tells you everything important about how the fund is performing, what it's investing in, and how much it costs you.

Fund houses like HDFC Mutual Fund, ICICI Prudential, SBI Mutual Fund, and others publish these factsheets on their websites and on AMFI's portal (amfiindia.com). If you're ever considering investing in a fund, reading its factsheet should be your first step — not the fund manager's sales pitch or your uncle's recommendation.

A typical factsheet contains:
- **Fund name and category** (e.g., HDFC Top 100 Fund — Large Cap)
- **Returns** (1 year, 3 year, 5 year, since inception)
- **AUM** (Assets Under Management)
- **Expense ratio**
- **Top holdings** (which stocks the fund owns)
- **Benchmark comparison** (how the fund did vs its benchmark index)
- **Risk metrics** (standard deviation, Sharpe ratio, beta)
- **Fund manager details**

Let's break down the most important ones.

### Returns: How Much Money Has the Fund Made?

Returns tell you how much your investment would have grown over a specific period. They're usually shown as **annualised returns** (also called CAGR — Compounded Annual Growth Rate).

For example, a factsheet might show:

| Period | Fund Return | Benchmark Return |
|--------|------------|-----------------|
| 1 Year | 18.5% | 16.2% |
| 3 Year | 14.3% | 13.8% |
| 5 Year | 12.7% | 11.9% |

This means if you had invested ₹10,000 three years ago, it would have grown to approximately ₹14,930 at 14.3% annualised returns.

**Important:** Always compare fund returns against the benchmark. A fund that returned 12% when its benchmark returned 11% has done well. A fund that returned 12% when its benchmark returned 15% has underperformed — you'd have been better off in a simple index fund!

### AUM: How Big Is the Fund?

**AUM (Assets Under Management)** is the total value of all the money invested in a fund. If HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund has an AUM of ₹45,000 crore, it means investors have collectively put ₹45,000 crore into that fund.

Why does AUM matter?
- **Very large AUM** (above ₹20,000-30,000 crore) in mid-cap or small-cap funds can be a problem — the fund manager may struggle to find enough good companies to invest in without moving stock prices.
- **Very small AUM** (below ₹500 crore) can be risky — if many investors withdraw at once, the fund could face liquidity issues.
- **Sweet spot** for most categories: ₹2,000 to ₹15,000 crore is generally comfortable.

Large-cap funds like SBI Bluechip Fund can handle larger AUMs because they invest in big, liquid companies. Small-cap funds face more constraints.

### Expense Ratio: The Hidden Cost

The **expense ratio** is the annual fee the fund charges you for managing your money. It's expressed as a percentage of your investment. If a fund has an expense ratio of 1.5%, it means ₹1,500 per year is deducted from every ₹1,00,000 you invest.

SEBI has set maximum expense ratio limits:
- **Equity funds:** Up to 2.25% (but most good funds charge 0.5% to 1.5%)
- **Debt funds:** Up to 2.00%
- **Index funds:** Up to 1.00% (typically 0.1% to 0.5%)

Why does this matter so much? Because the expense ratio is deducted from your returns **every single year**, and it compounds — just like returns do, but in reverse.

Example: Two funds both earn 12% per year. Fund A charges 0.5% expense ratio (net return: 11.5%). Fund B charges 1.5% (net return: 10.5%). Over 20 years on ₹1,00,000:
- Fund A: approximately ₹8,95,000
- Fund B: approximately ₹7,33,000

That's a difference of ₹1,62,000 — just from expense ratio! Always check this number.

### Benchmark Comparison and Risk Metrics

Every fund is compared against a **benchmark index** — this is the standard it tries to beat. Examples:
- Large-cap funds benchmarked against **Nifty 50** or **BSE 100**
- Mid-cap funds benchmarked against **Nifty Midcap 150**
- Balanced funds benchmarked against a **composite index** (e.g., 65% Nifty 50 + 35% CRISIL Bond Fund Index)

If a fund consistently beats its benchmark, the fund manager is earning their fee. If it consistently underperforms, you might as well invest in a cheaper index fund that just tracks the benchmark.

Key risk metrics you'll see:
- **Standard Deviation:** Measures how much the fund's returns fluctuate. Higher = more volatile. A fund with 18% standard deviation swings more wildly than one with 10%.
- **Sharpe Ratio:** Measures return per unit of risk. Higher is better. A Sharpe of 1.5 is better than 0.8 because you're getting more reward for each bit of risk you're taking.
- **Beta:** Measures how much the fund moves relative to the market. A beta of 1.0 means it moves exactly with the market. 1.2 means it moves 20% more than the market (higher risk, higher potential return). 0.8 means it's calmer than the market.

You don't need to memorise formulas — just remember: **lower expense ratio, consistent benchmark beating, reasonable risk metrics = a solid fund.**

## Real-World Example

Let's look at a simplified factsheet for the **ICICI Prudential Bluechip Fund** (a popular large-cap equity fund in India):

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Category | Large Cap (Equity) |
| AUM | ~₹48,000 crore |
| Expense Ratio | ~1.07% (Direct Plan) |
| Benchmark | Nifty 100 TRI |
| 1-Year Return | ~18% |
| 3-Year Return (CAGR) | ~14% |
| 5-Year Return (CAGR) | ~13% |
| Top Holdings | HDFC Bank, Reliance, Infosys, ICICI Bank, TCS |
| Standard Deviation | ~14% |
| Sharpe Ratio | ~0.9 |

What can we learn from this?
1. The fund has beaten its benchmark slightly (benchmark returned ~13% over 5 years vs fund's ~13%) — decent but not exceptional
2. The expense ratio of 1.07% is reasonable for an active large-cap fund
3. The AUM of ₹48,000 crore is large but manageable for large-cap investing
4. Top holdings are blue-chip Indian companies — exactly what you'd expect from a large-cap fund
5. The Sharpe ratio of 0.9 means the fund gives decent returns relative to the risk taken

**AMFI** maintains a database where you can compare factsheets of all SEBI-registered mutual funds side by side. This is one of the most powerful free tools available to Indian investors.

## Quiz

1. What does AUM stand for? — A) Annual Unit Management B) Assets Under Management C) Average Utilisation Metric D) All Units Multiplied (Answer: B)

2. A fund has an expense ratio of 1.5%. What does this mean? — A) You pay ₹1,500 one-time B) The fund takes 1.5% of your investment annually as fees C) You get 1.5% bonus returns D) The government charges 1.5% tax (Answer: B)

3. Which expense ratio is generally BETTER for an investor? — A) 2.0% B) 1.5% C) 0.5% D) They're all the same (Answer: C)

4. If a fund's 5-year return is 12% but its benchmark returned 14%, what does this indicate? — A) The fund has outperformed B) The fund has underperformed C) The fund is performing perfectly D) Benchmarks don't matter (Answer: B)

5. What does a higher Sharpe Ratio indicate? — A) Higher fees B) More risk C) Better return per unit of risk taken D) Lower AUM (Answer: C)
