# Module 6: Real Estate — Renting vs Buying

**Year:** 8 (Age 20)
**Month:** 6
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the true costs of buying vs renting a home in India
- Learn the "price-to-rent ratio" and how to evaluate real estate as an investment
- Recognise the cultural and financial pressures around home ownership
- Make an informed decision about when (and whether) to buy property

## Key Concepts

### The Indian Dream — "Buy a House as Soon as Possible"

In India, buying a house is considered the ultimate financial milestone. Parents push it. Relatives ask about it at every wedding. "Ghar kab loge?" (When will you buy a house?) is practically a national obsession. Owning property is seen as security, status, and smart investing all at once.

But here's what nobody tells you: **in many Indian cities, renting is financially superior to buying — especially for young people.** The math doesn't always support home ownership, and the cultural pressure to buy early can actually slow down your wealth creation.

That doesn't mean buying is always wrong. It means you should run the numbers before making one of the biggest financial decisions of your life.

### The True Cost of Buying a Home

Let's say you buy a ₹50 lakh apartment in a Tier-1 city (Pune, Bangalore, Hyderabad). Here's what it actually costs over 20 years:

**Down payment:** 20% = ₹10,00,000 (out of pocket, no returns on this money)
**Home loan:** ₹40,00,000 at 8.5% for 20 years
**Monthly EMI:** ₹34,713
**Total EMI paid over 20 years:** ₹83,31,120
**Total interest paid:** ₹43,31,120 (more than the original loan!)
**Plus:** Registration (5–7% of property value = ₹2.5–3.5L), stamp duty, GST (if under construction), maintenance (₹2,000–5,000/month), property tax (₹5,000–15,000/year), interior costs (₹3–8L), society charges, insurance

**Total cost of ownership over 20 years: approximately ₹95–100 lakhs** for a ₹50 lakh property.

**Meanwhile, the property might appreciate to ₹1.2–1.5 crore** (assuming 5–6% annual appreciation in most Indian cities — not the 15–20% your uncle claims).

### The Renting Alternative

Renting the same ₹50 lakh apartment might cost ₹12,000–₹18,000/month in most Indian cities (price-to-rent ratio of 2–3%). Over 20 years, that's ₹28.8–43.2 lakh in rent.

"But you're throwing money away on rent!" Not quite. Here's what renters do with the money they save:

- **₹10 lakh down payment** invested in equity mutual funds at 12% for 20 years = **₹96 lakh**
- **EMI minus rent difference** (₹34,713 - ₹15,000 = ₹19,713/month) invested in SIPs at 12% for 20 years = **₹1.9 crore**

**Total wealth from renting + investing: ₹2.86 crore**
**Total wealth from buying (property value minus total cost): ₹1.5Cr - ₹1Cr = ₹50 lakh net**

In this scenario, **renting and investing the difference creates 5.7x more wealth** than buying.

### When Buying DOES Make Sense

Buying makes sense when:

1. **You plan to live in the same city for 10+ years.** Transaction costs (registration, stamp duty, brokerage) are 8–12% of property value. If you move every 3–4 years, these costs eat your gains.

2. **The price-to-rent ratio is below 15.** Calculate this: Property price ÷ Annual rent. If a ₹50 lakh property rents for ₹4 lakh/year, the ratio is 12.5 — buying starts making sense. In most metros, this ratio is 25–40, favouring renting.

3. **You value the emotional security of ownership.** No landlord telling you to leave. No rent increases. Freedom to modify your home. This has real psychological value that the spreadsheet doesn't capture.

4. **You're using a home loan for tax benefits.** Section 24(b) allows ₹2 lakh deduction on home loan interest, and Section 80C allows ₹1.5 lakh on principal. But the new tax regime eliminates these benefits, making buying less attractive.

5. **Real estate is part of a diversified portfolio.** If you already have equity, debt, and gold, adding property diversifies your assets. But property should not be 80% of your net worth — which is common in Indian households.

### The Rent-vs-Buy Framework for Young Indians

At 20, the answer is almost certainly **rent.** You don't know which city you'll settle in. Your income will grow significantly over the next 5–10 years. Locking ₹10 lakh into a down payment at 22 means losing the compounding potential of that money during your highest-growth earning years.

**The smarter path:** Rent cheaply, invest aggressively, buy when you're 30+ with a stable location, higher income, and a substantial down payment from your investments.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Kabir, your trust fund will give you ₹4,68,000 at maturity. That's close to a down payment on a small apartment in a Tier-2 city. Your relatives might suggest: "Buy a flat! Real estate always goes up!"

But consider: if you invest that ₹4.68L in equity mutual funds at 12% for 10 years, it grows to approximately **₹14.5 lakh.** Add monthly SIPs of ₹10,000, and you'll have approximately **₹37 lakh** in 10 years. That's a much bigger down payment for a much better property — when you actually know where you want to live.

Your trust fund is a launchpad. Don't lock it into illiquid real estate at 21. Let it compound.

## Quiz

1. A ₹50 lakh home loan at 8.5% for 20 years has an EMI of ₹34,713. How much total interest do you pay? — A) ₹20 lakh B) ₹33 lakh C) ₹43 lakh D) ₹50 lakh (Answer: C)

2. What is the "price-to-rent ratio" and what does it indicate? — A) Property price × Annual rent; higher is better for buying B) Property price ÷ Annual rent; below 15 favours buying C) Monthly rent ÷ Property price; below 1% favours renting D) It's the same as EMI calculation (Answer: B)

3. In which scenario does renting + investing beat buying? — A) When price-to-rent ratio is above 20 B) When you plan to move cities in 3 years C) When your rent is low and you invest the EMI-rent difference D) All of the above (Answer: D)

4. What percentage of property value goes to registration, stamp duty, and transaction costs? — A) 1–2% B) 3–5% C) 8–12% D) 15–20% (Answer: C)

5. At age 20 with ₹4.68L from a trust fund, what's the smartest real estate move? — A) Buy a flat immediately B) Invest the money and buy property later when you have more clarity and capital C) Buy land in a developing area D) Take a home loan and buy a bigger flat (Answer: B)
