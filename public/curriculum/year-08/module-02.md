# Module 2: Personal Loans & Debt Traps to Avoid

**Year:** 8 (Age 20)
**Month:** 2
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the different types of personal loans available in India
- Learn to calculate the true cost of borrowing (EMI, total interest, hidden charges)
- Recognise predatory lending practices and debt traps
- Build a framework for deciding when borrowing makes sense vs when it doesn't

## Key Concepts

### Personal Loans — The Basics

A personal loan is an unsecured loan — meaning there's no collateral (no house, no car pledged). Because the bank takes on more risk, personal loans carry higher interest rates than home loans or car loans. In India, personal loan rates typically range from 10.5% to 24% per annum depending on your income, employer, credit score, and relationship with the bank.

You borrow a lump sum and repay it in fixed monthly instalments (EMIs) over a set tenure — usually 1 to 5 years. The EMI is calculated using a formula that combines principal repayment and interest. Banks use a "reducing balance" method, meaning you pay interest only on the outstanding amount, not the original loan.

As a 20-year-old, you'll be targeted by instant loan apps — apps that promise ₹10,000–₹5,00,000 in minutes with minimal documentation. Many of these are predatory. We'll cover how to identify them.

### The True Cost of Borrowing

When a bank says "personal loan at 12% interest," that's not the full story. Here's what you actually pay:

**Processing fee:** 1–3% of the loan amount, deducted upfront. On a ₹1,00,000 loan, that's ₹1,000–₹3,000 gone before you see a rupee.

**GST:** 18% GST on the processing fee and any other charges.

**Foreclosure charges:** If you want to repay early, some banks charge 2–5% of the outstanding amount. This traps you into paying interest for the full tenure.

**Insurance premium:** Banks often bundle loan insurance (credit protect plans) without clearly disclosing it. This adds ₹2,000–₹5,000 to your cost.

**Let's do the math.** A ₹1,00,000 personal loan at 14% for 2 years:
- EMI: ₹4,801
- Total paid: ₹1,15,224
- Total interest: ₹15,224
- Plus processing fee (2%): ₹2,000
- Plus GST on fee: ₹360
- **True cost of borrowing: ₹17,584** (17.6% effective cost on ₹1,00,000)

Always calculate the **total cost of borrowing**, not just the interest rate.

### Debt Traps — How People Get Stuck

**The EMI stacking trap:** Taking one loan is manageable. Taking a second while repaying the first is risky. Taking a third is a trap. Your total EMIs should never exceed 40% of your take-home income. If your salary is ₹30,000 and you're already paying ₹12,000 in EMIs, any new loan pushes you into dangerous territory.

**The "balance transfer" trap:** "Transfer your existing loan to us at a lower rate!" Sounds good, but the new bank charges processing fees again, and the lower rate often comes with a longer tenure — meaning you pay less per month but more overall.

**The "top-up" trap:** Banks offer to add more money to your existing loan. This resets the clock, adds fresh interest, and keeps you in debt longer.

**The instant loan app trap:** Apps like those found on Google Play promise quick cash but charge 36–80% annualised interest, disguised as "processing fees" and "service charges." Some apps access your contacts and harass your family if you're late on payments. **Never borrow from unlicensed lending apps.** Check if the lender is RBI-registered on the RBI website.

**The credit card EMI to personal loan pipeline:** Max out your credit card → pay minimum due → bank offers EMI conversion → you accept → interest accrues → you take a personal loan to pay off the card → cycle repeats. This is the debt trap in its purest form.

### When Borrowing Makes Sense

Not all debt is bad. Borrowing makes sense when:
- **The asset appreciates:** A home loan (property value grows), an education loan (earning potential increases)
- **The return exceeds the cost:** If a course costs ₹1,00,000 but increases your salary by ₹5,00,000/year, the ROI justifies it
- **It's an emergency you can't cover with savings:** Medical emergency, urgent home repair
- **You have a clear repayment plan:** You know exactly how you'll repay, with buffer for contingencies

Borrowing is a bad idea when:
- It funds consumption (new phone, vacation, shopping)
- You're borrowing to repay another loan
- You don't know the total cost
- You're relying on future income that isn't guaranteed

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Kabir, your trust fund has ₹4,68,000 and matures in one year. This is your safety net. Before taking any personal loan in the next 12 months, ask yourself: "Can my trust fund cover this instead?"

If you need ₹30,000 for a laptop for college, consider whether it's worth paying ₹3,000+ in interest and fees on a personal loan when your trust fund — which is growing at 12%+ per year — could cover it. (Though ideally, you'd have a small emergency fund separately so you don't touch the trust.)

More importantly, avoid the temptation to take a loan against your trust fund or spend prematurely. You're one year from receiving ₹4,68,000. That's your launchpad — down payment for a home, seed capital for a business, or a substantial investment portfolio. Every rupee of unnecessary debt you take now reduces that launchpad.

## Quiz

1. What makes a personal loan "unsecured"? — A) It has no interest B) No collateral is pledged C) You don't need to repay it D) The bank doesn't check your credit score (Answer: B)

2. A ₹1,00,000 loan at 14% for 2 years has an EMI of ₹4,801. What is the total interest paid? — A) ₹14,000 B) ₹15,224 C) ₹28,000 D) ₹96,020 (Answer: B)

3. Why is the "instant loan app" model dangerous? — A) They charge annualised rates of 36-80% and use harassment for recovery B) They give too much money C) They require too much documentation D) They're only available on weekends (Answer: A)

4. Your salary is ₹35,000/month. You already pay ₹14,000 in EMIs. Should you take another loan with a ₹5,000 EMI? — A) Yes, you still have ₹16,000 left B) No, total EMIs would exceed 40% of income C) Yes, as long as the interest rate is low D) Only if it's a home loan (Answer: B)

5. Which of these is a GOOD reason to take a personal loan? — A) Buying the latest iPhone B) Funding a certification course that increases your salary by ₹3L/year C) Going on a vacation D) Paying credit card bills from another card (Answer: B)
