## Key Concepts

### What is Portfolio Rebalancing?

Portfolio rebalancing is the process of realigning the weightings of assets in your investment portfolio. Over time, some investments grow faster than others, shifting your portfolio away from your intended allocation. Rebalancing brings it back to your target.

Here's a simple example: You decide on a 60:40 equity-to-debt allocation. After a year of a bull market, your equity grows to 70% of the portfolio while debt shrinks to 30%. You now have more risk than you intended. Rebalancing means selling some equity and buying debt to return to 60:40.

This sounds counterintuitive — why sell what's performing well? Because rebalancing enforces the oldest investment discipline: **buy low, sell high.** When equity has surged, you sell some (selling high). When equity has fallen, you buy more (buying low). Rebalancing automates this discipline.

### The Two Approaches to Rebalancing

**Calendar-Based Rebalancing:** You rebalance at fixed intervals — every 6 months, annually, or quarterly. Simple, easy to implement. Most Indian investors should use this approach. Pick a date (say, your birthday or April 1st) and check your allocation. If any asset class has drifted more than 5% from target, rebalance.

**Threshold-Based Rebalancing:** You rebalance whenever an asset class drifts beyond a pre-set threshold — typically ±5% from the target. For example, if your target is 60% equity and it hits 65% or drops to 55%, you rebalance immediately regardless of the date. This is more responsive but requires more monitoring.

**Hybrid approach (recommended):** Check quarterly, rebalance only if drift exceeds 5%. This combines the simplicity of calendar-based with the responsiveness of threshold-based.

### How to Rebalance in Practice

**Method 1: Sell and Buy.** Sell the overperforming asset, buy the underperforming one. This triggers capital gains tax (10% on equity gains above ₹1 lakh in a financial year for long-term, 20% with indexation for debt). Best for large drifts (>10%).

**Method 2: Redirect New Investments.** Instead of selling, direct your new SIPs entirely into the underperforming asset until the allocation corrects. This avoids tax and transaction costs. Best for small drifts (5–10%). This is the ideal method for young investors who are still adding money.

**Method 3: Use Fund-of-Funds or Balanced Advantage Funds.** These funds automatically rebalance internally. HDFC Balanced Advantage Fund, ICICI Prudential Balanced Advantage Fund, and Edelweiss Balanced Advantage Fund do this for you. You invest in one fund and let the fund manager handle rebalancing. Best for investors who don't want to think about it.

### Rebalancing Your Trust Fund Portfolio

Your Kabir Mehra Growth Trust has been invested for 5 years through SIPs in mutual funds. The current value is ₹4,68,000 — a 56% gain. As the trust approaches maturity, you face a critical rebalancing question: **should you shift from aggressive equity to safer debt?**

This is called a **glide path strategy.** As your investment goal approaches (in this case, trust maturity at age 21), you gradually shift from equity to debt to protect your gains. If the market crashes 30% six months before your trust matures, you lose ₹1,40,000. That's painful.

A smart glide path for a trust approaching maturity:
- **12 months before maturity:** 60% equity, 40% debt
- **6 months before maturity:** 40% equity, 60% debt
- **3 months before maturity:** 20% equity, 80% debt
- **At maturity:** 100% in liquid/ultra-short debt funds

This protects your gains while still capturing some equity upside.

### Common Rebalancing Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Rebalancing too frequently.** Checking daily and rebalancing monthly generates excessive transaction costs and taxes. Once a quarter is sufficient.

**Mistake 2: Never rebalancing.** "My equity funds are doing great, why sell?" Because a 90% equity portfolio carries far more risk than a 60% equity portfolio. One market crash and your gains evaporate.

**Mistake 3: Emotional rebalancing.** Selling equity because the market "feels high" or buying more because it "feels low" isn't rebalancing — it's market timing. Rebalance based on allocation targets, not feelings.

**Mistake 4: Ignoring tax implications.** Every sell transaction in equity mutual funds above ₹1 lakh in gains triggers 10% LTCG tax. Factor this into your rebalancing decisions. Use the SIP redirection method when possible.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Kabir, your trust fund's current allocation likely skews heavily toward equity given the 56% gain over 5 years. If your original allocation was 70:30 equity-to-debt, it's probably closer to 80:20 now after this bull run.

With 12 months to maturity, consider this rebalancing plan:
1. **This month:** Shift 20% of your equity holdings to short-duration debt funds or liquid funds. This locks in some gains.
2. **6 months out:** Shift another 20% to debt. Your portfolio should now be 40:60 equity-to-debt.
3. **3 months out:** Move to 20:80.
4. **At maturity:** Everything in liquid funds, ready for your decision — withdraw, reinvest, or a mix.

This protects your ₹1,68,000 gain from market volatility while still letting the remaining equity grow if markets continue rising. You're not trying to time the market — you're managing risk as your goal approaches.

## Quiz

1. What is portfolio rebalancing? — A) Buying more stocks B) Realigning your portfolio to its target asset allocation C) Selling everything and starting fresh D) Moving all money to fixed deposits (Answer: B)

2. Why does rebalancing enforce "buy low, sell high"? — A) Because you buy more of what's fallen and sell what's risen B) Because it predicts market movements C) Because it guarantees profits D) Because it reduces taxes (Answer: A)

3. Your target allocation is 60:40 equity-to-debt. After a bull market, it's 72:28. Using the SIP redirection method, you should: — A) Sell equity and buy debt B) Direct all new SIPs into debt until 60:40 is restored C) Do nothing D) Increase your equity target to 72% (Answer: B)

4. What is a "glide path" strategy for a trust approaching maturity? — A) Increasing equity exposure B) Gradually shifting from equity to debt as the goal approaches C) Keeping the same allocation forever D) Moving everything to gold (Answer: B)

5. How often should most Indian investors rebalance? — A) Daily B) Weekly C) Quarterly (if drift exceeds 5%) D) Only during market crashes (Answer: C)
