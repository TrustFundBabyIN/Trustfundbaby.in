# Module 1: Credit Cards Deep Dive — Rewards, Cashback, Traps

**Year:** 8 (Age 20)
**Month:** 1
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand how credit cards work and how issuers make money
- Learn to evaluate reward programs and cashback offers critically
- Recognise common credit card traps that lead to debt spirals
- Build a strategy for using credit cards as a financial tool, not a liability

## Key Concepts

### How Credit Cards Actually Work

A credit card is essentially a short-term loan from a bank. When you swipe, the bank pays the merchant on your behalf, and you pay the bank back later. If you clear your entire bill by the due date (usually 15–20 days after the statement date), you pay zero interest. This is called the **interest-free period**, and it's the single most important feature of a credit card.

In India, credit cards are issued by banks like HDFC, SBI, ICICI, Axis, and fintech players like OneCard and Fi. The interest-free period can range from 20 to 50 days depending on when you make the purchase within your billing cycle. The key rule: **always pay the full amount due, never just the minimum.**

Your credit limit is based on your income, credit score (CIBIL score), and existing relationship with the bank. As a 20-year-old with no credit history, you'll likely start with a limit of ₹20,000–₹50,000. That's fine. Start small, build history.

### Rewards, Points & Cashback — Reading the Fine Print

Credit card rewards in India come in several forms: cashback (direct money back), reward points (redeemable for vouchers or products), air miles, and lifestyle benefits (airport lounge access, dining discounts). Cards like HDFC Millennia, SBI Cashback, and Amazon Pay ICICI are popular among young users.

Here's what most people miss: reward programs are designed so the bank always wins. If a card gives 5% cashback on Amazon, it's because Amazon is paying the bank a referral fee. If a card gives 2% flat cashback, the merchant discount fee (1.5–2.5%) covers it. The bank isn't losing money — you're the product.

**The golden rule of credit card rewards:** Never spend money *because* of a reward. If you were going to buy something anyway and you get cashback, that's a win. If you're buying something unnecessary to "earn points," the bank has already won.

Watch out for annual fees (₹500–₹5,000), reward point expiry (typically 2–3 years), minimum spend thresholds before rewards kick in, and excluded categories (fuel surcharges, rent payments, EMI conversions often don't earn rewards).

### The Traps — How Credit Cards Create Debt

The most dangerous feature of a credit card is the **minimum payment due**. This is typically 5% of your total outstanding balance. If you owe ₹20,000, the minimum payment is just ₹1,000. Paying only this feels easy, but the remaining ₹19,000 accrues interest at 36–42% per annum (3–3.5% per month). This is among the most expensive debt you can carry.

**The EMI trap:** Banks aggressively push EMI conversions at checkout. "Convert your ₹30,000 purchase into 12 easy EMIs of ₹2,750!" Sounds great until you calculate the effective interest rate — often 14–18% with processing fees. Always calculate the total cost before converting.

**The cash withdrawal trap:** Withdrawing cash from an ATM using a credit card triggers an immediate cash advance fee (2.5–3.5% of the amount) plus interest from day one — no interest-free period. Never do this unless it's a genuine emergency.

**The "spend threshold" trap:** "Spend ₹1,00,000 this quarter and get a ₹5,000 voucher!" This tempts you to overspend by ₹40,000 to earn a ₹5,000 reward. That's a net loss of ₹35,000.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Kabir, you're about to receive ₹4,68,000 from your trust fund in one year. Having a credit card now and using it responsibly builds your CIBIL score, which will matter when you apply for a home loan, car loan, or even a business loan later. Think of your credit card as a credit-building tool, not a spending tool.

Start with a secured credit card (backed by an FD of ₹10,000–₹20,000) if banks won't give you an unsecured one. Use it for small, recurring purchases — your phone recharge, OTT subscriptions, groceries. Set up auto-pay for the full amount. In 12 months, you'll have a 750+ CIBIL score, putting you in a strong position when your trust matures and you start making bigger financial decisions.

Your trust fund's ₹1,68,000 gain took five years of disciplined SIP investing. A single year of credit card debt at 40% interest can cost you ₹20,000–₹30,000 in interest alone. Protect your gains by staying debt-free.

## Quiz

1. What happens if you only pay the "minimum amount due" on your credit card? — A) Your credit score improves B) The remaining balance accrues interest at 36-42% p.a. C) The bank waives the remaining balance D) Nothing, it's the same as paying in full (Answer: B)

2. What is the "interest-free period" on a credit card? — A) A period where you don't need to pay anything B) 20-50 days where no interest is charged if you pay the full bill C) A promotional offer that lasts one month D) The time before your card is activated (Answer: B)

3. Why should you avoid withdrawing cash from an ATM using a credit card? — A) It's illegal B) Interest is charged from day one plus a cash advance fee C) The ATM won't accept credit cards D) Your card gets blocked (Answer: B)

4. A card offers 5% cashback on groceries. You spend ₹5,000 on groceries you need and ₹3,000 on groceries you don't need just to "maximise rewards." What's your net gain/loss? — A) +₹400 B) -₹2,600 C) -₹3,000 D) +₹250 (Answer: B)

5. Which of these is the BEST strategy for a 20-year-old using a credit card? — A) Get the highest limit possible B) Use it for small purchases and auto-pay the full amount monthly C) Pay only the minimum due to build credit D) Use it for cash withdrawals when short on money (Answer: B)
