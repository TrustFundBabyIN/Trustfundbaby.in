# Module 3: Life & Health Insurance — What You Actually Need

**Year:** 8 (Age 20)
**Month:** 3
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the difference between life insurance and health insurance
- Learn what kind and how much coverage you actually need at age 20
- Recognise common mis-selling practices in the insurance industry
- Make informed decisions about term plans, health policies, and riders

## Key Concepts

### Health Insurance — Your Most Important Financial Product

If there's one financial product every 20-year-old in India should have, it's health insurance. A single hospitalisation for dengue, appendicitis, or a road accident can cost ₹50,000–₹5,00,000. Without insurance, this wipes out savings. With a good health plan, you pay almost nothing out of pocket.

Health insurance works on a simple principle: you pay an annual premium, and the insurer covers your hospitalisation expenses up to a sum insured. In India, a ₹5 lakh health insurance plan costs roughly ₹5,000–₹8,000 per year for a 20-year-old. That's ₹400–₹650 per month — less than your monthly phone bill.

**Key terms to know:**
- **Sum Insured (SI):** Maximum amount the insurer will pay per year (₹3L, ₹5L, ₹10L, etc.)
- **Room rent limit:** Many policies cap the room category covered. If your policy covers "shared room" and you take a private room, you pay the difference
- **Co-payment:** You pay a fixed percentage (10–20%) of every claim
- **Waiting period:** Pre-existing diseases aren't covered for 2–4 years after buying the policy
- **Cashless vs Reimbursement:** Cashless means the hospital bills the insurer directly. Reimbursement means you pay first and claim later. Always prefer cashless network hospitals
- **No-Claim Bonus (NCB):** If you don't claim in a year, your sum insured increases by 10–50% for the next year, free

**Top plans for young Indians (2025–26):** HDFC Ergo Optima Secure, Niva Bupa ReAssure, Care Advantage, ICICI Lombard Complete Health Insurance. Compare on PolicyBazaar or Ditto (free advisory, no spam).

### Life Insurance — Do You Even Need It?

Here's a truth the insurance industry doesn't want you to hear: **if nobody depends on your income, you don't need life insurance.** At 20, with no spouse, no children, and no EMIs that would burden your parents if something happened to you, a life insurance policy is unnecessary expense.

Life insurance comes in several forms:
- **Term Insurance:** Pure protection. You pay a premium, and if you die during the term, your nominee gets the sum assured. If you survive, you get nothing back. This is the cheapest and best form of life insurance. A ₹1 crore term plan costs ₹500–₹800/month for a 25-year-old.
- **Endowment Plans:** Insurance + savings combined. Terrible product. Low returns (4–6%), high premiums, opaque charges. Avoid.
- **ULIPs (Unit Linked Insurance Plans):** Insurance + market investment. High charges eat into returns. Better to buy term insurance and invest the rest in mutual funds separately. Avoid.
- **Money-Back Policies:** Periodic payouts during the term. Sounds attractive but the effective returns are dismal (3–5%). Avoid.

**The rule:** Buy term insurance when someone depends on your income. At 20, that's probably nobody. When you get married, have kids, or take a large loan — that's when you buy term cover.

### Riders — Add-ons Worth Considering

Riders are optional add-ons to your base insurance policy. Some are worth it, some aren't.

**Good riders:**
- **Critical Illness Rider:** Pays a lump sum if diagnosed with cancer, heart attack, stroke, etc. Worth adding to health or term insurance.
- **Accidental Death & Disability Rider:** Additional payout in case of accident. Cheap (₹50–₹100/year extra) and worth having.
- **Waiver of Premium:** If you become disabled and can't work, future premiums are waived. Useful for term plans.

**Riders to skip:**
- **Hospital Cash Rider:** Pays ₹1,000–₹2,000/day during hospitalisation. Sounds good but your health insurance already covers expenses. This is redundant.
- **Return of Premium Rider:** You get premiums back if you survive the term. Sounds attractive but significantly increases your premium. You're better off investing the difference.

### The Mis-Selling Problem

Insurance mis-selling is rampant in India. Agents push endowment and ULIP plans because they earn 25–40% commission on the first year's premium, versus just 5–10% on term plans. They'll say things like "this plan gives you insurance AND investment" or "you get your money back."

**Red flags:**
- Agent says "this is a tax-saving investment" (it's insurance, not investment)
- Agent promises specific returns ("you'll get 12% guaranteed")
- Agent discourages you from buying term insurance ("you get nothing back")
- Agent bundles insurance with a loan or credit card without asking

**How to protect yourself:** Always buy insurance online (directly from insurer's website or PolicyBazaar). Read the policy document, not just the brochure. Ask for the "illustration" showing actual vs guaranteed returns. Never buy insurance as an investment.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Kabir, as your trust fund approaches maturity, consider this: a ₹5 lakh health insurance policy costs about ₹6,000/year. That's less than what your trust fund earns in one month of market growth (₹4,68,000 growing at ~12% = ~₹4,680/month). Your trust fund's returns alone could pay for your health insurance for decades.

One hospitalisation without insurance could cost ₹1,00,000–₹3,00,000. That's 20–65% of your trust fund, wiped out in a week. Health insurance protects your wealth. Think of it as the cheapest defence you can buy.

After your trust matures, your first financial moves should be: (1) health insurance, (2) a 6-month emergency fund, (3) continue investing. Life insurance can wait until someone depends on your income.

## Quiz

1. A ₹5 lakh health insurance plan costs ₹7,000/year. A single hospitalisation without insurance costs ₹2,00,000. What's the break-even point (how many years of premiums = one hospitalisation)? — A) 7 years B) 28.5 years C) 14 years D) 1 year (Answer: B)

2. Which type of life insurance is the BEST value for money? — A) ULIP B) Endowment C) Term Insurance D) Money-Back Policy (Answer: C)

3. At age 20 with no dependents, when should you buy life insurance? — A) Immediately B) Only when someone depends on your income C) Before health insurance D) Never (Answer: B)

4. An agent says "this ULIP gives you 12% guaranteed returns." What should you do? — A) Buy it immediately B) Ask for the illustration showing guaranteed vs non-guaranteed returns C) Ask for a higher sum assured D) Negotiate the premium down (Answer: B)

5. What is "No-Claim Bonus" in health insurance? — A) A reward for not smoking B) Your sum insured increases if you don't claim in a year C) You get cashback for every claim D) Your premium doubles after a claim (Answer: B)
