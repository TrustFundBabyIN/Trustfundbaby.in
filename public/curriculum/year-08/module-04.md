## Key Concepts

### What is NPS and Why Should a 20-Year-old Care?

The National Pension System (NPS) is a government-backed retirement savings scheme regulated by PFRDA (Pension Fund Regulatory and Development Authority). It lets you invest money during your working years and withdraw a portion as lump sum at 60, with the rest converted to a monthly pension (annuity).

"But retirement is 40 years away!" Exactly. That's your superpower. Starting at 20 gives your money four decades to compound. Here's the math that should make you sit up:

If you invest ₹5,000/month in NPS from age 20 to 60 (40 years) at an average 10% return, you'll accumulate approximately **₹2.65 crores.** Start the same ₹5,000/month at age 30, and you'll accumulate roughly **₹95 lakhs.** Ten years of head start = ₹1.7 crores more. That's the cost of waiting.

NPS invests your money in a mix of equity (E), corporate bonds (C), and government bonds (G). You choose the allocation, or let the auto-choice lifecycle fund adjust it for you (more equity when young, shifting to bonds as you age).

### NPS Account Types — Tier I vs Tier II

**Tier I (Mandatory Retirement Account):**
- This is the main NPS account
- Money is locked until age 60 (with partial withdrawal exceptions)
- Tax benefits under Section 80CCD(1), 80CCD(1B), and 80CCD(2)
- Minimum annual contribution: ₹1,000
- No maximum limit (but tax benefits are capped)

**Tier II (Voluntary Savings Account):**
- Works like a mutual fund — withdraw anytime
- No lock-in, but also **no tax benefits**
- Can only open if you have a Tier I account
- Useful as a flexible savings vehicle, but lacks the tax advantages of Tier I

For most 20-year-olds, **start with Tier I.** The lock-in is actually a feature, not a bug — it prevents you from touching the money.

### Tax Benefits — The Triple Advantage

NPS offers tax benefits that few other instruments can match:

1. **Section 80CCD(1):** Deduction up to 10% of salary (basic + DA) within the overall ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit
2. **Section 80CCD(1B):** Additional ₹50,000 deduction OVER AND ABOVE the ₹1.5 lakh 80C limit. This is exclusive to NPS
3. **Section 80CCD(2):** Employer contribution up to 10% of salary is deductible (no upper limit in the new tax regime)

For a young professional earning ₹6 lakh/year:
- ₹1.5 lakh under 80C (ELSS, insurance, PPF, etc.)
- Additional ₹50,000 under 80CCD(1B) for NPS
- Total deduction: ₹2 lakh → saves ₹31,200 in tax (at 20% slab in new regime)

That's ₹31,200 back in your pocket just for investing ₹50,000 in NPS. Effective cost of investing: ₹18,800 for ₹50,000 of investment.

### The Auto-Choice Lifecycle Fund

If you don't want to actively manage your NPS allocation, choose **Auto Choice — Aggressive (LC-75).** This allocates 75% to equity when you're under 35, gradually shifting to bonds as you age. It's a set-and-forget approach perfect for young investors.

The three Auto-Choice options:
- **Aggressive (LC-75):** 75% equity, 15% corporate bonds, 10% govt bonds (under 35)
- **Moderate (LC-50):** 50% equity, 30% corporate bonds, 20% govt bonds
- **Conservative (LC-25):** 25% equity, 45% corporate bonds, 30% govt bonds

At 20, Aggressive is the clear choice. You have 40 years to ride out market volatility.

### Withdrawal Rules — Know the Lock-in

At age 60, you can withdraw 60% of your corpus as lump sum (tax-free under the new tax regime). The remaining 40% must be used to buy an annuity (monthly pension). If the total corpus is less than ₹5 lakh, you can withdraw 100% as lump sum.

**Partial withdrawal:** After 3 years, you can withdraw up to 25% of your own contributions for specific purposes — higher education, marriage, house purchase, medical emergency. You can do this up to 3 times during the entire tenure, with a 5-year gap between withdrawals.

**Early exit (before 60):** You can exit after 10 years, but must use 80% of the corpus to buy an annuity. Only 20% can be taken as lump sum. This is expensive — avoid early exit if possible.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Kabir, your trust fund is a medium-term wealth engine. NPS is your ultra-long-term wealth engine. They serve different purposes.

Imagine this: when your trust matures, you take ₹50,000 and start an NPS SIP of ₹4,000/month. At 10% average returns over 40 years, that ₹50,000 lump sum + ₹4,000/month SIP grows to approximately **₹3.2 crores** by age 60. You'd invest a total of about ₹19.7 lakh over 40 years. The remaining ₹21 crores+ is pure compounding.

But here's the practical reality: as a final-year college student, you probably can't afford ₹4,000/month right now. Start with ₹1,000/month — the NPS minimum. Even ₹1,000/month from age 20 to 60 at 10% returns gives you approximately **₹63 lakhs.** That's from just ₹4.8 lakh of total investment. The compounding does the heavy lifting.

Your trust fund gave you ₹1,68,000 in gains over 5 years. NPS will give you ₹58+ lakhs in gains over 40 years. They work together.

## Quiz

1. If you invest ₹5,000/month in NPS from age 20 to 60 at 10% returns, approximately how much will you accumulate? — A) ₹50 lakhs B) ₹1 crore C) ₹2.65 crores D) ₹5 crores (Answer: C)

2. What is the additional tax benefit unique to NPS under Section 80CCD(1B)? — A) ₹1.5 lakh deduction B) ₹50,000 deduction above the 80C limit C) ₹1 lakh deduction D) No additional benefit (Answer: B)

3. Which NPS Auto-Choice option is best for a 20-year-old? — A) Conservative (LC-25) B) Moderate (LC-50) C) Aggressive (LC-75) D) None, pick individual funds (Answer: C)

4. At age 60, what percentage of your NPS corpus can you withdraw as tax-free lump sum? — A) 40% B) 50% C) 60% D) 100% (Answer: C)

5. Why is the NPS lock-in until age 60 actually beneficial for young investors? — A) It gives higher interest rates B) It prevents impulsive withdrawals and allows decades of compounding C) The government adds bonus money D) It reduces your tax rate (Answer: B)
