# Module 7: Starting Your Own Business — Basics

**Year:** 8 (Age 20)
**Month:** 7
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the fundamentals of starting a business in India
- Learn the different business structures and their legal implications
- Know the essential financial planning for a new business
- Evaluate whether entrepreneurship is right for you at this stage

## Key Concepts

### Why Start a Business at 20?

India is in the middle of an entrepreneurship explosion. UPI has made digital payments ubiquitous. GST has simplified (somewhat) the tax system. Startup India provides tax holidays and funding support. The barriers to starting a business have never been lower.

At 20, you have three massive advantages: **time, low obligations, and tolerance for failure.** You probably don't have a family to support, a home loan EMI, or lifestyle expenses you can't cut. If your first business fails, you lose time and a small amount of money — but gain invaluable experience. At 35, with a mortgage and two kids, failure has much higher stakes.

This doesn't mean you should drop out of college and start a startup. It means you should start learning the mechanics of business now, even if your "real" business starts after graduation.

### Business Structures in India

**Sole Proprietorship:** Simplest form. You ARE the business. No separate legal entity. Easy to start (just get a GST registration and shop act license). You're personally liable for all debts. Best for freelancers, small traders, service providers. Revenue: ₹0 to ₹50 lakh/year.

**Partnership / LLP (Limited Liability Partnership):** Two or more people running a business together. LLP gives you limited liability (your personal assets are protected from business debts). Requires registration with the Ministry of Corporate Affairs. Best for professional services, small agencies. Registration cost: ₹5,000–₹15,000.

**Private Limited Company:** A separate legal entity. You own shares. Limited liability. Required if you want to raise VC funding. More compliance (annual filings, board meetings, auditor). Registration cost: ₹7,000–₹15,000. Annual compliance cost: ₹15,000–₹30,000. Best for scalable startups seeking investment.

**One Person Company (OPC):** A company with just one director/shareholder. Gives you the benefits of a private limited (limited liability, separate entity) without needing partners. Good middle ground for solo founders.

**Recommendation for a 20-year-old:** Start as a sole proprietorship. Test your idea. If it works and you want to raise funding or bring in partners, convert to LLP or Private Limited later. Don't over-engineer the legal structure before validating the business.

### The Financial Basics — What You Must Know

**Revenue vs Profit:** Revenue is money coming in. Profit is what's left after expenses. A business doing ₹10 lakh revenue but spending ₹11 lakh is losing ₹1 lakh. Focus on profit, not vanity revenue numbers.

**Cash Flow is King:** You can be profitable on paper and still go broke. If customers pay you in 60 days but vendors demand payment in 15 days, you have a cash flow gap. Many profitable businesses die from cash flow problems. Always track when money comes in vs when it goes out.

**Fixed vs Variable Costs:** Fixed costs (rent, salaries, software subscriptions) stay the same whether you sell 1 unit or 1,000. Variable costs (raw materials, shipping, payment gateway fees) scale with sales. Keep fixed costs LOW when starting — work from home, use free tools, don't hire until absolutely necessary.

**Break-Even Point:** The point where revenue equals total costs. Below this, you're losing money. Above it, you're profitable. Calculate this before starting: Fixed Costs ÷ (Price per unit - Variable Cost per unit) = Units needed to break even.

**Working Capital:** Money needed to run day-to-day operations. If you sell on credit, you need working capital to cover expenses until customers pay. Rule of thumb: have 3–6 months of operating expenses as working capital before starting.

### The Lean Startup Approach

Don't spend ₹5 lakh building a product nobody wants. Start lean:

1. **Identify a problem** you or people around you face
2. **Validate demand** — talk to 20 potential customers. Would they pay for a solution?
3. **Build a Minimum Viable Product (MVP)** — the simplest version that solves the core problem
4. **Get your first paying customer** — even one paying customer validates the idea more than 1,000 survey responses
5. **Iterate** — improve based on feedback, charge more as you improve

This approach costs ₹5,000–₹50,000 to start, not ₹5,00,000. Your trust fund at maturity could easily seed a lean startup.

### Legal Essentials — Don't Skip These

- **GST Registration:** Mandatory if revenue exceeds ₹20 lakh/year (₹10 lakh for special category states). Even below this threshold, voluntary GST registration helps if you sell to businesses (they can claim input credit).
- **Current Account:** Open a separate bank account for your business. Never mix personal and business finances.
- **Basic Accounting:** Use Tally, Zoho Books, or even a Google Sheet. Track every rupee in and out. This isn't optional — it's required for GST filing and income tax.
- **Contracts:** Get agreements in writing. Even a simple email confirmation is better than verbal promises. Use legal templates from websites like LegalDesk or Vakilsearch.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Kabir, your trust fund gives you ₹4,68,000 at maturity. That's more than enough seed capital for a lean startup. But here's the discipline: **don't invest more than 20% of your trust fund in a business idea** (₹93,600). The rest should stay invested.

Why? Because most first businesses fail. The Indian startup failure rate is approximately 90%. If you put your entire trust fund into a business and it fails, you're starting from zero. If you put ₹93,600 and it fails, you still have ₹3,74,400 invested and growing. You can try again.

This isn't pessimism — it's risk management. The best entrepreneurs are not gamblers. They calculate risk, limit downside, and iterate fast. Your trust fund is your safety net. Use a small portion to test ideas, and let the rest compound.

Consider this: if you use ₹50,000 from your trust fund to start a freelance digital marketing business (laptop + tools + courses), and you earn ₹30,000/month within 6 months, your ROI is extraordinary. Low capital, high return, learning guaranteed. That's smarter than putting ₹4 lakh into an unproven product startup.

## Quiz

1. Which business structure is best for a 20-year-old testing their first business idea? — A) Private Limited Company B) LLP C) Sole Proprietorship D) Public Limited Company (Answer: C)

2. A business has ₹10 lakh revenue and ₹11 lakh expenses. What is the financial status? — A) Profitable with ₹10 lakh B) Loss of ₹1 lakh C) Break-even D) Cash flow positive (Answer: B)

3. Why is cash flow different from profit? — A) They're the same thing B) Cash flow tracks when money actually moves; profit is an accounting concept C) Cash flow is only for big companies D) Profit is more important than cash flow (Answer: B)

4. What is the recommended maximum to invest from your trust fund in a first business? — A) 100% — go all in B) 50% C) 20% D) 5% (Answer: C)

5. What is a "Minimum Viable Product" (MVP)? — A) The most expensive version of your product B) The simplest version that solves the core customer problem C) A product with no features D) A product only for testing, not selling (Answer: B)
