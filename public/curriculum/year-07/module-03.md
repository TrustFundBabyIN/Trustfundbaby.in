# Module 3: Understanding Salary Slips & CTC
**Year:** 7 (Age 19)

## Learning Objectives
- Decode a salary slip and understand every line item
- Know the difference between CTC, gross salary, and net salary (in-hand)
- Understand deductions like PF, professional tax, and TDS

## Key Concepts

### CTC vs Gross vs In-Hand: The Great Indian Salary Confusion
When a company offers you a job, they quote a "Cost to Company" (CTC) number. Let's say it's ₹6,00,000 per year. Fresh graduates celebrate — "I'm earning ₹50,000 a month!" But they're wrong. CTC is the total cost the company incurs for employing you. It includes components you'll never see in your bank account: employer's Provident Fund contribution, Gratuity provision, insurance premiums, and sometimes even the cost of your office laptop.

**Gross Salary** is what you earn before deductions — it excludes the employer's PF contribution and gratuity but includes your basic pay, HRA, special allowance, and bonuses. **Net Salary (In-Hand)** is what actually hits your bank account after YOUR deductions: employee PF contribution (12% of basic), Professional Tax (₹200/month in most states), and TDS (Tax Deducted at Source based on your projected annual income).

A typical breakdown for a ₹6 lakh CTC might look like: CTC = ₹6,00,000. Gross = ₹5,20,000. In-hand ≈ ₹4,10,000-4,30,000/year or roughly ₹34,000-36,000/month. That gap between ₹50,000 (CTC ÷ 12) and ₹35,000 (actual in-hand) shocks many first-time earners.

### Anatomy of a Salary Slip
Your monthly salary slip is a detailed breakdown. Here's what each component means:

**Basic Salary:** Usually 40-50% of your CTC. This is the foundation — your PF, bonus, and gratuity calculations are based on this number. **HRA (House Rent Allowance):** If you pay rent, a portion of HRA is tax-exempt under Section 10(13A). The exemption is the minimum of: actual HRA received, 50% of basic (metro) or 40% (non-metro), or actual rent minus 10% of basic. **Special Allowance:** The "catch-all" component companies use to make the CTC number look big. Fully taxable. **Employer PF Contribution:** 12% of basic goes to your EPF account. This is real money accumulating for your retirement — it's part of your CTC but not your in-hand. **TDS:** The company deducts tax monthly based on your projected annual income. If they deduct too much, you get a refund when you file ITR.

### Form 16: Your Tax Receipt
At the end of every financial year, your employer gives you Form 16. It's a certificate showing your total salary, exemptions claimed, deductions, and TDS deposited with the government. This document is pre-filled in your ITR — you barely need to do anything. Keep it safe; you'll need it if the tax department ever asks questions.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Your part-time job pays ₹8,000/month — no PF, no TDS, no complex slip. But imagine next year, you land a full-time job at ₹5,00,000 CTC. Your first salary slip might show: Basic = ₹2,00,000/year (₹16,667/month), HRA = ₹1,00,000/year, Special Allowance = ₹1,50,000/year, Employer PF = ₹24,000/year, Gratuity = ₹9,615/year, Insurance = ₹16,385/year. Your in-hand would be roughly ₹35,000-36,000/month after your PF, PT, and TDS. Meanwhile, your trust fund's ₹5,000 SIP continues — that's ₹60,000/year in systematic investing, building on your existing ₹3,42,000. Your trust is your silent wealth engine.

## Quiz
1. What does CTC stand for? — A) Cash To Credit B) Cost To Company C) Corporate Tax Component D) Calculated Total Compensation (Answer: B)
2. What is the typical employee PF contribution rate? — A) 5% of gross B) 12% of basic C) 10% of CTC D) 8% of net salary (Answer: B)
3. Which component of salary can be partially tax-exempt if you pay rent? — A) Basic Salary B) Special Allowance C) HRA D) Employer PF (Answer: C)
4. What document does your employer give you for filing ITR? — A) Salary Certificate B) Form 16 C) Bank Statement D) Offer Letter (Answer: B)
5. If your CTC is ₹6,00,000, your approximate monthly in-hand is closest to: — A) ₹50,000 B) ₹45,000 C) ₹35,000 D) ₹25,000 (Answer: C)
