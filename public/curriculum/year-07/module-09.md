# Module 9: Understanding Loans (Education, Personal)
**Year:** 7 (Age 19)
**Month:** 9
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the types of loans available to young Indians — education loan, personal loan, and their key differences
- Learn how interest rates work — fixed vs floating, reducing balance vs flat rate
- Know the true cost of borrowing using EMI calculations
- Understand when taking a loan is smart and when it's a trap

## Key Concepts

### Education Loans: Investing in Yourself
An education loan is one of the few loans that's genuinely an investment. If an MBA from a good college costs ₹15 lakh but increases your salary from ₹5 lakh to ₹15 lakh per year, the ROI is massive. Education loans in India are offered by banks (SBI, Bank of Baroda, HDFC) and NBFCs (Credila, Avanse).

Key features: Interest rates range from 8.5-14% depending on the bank, loan amount, and whether it's a secured (with collateral) or unsecured loan. Government banks offer lower rates for loans up to ₹7.5 lakh (no collateral required under the Credit Guarantee Fund Scheme). Moratorium period — you don't pay EMIs while studying + 6-12 months after getting a job. Interest may still accrue during this period (simple interest or compound, depending on the bank). Repayment tenure is typically 5-15 years. Tax benefit under Section 80E — the entire interest paid (no upper limit) is deductible from taxable income for up to 8 years.

**The smart move:** Take an education loan if the degree significantly increases your earning potential. Use Section 80E to save on tax while repaying. Don't take an education loan for a degree with poor placement records.

### Personal Loans: The Expensive Convenience
Personal loans are unsecured (no collateral), which is why they're expensive — 10.5-24% interest rates. Banks give them easily if you have a steady income and good CIBIL score. But "easily available" doesn't mean "should be taken."

Personal loans are EMI-based: you borrow a lump sum and repay in fixed monthly installments (12-60 months). The EMI is calculated using the reducing balance method — interest is charged only on the outstanding principal, not the original loan amount. This is better than flat rate (where you pay interest on the full amount throughout the tenure).

**When personal loans make sense:** Medical emergency beyond insurance coverage, genuine urgent need with no alternative, or consolidating high-interest credit card debt into a lower-rate personal loan.

**When they don't:** Buying a phone, funding a vacation, paying for a wedding you can't afford, or covering regular expenses (that's a budgeting problem, not a lending problem).

### How to Calculate True Cost
The EMI formula is: EMI = P × r × (1+r)^n / [(1+r)^n - 1], where P = principal, r = monthly interest rate, n = number of months. For a ₹3 lakh personal loan at 14% for 3 years: EMI ≈ ₹10,258. Total payment = ₹3,69,288. Interest paid = ₹69,288 — that's 23% extra. Use an online EMI calculator before taking any loan. Always know the total cost, not just the EMI.

### The Golden Rule
Borrow only for assets that appreciate (education, property) or emergencies. Never borrow for depreciating assets (phones, gadgets, vacations) or lifestyle inflation. Your trust fund grew ₹1,02,000 in 4 years. A personal loan's interest can eat that gain in 1 year.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Say you want to pursue a professional course costing ₹2 lakh. You have ₹3,42,000 in your trust but can't access it until 21. Options: (1) Education loan of ₹2 lakh at 10% for 3 years. EMI ≈ ₹6,453. Total interest: ₹32,308. Section 80E saves tax on this interest. (2) Personal loan of ₹2 lakh at 14% for 3 years. EMI ≈ ₹6,854. Total interest: ₹46,744. No tax benefit. (3) Wait until trust matures at 21, use ₹2 lakh from there. Zero interest, zero debt, but delayed by 2 years.

The best choice depends on how time-sensitive the course is. If the course increases your earning potential significantly, the education loan makes sense. If it can wait, let your trust do its job.

## Quiz
1. What is the typical interest rate range for education loans in India? — A) 4-6% B) 8.5-14% C) 18-24% D) 25-30% (Answer: B)
2. Section 80E provides tax benefit on: — A) Principal repayment B) Entire interest paid on education loan C) Both principal and interest D) Only for home loans (Answer: B)
3. A personal loan charges interest on: — A) The original loan amount throughout B) Only the outstanding reducing balance C) Nothing — it's interest-free D) A flat fee regardless of amount (Answer: B)
4. Which type of loan is MOST expensive in terms of interest rate? — A) Education loan B) Home loan C) Personal loan D) Gold loan (Answer: C)
5. Taking a loan for which purpose is considered financially smart? — A) Vacation B) New phone C) Education with good ROI D) Designer clothes (Answer: C)
