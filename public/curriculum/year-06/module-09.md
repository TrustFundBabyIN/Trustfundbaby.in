## Key Concepts

### Crashes Are Normal — Not Exceptions

Here's the most important thing to know about market crashes: **they happen regularly and they always recover**. Since independence, India's stock market has experienced dozens of significant corrections (10-20% drops), bear markets (20%+ drops), and outright crashes (30%+ in weeks). Every single time, the market eventually recovered and went on to new highs. Every. Single. Time.

This doesn't mean crashes aren't painful — they are. But understanding crash history turns panic into perspective. If you know that Sensex went from 100 in 1979 to 73,000+ in 2025 — despite numerous crashes along the way — you understand that volatility is the price of admission for long-term wealth creation.

### Major Crashes in Indian Market History

**1. Harshad Mehta Scam (1992):** The Sensex was artificially inflated by stockbroker Harshad Mehta using bank funds. When the scam was exposed, the Sensex crashed from 4,500 to about 2,500 — a 45% drop. This led to the creation of SEBI as a stronger regulator and the dematerialisation of shares. The market recovered within 3 years.

**2. Asian Financial Crisis (1997-98):** Currency devaluations across Southeast Asia rattled Indian markets. The Sensex fell about 40%. India's nuclear tests in 1998 added to the uncertainty. But the market bottomed in 1998 and doubled by 2000.

**3. Dot-Com Bust (2000-01):** IT stocks had gone parabolic. Infosys went from ₹300 to ₹10,000+ (split-adjusted). When the bubble burst, tech stocks crashed 60-80%. The Sensex fell from ~6,000 to ~2,600. This was devastating for people who chased IT stocks at peak valuations — a textbook case of herd mentality and recency bias (Module 8).

**4. Global Financial Crisis (2008):** The mother of all crashes. Lehman Brothers collapsed, global credit froze, and Sensex plummeted from 21,000 to about 8,000 — a 60% drop in one year. This was the scariest crash most living Indian investors have experienced. But those who stayed invested saw the Sensex recover to 21,000 by 2010 and surpass it significantly in the years after.

**5. COVID-19 Crash (2020):** In March 2020, the Sensex fell from 42,000 to 26,000 in weeks — a 38% drop. The fear was unprecedented — a global pandemic, lockdowns, economic shutdown. Yet the market bottomed on March 23, 2020, and within 8 months had recovered all losses. By 2024, the Sensex had nearly tripled from its COVID bottom.

### Why Crashes Recover — The Economic Engine

Markets recover because economies recover. India's GDP has grown from ₹10 lakh crore in 1991 to over ₹300 lakh crore in 2025. Companies keep innovating, people keep consuming, and the population keeps growing. A crash doesn't destroy these fundamental forces — it just temporarily scares investors into selling.

Every crash in Indian history has been a **buying opportunity in hindsight**. The investors who bought HDFC Bank or Infosys during the 2008 crash, when everyone was panicking, made fortunes. The ones who sold at the bottom locked in their losses permanently.

### The SIP Advantage During Crashes

This is where your trust's SIP strategy shines. When markets crash, your monthly ₹5,000 SIP buys **more units** at lower prices. This is called **rupee cost averaging** — you automatically buy more when prices are low and less when prices are high. Over time, this lowers your average purchase cost significantly.

If a crash happens and NAV drops from ₹100 to ₹70, your ₹5,000 SIP buys 71 units instead of 50. When the NAV recovers to ₹100, those extra units are worth much more. Crashes aren't just survivable with SIPs — they're actually beneficial.

### How to Prepare for the Next Crash

You will experience crashes in your investing lifetime. Here's how to prepare:

1. **Keep an emergency fund** (6 months expenses) outside your investments — so you never need to sell during a crash
2. **Diversify across asset classes** — your trust holds equity AND debt, which cushions falls
3. **Stay invested** — the worst thing you can do is sell during a crash
4. **Rebalance** — after a crash, equity becomes "cheap" — consider shifting some debt allocation to equity
5. **Zoom out** — always look at the 10-year chart when the 1-month chart scares you

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Your trust started in January 2025, during a period of relatively strong markets. But imagine a 25% crash happens next year — your ₹2,34,000 trust could temporarily drop to ₹1,75,000. The instinct would be to stop SIPs or sell. But history tells us the opposite: keep investing, because your ₹5,000 SIP is now buying more units at cheaper prices. Your trust's HDFC Mid-Cap fund (₹82,400) might temporarily drop to ₹62,000, but with continued SIPs at lower NAVs, the recovery would be even stronger.

The 2008 crash investors who held HDFC Mid-Cap saw their investments grow 5-10x over the next decade. Crashes are where real wealth is built — if you have the discipline to stay the course.
