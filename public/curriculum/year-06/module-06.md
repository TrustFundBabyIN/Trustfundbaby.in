## Key Concepts

### What Is an ETF?

An **Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)** is a type of investment fund that trades on stock exchanges just like individual stocks. Unlike mutual funds, which you buy and sell through the fund house at the end-of-day NAV, ETFs can be bought and sold throughout the trading day (9:15 AM to 3:30 PM) at real-time market prices. Think of an ETF as a basket of stocks or bonds that you can buy with a single transaction.

The most popular ETF in India is the **Nippon India ETF Nifty BeES**, which tracks the Nifty 50 index. When you buy one unit of this ETF, you're essentially buying a tiny slice of all 50 companies in the Nifty 50. It's like ordering a thali instead of selecting individual dishes — you get a pre-assembled, diversified meal in one go.

### ETFs vs Mutual Funds — Key Differences

The fundamental difference is **how they trade**. Mutual funds are bought and sold at the NAV (Net Asset Value) calculated at the end of each trading day. ETFs trade on the exchange throughout the day at market-determined prices, which can be slightly above or below the NAV (this difference is called the "premium" or "discount").

**Expense ratios** are another major difference. ETFs typically have much lower expense ratios than actively managed mutual funds. For example, the Nippon India ETF Nifty BeES has an expense ratio of about 0.05%, while an actively managed large-cap fund might charge 1-2%. Over decades, this difference compounds significantly — a 1.5% annual fee difference on a ₹10 lakh investment over 20 years can cost you several lakhs in returns.

However, ETFs require a **demat account** (like Zerodha, Groww, or Upstox) and you pay brokerage on each transaction, just like buying a stock. Mutual funds can be bought directly from the fund house without brokerage. Also, for SIP investing (which your trust uses), mutual funds are more convenient because they support automatic SIPs. ETFs require you to manually buy units each time, though some platforms are now offering ETF SIP features.

### Popular ETFs in India

India's ETF market has grown massively, driven by government disinvestment (selling stakes in public sector companies through ETFs) and retail investor awareness. Here are the key categories:

**Broad Market ETFs:**
- Nippon India ETF Nifty BeES — tracks Nifty 50
- SBI ETF Nifty 50 — another Nifty 50 tracker
- Motilal Oswal Nasdaq 100 ETF — tracks US tech giants (Apple, Microsoft, Google)

**Sectoral/Thematic ETFs:**
- CPSE ETF — tracks Central Public Sector Enterprises
- Bharat 22 ETF — 22 government-held companies
- Nippon India ETF PSU Bank BeES — tracks PSU banks

**Gold ETFs:**
- Nippon India ETF Gold BeES
- SBI Gold ETF

**Bond ETFs:**
- Bharat Bond ETF (various maturity dates) — invests in AAA-rated PSU bonds

### When to Choose ETFs Over Mutual Funds

ETFs make sense when you want:
1. **Low cost** — the cheapest way to own a diversified portfolio
2. **Intraday trading** — you want to buy/sell during market hours
3. **Sector bets** — you want specific exposure to gold, PSU banks, or US tech
4. **Transparency** — you know exactly what stocks are in the basket (published daily)

Mutual funds are better when you want:
1. **Automatic SIPs** — set and forget
2. **Active management** — a fund manager picking stocks
3. **No demat required** — simpler to start
4. **Small-cap or niche strategies** — many aren't available as ETFs

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Your trust currently invests in four mutual funds, which is great for hands-off, active management. But as you approach 21 and begin managing your own investments, ETFs could complement your portfolio. For example, adding a **Motilal Oswal Nasdaq 100 ETF** would give you exposure to US tech companies — something the Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund does to some extent, but an ETF would let you increase that exposure at a fraction of the cost.

Your trust's ICICI Corporate Bond Fund (₹34,600, +28.1%) could eventually be supplemented with a **Bharat Bond ETF** for lower-cost debt exposure. As you graduate from trust-fund beneficiary to independent investor, mixing mutual funds (for active management) with ETFs (for low-cost passive exposure) gives you the best of both worlds.
