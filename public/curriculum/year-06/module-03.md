# Module 3: P/E Ratio & Valuation Basics
**Year:** 6 (Age 18)
**Month:** 3
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what the P/E ratio tells investors about a stock
- Learn how to calculate and interpret P/E in the Indian market context
- Compare P/E ratios across sectors and against market benchmarks
- Distinguish between trailing P/E, forward P/E, and PEG ratio

## Key Concepts

### What Is the P/E Ratio?

The Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio is the most widely used valuation metric in the stock market. It answers a simple but critical question: **how much are investors willing to pay for every rupee of a company's earnings?** The formula is straightforward: **P/E = Stock Price ÷ Earnings Per Share (EPS)**.

For example, if a stock trades at ₹2,000 and its EPS over the last year is ₹100, the P/E ratio is 20. This means investors are paying ₹20 for every ₹1 of earnings. Think of it like buying a shop — if a shop earns ₹1 lakh per year and the owner asks ₹20 lakhs for it, you're paying a P/E of 20. The question is: is that shop worth ₹20 lakhs?

### High P/E vs Low P/E — It's Not Always Obvious

A common mistake beginners make is assuming low P/E = cheap = good deal, and high P/E = expensive = bad deal. Reality is more nuanced. A high P/E can mean two things: either the stock is genuinely overvalued, or the market expects the company to grow rapidly in the future, so investors are willing to pay a premium today.

Consider TCS versus a small IT company. TCS might trade at a P/E of 30 because investors trust its consistent growth, brand, and global presence. A small IT company trading at P/E of 12 might look "cheap," but it could be cheap because its revenue is declining and it's losing clients. Context matters enormously. In India, sectors like IT and FMCG (think Hindustan Unilever) naturally command higher P/E ratios because of stable, predictable earnings. Cyclical sectors like metals or real estate tend to have lower P/E ratios during boom periods (ironically) because the market knows their earnings won't last.

### The Nifty 50 P/E — A Market-Wide Gauge

The Nifty 50 has a combined P/E ratio that reflects the overall market valuation. Historically, the Nifty P/E has ranged between 15 (during crashes) and 35+ (during bull market peaks). As of recent years, the Nifty P/E has hovered around 20-24.

When the Nifty P/E is below 18, the market is generally considered undervalued — a good time to invest. When it crosses 28-30, caution is warranted — the market may be running ahead of fundamentals. This isn't a precise timing tool, but it helps you understand whether you're buying into an expensive or reasonably priced market.

### Forward P/E and the PEG Ratio

**Trailing P/E** uses earnings from the past 12 months. **Forward P/E** uses estimated earnings for the next 12 months, making it more forward-looking. If a company's trailing P/E is 25 but analysts expect strong growth, the forward P/E might be 18, making it look more attractive.

The **PEG Ratio** (P/E ÷ Earnings Growth Rate) takes this a step further. A P/E of 30 with 30% annual earnings growth gives a PEG of 1.0, which is considered fairly valued. A P/E of 15 with only 5% growth gives a PEG of 3.0, which is actually expensive. The PEG ratio helps you compare high-growth and low-growth companies on a level playing field.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Let's look at your trust's **HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund** (₹82,400, +30.8%). Mid-cap stocks in India typically trade at P/E ratios of 20-35, depending on the sector and growth prospects. This fund invests in companies like Persistent Systems, Max Healthcare, and Bajaj Finance — businesses that the market values at higher P/E ratios because of strong growth trajectories.

If you compare this to the **ICICI Corporate Bond Fund** in your trust (₹34,600, +28.1%), bonds don't have P/E ratios — they're valued on yield and credit quality. This is why diversification matters: when stock valuations look stretched (high P/E), bond funds provide stability. The HDFC Mid-Cap fund's 30.8% gain reflects both good stock selection and favourable market valuations during this period.

## Quiz
1. A stock priced at ₹500 has an EPS of ₹25. What is its P/E ratio? — A) 10 B) 20 C) 25 D) 50 (Answer: B)
2. A high P/E ratio always means a stock is overvalued. True or False? — A) True B) False — it can also indicate expected growth C) Depends on the sector D) Both B and C (Answer: D)
3. What is the historical P/E range of the Nifty 50? — A) 5-10 B) 10-15 C) 15-35 D) 40-60 (Answer: C)
4. What does the PEG ratio adjust for? — A) Dividends B) Earnings growth C) Debt levels D) Market cap (Answer: B)
5. A company has a P/E of 20 and 40% earnings growth. What is its PEG? — A) 0.5 B) 1.0 C) 2.0 D) 8.0 (Answer: A)
