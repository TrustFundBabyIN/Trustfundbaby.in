## Key Concepts

### What Is a Stock Exchange?

A stock exchange is a marketplace where buyers and sellers come together to trade shares of publicly listed companies. Think of it like a massive, digital version of a bazaar — except instead of vegetables and clothes, people are buying and selling pieces of companies. In India, we have two major exchanges: the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE) and the National Stock Exchange (NSE).

BSE is the older of the two, established in 1875, making it the oldest stock exchange in Asia. It's located on Dalal Street in Mumbai — a name every Indian investor knows. NSE was founded in 1992 and brought electronic trading to India, revolutionising how stocks are bought and sold. Today, both exchanges operate entirely online, and you can trade from your phone while sitting in a chai ki tapri.

### NSE vs BSE — What's the Difference?

Both exchanges list thousands of companies, but they differ in scale and index composition. BSE has over 5,000 listed companies and its flagship index is the **Sensex** (Sensitive Index), which tracks 30 large, well-established companies. NSE has around 2,000 listed companies and its benchmark index is the **Nifty 50**, which tracks 50 large-cap stocks across 13 sectors.

In practice, most retail investors in India trade on NSE because of its higher liquidity — meaning more buyers and sellers are active, so you can buy or sell stocks quickly without major price impact. However, BSE has more listed companies, giving investors a wider universe of stocks to choose from. SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) regulates both exchanges, ensuring fair play and investor protection.

### How Trading Works — The Mechanics

When you place a buy or sell order through a broker (like Zerodha, Groww, or Angel One), the order goes to the exchange's matching engine. If someone is willing to sell at your buy price (or buy at your sell price), the trade executes. This happens in milliseconds.

India follows a **T+1 settlement cycle** — this means if you buy shares today, they'll be delivered to your demat account by the next business day. The trading hours are 9:15 AM to 3:30 PM IST, Monday to Friday, with a pre-market session from 9:00 to 9:15 AM. Every transaction is overseen by clearing corporations (NSCCL for NSE, Indian Clearing Corporation for BSE) that guarantee settlement, so even if the other party defaults, your trade is safe.

### Sensex and Nifty — The Market Thermometer

When someone says "the market is up today," they're usually referring to Sensex or Nifty. These indices act as a thermometer for the overall market. If Nifty 50 rises by 200 points, it means the aggregate value of the 50 constituent stocks has gone up. The base year for Sensex is 1978-79 (base value 100), and for Nifty it's 1995 (base value 1,000).

As of recent data, Sensex hovers around 73,000-75,000 and Nifty around 22,000-23,000. These aren't random numbers — they represent decades of compounding, economic growth, and the collective confidence of millions of investors. When you invest in an index fund tracking Nifty 50, you're essentially buying a tiny slice of India's 50 biggest companies.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Your trust fund has been investing ₹5,000 monthly since January 2025. The **Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund** in your portfolio — currently valued at ₹64,200 (up 42.7%) — invests partly in stocks listed on NSE and BSE. When you see this fund's NAV growing, it's because the fund manager is buying shares on these exchanges and those shares are appreciating in value.

For example, Parag Parikh Flexi Cap holds stocks like ITC, Bajaj Holdings, and Microsoft (via international allocation). Every time ITC's stock price rises on BSE or NSE, your fund's NAV inches up. The Sensex and Nifty indices serve as benchmarks — fund managers aim to beat these indices, and Parag Parikh's 42.7% return significantly outpaces what the broader market delivered over the same period.
