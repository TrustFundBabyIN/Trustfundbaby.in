## Key Concepts

### What Is Technical Analysis?

While fundamental analysis asks "what should I buy?", technical analysis asks "when should I buy?" Technical analysis studies past price movements and trading volumes to predict future price direction. It assumes that all information about a company — earnings, news, management changes — is already reflected in the stock price, and that prices move in trends driven by human psychology.

Technical analysts (or "chartists") live on NSE and BSE trading floors in spirit, if not literally. They use candlestick charts, trend lines, moving averages, and various indicators to spot opportunities. In India, technical analysis is hugely popular among retail traders on platforms like Zerodha Kite, Upstox, and Angel One, where real-time charting tools are freely available.

### Reading Candlestick Charts

A candlestick shows four prices for a given time period (1 minute, 1 day, 1 week): **Open, High, Low, and Close (OHLC)**. If the close is higher than the open, the candle is usually green (or hollow) — indicating a bullish (upward) session. If the close is lower, it's red (or filled) — bearish (downward).

The thin lines extending above and below the candle body are called **wicks** (or shadows). They show the highest and lowest prices during that period. A long wick above a small body suggests sellers pushed the price down from its high — a potential reversal signal.

Common single-candle patterns include the **Doji** (open and close are almost equal — indecision), **Hammer** (long lower wick, small body at the top — bullish reversal), and **Shooting Star** (long upper wick, small body at the bottom — bearish reversal). In India, patterns on Nifty 50 stocks tend to be more reliable than on small-caps because of higher trading volume.

### Support, Resistance, and Trend Lines

**Support** is a price level where a falling stock tends to stop and bounce back up — like a floor. **Resistance** is a price level where a rising stock tends to stall and fall back — like a ceiling. These levels exist because of collective human psychology: traders remember previous prices where the stock reversed.

For example, if Reliance stock has bounced off ₹2,400 three times in the past year, ₹2,400 becomes a strong support level. If it keeps failing to break above ₹2,800, that's resistance. When a stock finally breaks through resistance with high volume, the old resistance often becomes the new support — and vice versa.

**Trend lines** connect successive highs (in a downtrend) or successive lows (in an uptrend). An uptrend is defined as a series of higher highs and higher lows. A downtrend is lower highs and lower lows. The golden rule of technical analysis: **trade with the trend**. Fighting the trend is like swimming against the current — exhausting and usually futile.

### Moving Averages — The Smooth Operator

A **moving average (MA)** smooths out price data by calculating the average closing price over a specific number of days. The two most popular are the **50-day MA** (short-term trend) and the **200-day MA** (long-term trend).

When the 50-day MA crosses above the 200-day MA, it's called a **Golden Cross** — a bullish signal. When the 50-day crosses below the 200-day, it's a **Death Cross** — bearish. In India, the Nifty 50's Golden Cross in 2020 after the COVID crash signalled the start of a massive bull run. These aren't foolproof, but they're widely watched by traders and institutional investors alike.

### Limitations of Technical Analysis

Technical analysis is not a crystal ball. It works best in liquid markets (like Nifty 50 stocks) and shorter time frames. It can't predict black swan events — a sudden policy change by RBI, a geopolitical crisis, or a company-specific scandal can blow through every support level on the chart. Many successful Indian investors, including the ones managing your trust's funds, primarily use fundamental analysis and only occasionally reference technical levels for timing entries.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Your trust's **HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund** (₹82,400, +30.8%) is managed fundamentally, but the fund managers likely use technical analysis to time their entries and exits. If they see a mid-cap stock they like fundamentally, they might wait for it to hit a support level before buying — getting a better price.

For instance, if the fund wanted to increase its position in a stock like Coforge (an IT mid-cap), the manager might look at the 200-day moving average as a potential buy zone. Combining fundamental analysis ("this company is worth owning") with technical analysis ("the price is at a good entry point") is the hallmark of sophisticated fund management.
