## Key Concepts

### Why Go International?

India is one of the fastest-growing major economies, but putting all your eggs in one basket — even a great basket — is risky. International investing provides **geographic diversification**, meaning your portfolio isn't entirely dependent on India's economic and political fortunes. If India's market stagnates due to a policy misstep or global event, international holdings can cushion the blow.

Moreover, some of the world's greatest companies — Apple, Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Tesla, Nvidia — aren't listed in India. These companies have generated extraordinary returns over the past decade. As an Indian investor, excluding them from your portfolio means missing out on some of the best investment opportunities in human history. The US stock market (S&P 500) has outperformed the Indian market (Nifty 50) over several long-term periods, particularly driven by the tech sector.

These funds handle currency conversion, international brokerage, and regulatory compliance — you just invest in rupees like any other mutual fund.

**2. Direct International Stocks (via Liberalised Remittance Scheme - LRS):**
Indian residents can remit up to $250,000 per financial year abroad under RBI's LRS. Platforms like **Vested**, **INDmoney**, and **Groww** allow you to buy US stocks directly — Apple, Tesla, Amazon, or even fractional shares (buying a portion of a ₹1.5 lakh-per-share stock for ₹1,000).

**3. International ETFs Listed in India:**
Some ETFs traded on NSE/BSE give international exposure:
- Motilal Oswal Nasdaq 100 ETF
- Nippon India ETF Hang Seng BeES (Hong Kong)
- Mirae Asset NYSE FANG+ ETF

**4. Global Mutual Funds of Funds (FoFs):**
These are Indian mutual funds that invest in underlying international mutual funds. They provide diversification across multiple countries and asset classes in a single investment.

### Currency Risk — The Double-Edged Sword

When you invest internationally, you're exposed to **currency risk**. If you invest in US stocks and the rupee depreciates against the dollar, you gain — your dollar-denominated returns become worth more in rupees. If the rupee appreciates, you lose some returns.

Historically, the Indian rupee has depreciated against the US dollar over the long term (from ₹8 in 1991 to ₹83+ in 2025). This means Indian investors in US markets have generally enjoyed a currency tailwind. A 10% return in US stocks becomes a 12-13% return in rupee terms when the rupee depreciates 2-3% annually. However, this isn't guaranteed — sharp rupee appreciation can hurt returns.

### LRS Rules and Taxation

If investing directly via LRS:
- **TCS (Tax Collected at Source):** 20% TCS on remittances above ₹7 lakh per year (claimable as tax credit while filing ITR)
- **Capital Gains Tax:** Short-term gains (holding < 24 months for listed international stocks) taxed at your income tax slab rate. Long-term gains (24+ months) taxed at 12.5% above ₹1.25 lakh
- **Dividend Tax:** US dividends are taxed at 25% in the US (reduced from 30% under India-US tax treaty). You can claim credit for this tax in India

For Indian mutual funds with international exposure, the taxation follows the same rules as domestic equity funds (since the 2023-24 budget changes), making them more tax-efficient for most investors.

### Building a Global Portfolio — Practical Advice

A sensible allocation for an Indian investor:
- **60-70% Indian equities** — your home market, which you understand best
- **15-25% International equities** — US, global, or emerging markets
- **10-15% Debt** — Indian bonds/FDs for stability

Your trust already does this implicitly — the Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund's international allocation means you're already invested in global giants. When you take over at 21, consider increasing international exposure through a dedicated US index fund (like Motilal Oswal S&P 500) for broader diversification.
