## Key Concepts

### The Three Pillars of Financial Statements

Every publicly listed company in India — from Reliance Industries to HDFC Bank — must publish its financial statements quarterly and annually. These are audited by independent chartered accountants and filed with the stock exchanges. There are three key documents you need to understand: the **Balance Sheet**, the **Profit & Loss Statement** (P&L), and the **Cash Flow Statement**.

Think of it this way: the Balance Sheet is a snapshot — it tells you what the company owns (assets) and owes (liabilities) at a specific point in time. The P&L statement is a movie — it shows how much money came in (revenue), how much went out (expenses), and what was left (profit) over a period. The Cash Flow Statement tracks the actual movement of cash, because profit and cash are not the same thing. A company can show profit on paper but still run out of cash.

### Reading the Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet follows one golden rule: **Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders' Equity**. Everything the company owns is funded either by debt (liabilities) or by the owners' money (equity).

On the asset side, you'll see current assets (cash, inventory, receivables — things convertible to cash within a year) and non-current assets (property, plants, machinery, long-term investments). On the liabilities side, there are current liabilities (short-term loans, payables) and non-current liabilities (long-term debt, bonds issued). Shareholders' equity includes the share capital raised and retained earnings (profits the company kept instead of distributing as dividends).

A healthy balance sheet typically shows manageable debt levels. In India, the **Debt-to-Equity Ratio** is a key metric — for most sectors, a ratio below 1 is considered healthy. Banks are an exception since their business model involves taking deposits (debt) and lending. When you look at HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund in your trust, many of its holdings are companies with strong balance sheets and low debt.

### The P&L Statement — Where the Money Comes From

The Profit & Loss statement starts with **Revenue** (also called "top line") — the total sales a company made during the period. From this, the company subtracts various costs: cost of goods sold, operating expenses, depreciation, interest on loans, and taxes. What's left at the bottom is the **Net Profit** (the "bottom line").

The progression matters. **Gross Profit** (Revenue minus Cost of Goods) tells you about production efficiency. **Operating Profit** (Gross Profit minus Operating Expenses) tells you about operational efficiency. **Net Profit** (after interest and taxes) tells you what shareholders actually get. If a company's revenue is growing but net profit is shrinking, that's a warning sign — costs are eating into margins.

In India, companies like TCS and Infosys are known for fat profit margins (20-25% net margin) because they sell software services with low variable costs. On the other hand, a steel company like Tata Steel has thin margins because raw materials are expensive. Comparing margins only makes sense within the same sector.

### Cash Flow Statement — Follow the Money

This is the most underrated financial statement. It tracks three types of cash movement: **Operating Activities** (cash from core business), **Investing Activities** (cash spent on assets or earned from selling them), and **Financing Activities** (cash from borrowing or issuing shares, minus repayments and dividends).

A company with positive operating cash flow is generating real money from its business. If a company reports profits but has negative operating cash flow, it could mean customers aren't paying on time or the company is inflating revenue. This is a major red flag. In India, several infrastructure and real estate companies have faced trouble precisely because they showed profits on the P&L but had terrible cash flows.

## Quiz
1. What is the fundamental equation of a Balance Sheet? — A) Revenue = Expenses + Profit B) Assets = Liabilities + Equity C) Cash = Revenue - Expenses D) Profit = Revenue - Tax (Answer: B)
2. Which financial statement shows actual cash movement? — A) Balance Sheet B) P&L Statement C) Cash Flow Statement D) Annual Report (Answer: C)
3. What does "top line" refer to? — A) Net Profit B) Revenue C) Operating Profit D) Gross Profit (Answer: B)
4. A company shows profits but negative operating cash flow. What does this suggest? — A) Strong growth B) Potential accounting issues C) High dividends D) Low debt (Answer: B)
5. What is generally considered a healthy Debt-to-Equity ratio for non-banking companies in India? — A) Below 0.5 B) Below 1 C) Below 2 D) Below 3 (Answer: B)
