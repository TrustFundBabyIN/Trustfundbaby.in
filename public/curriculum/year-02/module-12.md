## Key Concepts

### What You Learned This Year — The Big Picture
Year 2 was about understanding the systems that move money through India and the world. Let's connect the dots:

**Banks are the foundation.** You learned how banks work — taking deposits, lending money, and profiting from the interest rate spread. The RBI watches over everything, setting the repo rate that ripples through every financial product you'll ever use. Without banks, the economy wouldn't function.

**Interest and inflation are locked in battle.** Your money in a savings account earns 3–4% interest, but if inflation is running at 6%, you're actually losing purchasing power by 2–3% every year. This is why simply "saving" isn't enough — you need to invest in things that grow faster than inflation. Compound interest is your superpower when your investments beat inflation consistently over time.

**FDs and RDs are safe but limited.** They're great for guaranteed returns and building the saving habit, but their returns barely keep up with inflation. They're part of your diversification strategy, not the whole strategy.

**The stock market is where long-term wealth is built.** Shares represent ownership in real companies. BSE and NSE are India's marketplaces. SEBI keeps things fair. Stock prices move based on supply and demand, company performance, and market sentiment — but over long periods, good companies grow and so does your investment.

**Diversification protects you.** Don't bet everything on one stock, one sector, or one asset class. Spread your investments across equity, debt, gold, and cash. Mutual funds give you instant diversification even with small amounts — which is exactly what your Trust Fund Baby SIP does.

**Digital money is India's present and future.** UPI has made India the world leader in digital payments. Understanding how digital payments work — and how to protect yourself from scams — is essential financial literacy.

### Your Trust Fund Baby Portfolio — Where You Stand
Your Trust Fund Baby investment has been running for about 2 years now. Here's what's likely happening:

Your monthly SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) is buying units of a mutual fund. Some months, when the market is low, your fixed SIP amount buys more units (because each unit is cheaper). Other months, when the market is high, it buys fewer units. Over time, this averages out your purchase price — this is called rupee cost averaging, something you learned about in Year 1.

The power of compounding is just starting to show. In Year 1, the returns might have seemed small. By Year 2, you're starting to see returns on your returns. By Year 5 or 6, the compounding effect will be dramatic. And by Year 7, when you turn 21 and receive the trust fund, the growth could be substantial — especially if the fund has been investing in equity mutual funds that have averaged 12–15% annual returns.

Think about it this way: if your SIP is ₹5,000 per month and the fund grows at 12% annually, by age 21 you'll have invested ₹4,20,000 (84 months × ₹5,000), but the value of your portfolio could be approximately ₹5,50,000–₹6,00,000 thanks to compounding. The longer you stay invested, the more powerful this effect becomes.

## Real-World Example
Meet Priya (a fictional composite based on real stories). Priya's parents started a Trust Fund Baby SIP of ₹3,000 per month when she was 12. By the time she was 14 (end of Year 2), her fund had grown to about ₹80,000 on total invested capital of ₹72,000. She understood that the ₹8,000 gain was from her investments growing, not just the money she put in. She also knew that during one bad month, the value dropped to ₹68,000 — but she didn't panic because she understood market fluctuations. By the time she's 21, her ₹3,000 monthly SIP could grow to approximately ₹4–5 lakh. The financial literacy she gained from Trust Fund Baby's curriculum meant she didn't just receive money at 21 — she received the knowledge to make that money work for the rest of her life.

## Quiz
1. If your savings account earns 3.5% interest but inflation is 6%, what's happening to your purchasing power? — A) Growing by 9.5% B) Shrinking by 2.5% C) Staying the same D) Growing by 3.5% (Answer: B)
2. What is rupee cost averaging? — A) Converting rupees to dollars B) Buying more units when prices are low and fewer when high through regular SIP C) Averaging your bank balance daily D) The RBI's exchange rate policy (Answer: B)
3. Which regulatory body oversees India's stock markets? — A) RBI B) NPCI C) SEBI D) DICGC (Answer: C)
4. If you invest ₹5,000 per month via SIP for 84 months at 12% annual growth, approximately how much will your portfolio be worth? — A) ₹4,20,000 (same as invested) B) ₹5,50,000–₹6,00,000 C) ₹10,00,000 D) ₹4,50,000 (Answer: B)
5. What is the single biggest advantage you have as a 14-year-old investor? — A) You have more money than adults B) Time — you have more years for compounding to work C) You don't pay taxes D) You get better interest rates (Answer: B)
