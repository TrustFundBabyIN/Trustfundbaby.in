# Module 1: How Banks Work
**Year:** 2 (Age 14)
**Month:** 1
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what banks do and why they exist
- Learn how banks make money using deposits
- Know the difference between savings and current accounts
- Understand the role of RBI in regulating Indian banks

## Key Concepts

### What Is a Bank, Really?
Think of a bank as a giant, super-secure piggy bank that millions of people trust with their money. But unlike your piggy bank at home, a bank doesn't just sit on your cash — it puts your money to work.

When you deposit ₹10,000 in your SBI savings account, the bank doesn't lock that exact note in a vault for you. It takes that money and lends part of it to someone else — maybe a person buying a house, or a business owner expanding their shop. In return for lending out the money, the bank charges interest. A portion of that interest comes back to you as the interest on your savings account. That's the basic deal: you park your money safely, the bank puts it to work, and both of you benefit.

Banks have been around for centuries. In India, the Reserve Bank of India (RBI) acts as the "bank of banks" — it makes sure all banks play fair, keep enough cash reserves, and don't take reckless risks with your money. Without the RBI watching over things, banks could go wild with your deposits, and we'd have chaos.

### Types of Accounts
When you walk into an HDFC or ICICI branch to open an account, you'll hear two main terms: savings account and current account.

A **savings account** is for regular people like you and me. You deposit money, earn a small interest (usually 2.5–4% per year), and can withdraw when you need it. There are limits on how many times you can withdraw in a month, but for everyday use, it's perfect. Most students and families have savings accounts.

A **current account** is built for businesses. Shop owners, companies, and freelancers who do lots of transactions every day need an account with no withdrawal limits. The catch? Most current accounts don't earn interest. They're built for convenience, not growth.

There's also a **fixed deposit (FD)** account, which we'll cover in detail in Module 3. For now, know that it's where you lock away a lump sum for a fixed period and earn higher interest. Think of it as the bank paying you a "thank you" bonus for letting them use your money for longer.

### How Banks Make Money
Banks are businesses, and they need to make a profit. Their main trick is called the **spread** — the difference between the interest they charge borrowers and the interest they pay depositors.

Here's an example: You put ₹1,00,000 in your savings account at 3.5% interest. The bank lends ₹80,000 of that to someone as a home loan at 8.5% interest. The bank earns 8.5% on the loan but only pays you 3.5%. That 5% gap (minus their operating costs) is their profit.

This doesn't mean the bank is ripping you off. They're providing a service — keeping your money safe, giving you ATM access, online banking, and the ability to transfer money via UPI. They also take on the risk that the borrower might not repay. If someone defaults on a loan, the bank loses money, not you. Your deposit is safe.

Banks also make money from fees — ATM charges, account maintenance fees, credit card interest, and charges on services like demand drafts and foreign exchange. But lending is the big one.

## Real-World Example
In 2016, India did something dramatic — demonetisation. The government announced that ₹500 and ₹1,000 notes were no longer legal tender overnight. Millions of people rushed to banks to deposit or exchange their old notes. This event showed exactly how important banks are. People couldn't just keep cash under their mattresses — they needed banks to convert their money. Banks across India, from SBI branches in small towns to HDFC branches in Mumbai, worked overtime to handle the rush. It also pushed millions of Indians toward digital banking and UPI for the first time. Your parents probably remember standing in those long queues!

## Quiz
1. What does a bank primarily do with your deposits? — A) Locks them in a vault forever B) Lends them to borrowers to earn interest C) Sends them to the RBI D) Uses them to buy gold (Answer: B)
2. What is the role of the RBI? — A) It runs all banks in India B) It regulates and supervises banks C) It only prints currency notes D) It gives loans to citizens (Answer: B)
3. Which type of account is designed for businesses with many daily transactions? — A) Savings account B) Fixed deposit C) Current account D) Recurring deposit (Answer: C)
4. How do banks mainly earn profit? — A) By charging ATM fees only B) Through the interest rate spread between deposits and loans C) By selling insurance D) From government subsidies (Answer: B)
5. During demonetisation in 2016, what happened to old ₹500 and ₹1,000 notes? — A) They became more valuable B) They were declared no longer legal tender C) They were converted to gold D) Nothing changed (Answer: B)
