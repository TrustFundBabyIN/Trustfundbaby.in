# Module 5: The Indian Rupee Story
**Year:** 2 (Age 14)
**Month:** 5
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the history of Indian currency
- Learn how the rupee's value is determined against other currencies
- Know what foreign exchange (forex) means and why it matters
- Understand the factors that strengthen or weaken the rupee

## Key Concepts

### A Brief History of the ₹
The Indian rupee has a fascinating history stretching back centuries. The word "rupee" comes from the Sanskrit word "rupya," meaning "wrought silver." The first rupee coins were introduced by Sher Shah Suri in the 16th century — silver coins that became the standard across the subcontinent.

During British rule, the rupee went through many changes. The British East India Company and later the British Crown issued their own versions. After independence in 1947, the Reserve Bank of India took full control of currency issuance. The symbol ₹ was officially adopted in 2010 — designed by D. Udaya Kumar, an IIT postgraduate. It cleverly combines the Devanagari "र" (ra) with the Roman "R," reflecting India's dual script tradition.

The rupee has been through tough times too. In 1947, ₹1 was worth about $0.30 (about 4.16 rupees per dollar). By the early 1990s, India was nearly bankrupt — we had only enough foreign reserves to cover two weeks of imports. The government had to airlift 47 tonnes of gold to the Bank of England as collateral for a loan. This crisis triggered the landmark 1991 economic reforms under PM Narasimha Rao and Finance Minister Manmohan Singh, which opened up India's economy to the world. That crisis and those reforms shaped the India you live in today.

### Exchange Rates — How Much Is ₹1 Worth Abroad?
The **exchange rate** tells you how much one currency is worth compared to another. As of recent years, ₹1 = roughly $0.012, which means $1 = roughly ₹83. This doesn't mean the rupee is "weak" — it just means the numbers work differently.

Exchange rates are determined by supply and demand in the global foreign exchange market — the largest financial market in the world, where trillions of dollars are traded daily. When foreign investors want to invest in India (buying Indian stocks, setting up factories), they need rupees, which increases demand and strengthens the rupee. When Indians import goods (buying iPhones, crude oil), they need dollars, which increases demand for dollars and weakens the rupee.

The RBI doesn't just sit and watch. It actively manages the exchange rate by buying or selling dollars from its foreign exchange reserves (India currently holds over $600 billion in reserves). If the rupee is falling too fast, the RBI sells dollars to stabilise it. If the rupee is too strong (which hurts Indian exporters), the RBI might buy dollars to bring it down.

### What Makes the Rupee Strong or Weak?
Several factors move the rupee's value:

**Trade balance**: India imports more than it exports (especially crude oil). This constant demand for dollars puts downward pressure on the rupee. When oil prices spike, the rupee usually weakens.

**Foreign investment**: When foreign investors pour money into Indian markets, they bring in dollars, which strengthens the rupee. When they pull out (like during global crises), the rupee weakens.

**Interest rates**: Higher interest rates in India compared to the US attract foreign capital (investors chase higher returns), strengthening the rupee.

**Political stability and economic growth**: A stable, growing India attracts more investment. Uncertainty — elections, policy changes, geopolitical tensions — can cause the rupee to fluctuate.

For you as a young investor, the exchange rate matters because your Trust Fund Baby investments might include international mutual funds. If you invest in a fund that holds US stocks and the rupee weakens against the dollar, your returns in rupees go up (because each dollar is worth more rupees). Currency movements can boost or reduce your investment returns.

## Real-World Example
In 2022, when the Russia-Ukraine war started, global oil prices shot up. India, being one of the world's largest oil importers, suddenly needed many more dollars to buy the same amount of oil. This increased dollar demand and the rupee fell from about ₹75 to ₹83 per dollar within months. Your parents probably noticed petrol prices crossing ₹100 per litre. The RBI stepped in, using its massive reserves to prevent a free fall. Meanwhile, IT companies like Infosys and TCS actually benefited because they earn in dollars — when the rupee weakens, their dollar earnings convert to more rupees. It's all connected.
