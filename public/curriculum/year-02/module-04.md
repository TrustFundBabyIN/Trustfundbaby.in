# Module 4: Inflation — Why Prices Rise

## Learning Objectives

## Key Concepts

### What Is Inflation?
Inflation is the gradual increase in prices over time. Remember when a plate of vada pav cost ₹10? Now it's ₹20–25 in most cities. A movie ticket that was ₹150 a few years ago is now ₹300+ at a multiplex. That's inflation in action — the same amount of money buys you less and less each year.

But inflation isn't just about street food and movies. It affects everything — groceries, clothes, petrol, school fees, rent. When economists say inflation is at 6%, they mean that, on average, prices across the country have gone up by 6% compared to last year. So something that cost ₹100 last year now costs ₹106.

A little inflation (2–4%) is actually healthy for an economy. It means people are spending, businesses are growing, and wages are increasing. The problem starts when inflation shoots up too fast — like during the 2022 global crisis when petrol prices hit ₹100+ per litre and cooking oil prices nearly doubled. That kind of inflation hurts everyone, especially families with fixed incomes.

### Purchasing Power — Your Money's Shrinking Superpower
**Purchasing power** is what your money can actually buy. Inflation silently eats away at this power every year.

Here's a stark example. Suppose you have ₹1,00,000 today and you stuff it in your locker (earning zero interest). If inflation runs at 6% per year, after 10 years, your ₹1,00,000 will still be ₹1,00,000 — but it'll only buy what ₹55,000 buys today. You've lost almost half your purchasing power without spending a single rupee. The money didn't disappear, but its value did.

This is exactly why "save money" alone is bad advice. You need to "save AND invest" — because your investments need to grow faster than inflation to actually make you richer. If your savings account gives 3.5% interest but inflation is 6%, you're still losing 2.5% in real purchasing power every year. You need returns that beat inflation — and that's where mutual funds, stocks, and other investments come in.

### CPI — How India Measures Inflation
In India, inflation is measured using the **Consumer Price Index (CPI)**, also called the inflation rate. The CPI tracks the prices of a "basket" of everyday goods and services — rice, dal, vegetables, milk, rent, clothes, transport, and more. Government statisticians collect prices from thousands of markets across India every month.

If the CPI goes up by 5% compared to last year, inflation is 5%. The RBI uses this number to decide interest rates. If inflation is too high, the RBI raises the repo rate to cool things down. If inflation is too low (yes, that can be a problem too — it means people aren't spending), the RBI cuts rates to encourage borrowing and spending.

Different categories of goods inflate at different rates. Education and healthcare costs in India have been rising at 8–10% per year — much faster than the overall CPI. This is why parents start education funds early. Your Trust Fund Baby investment is designed to grow faster than education inflation so that when you turn 21, the money actually has more purchasing power, not less.

## Real-World Example
Think about the Indian thali — that complete meal with roti, rice, dal, sabzi, and pickle. In 2010, a home-cooked thali cost roughly ₹25–30 in ingredients. By 2024, the same thali costs ₹50–60 or more. The price doubled in about 14 years. Now here's the scary part: if wages don't double at the same pace, families have to cut back — smaller portions, fewer vegetables, skipping that extra roti. Inflation doesn't just change numbers on a screen; it changes what's on your plate. RBI tracks "thali inflation" informally because food prices are the most visible and politically sensitive part of inflation in India.

## Quiz
1. What is inflation? — A) When prices go down over time B) The gradual increase in prices over time C) When the government prints more money D) A tax on goods and services (Answer: B)
2. If inflation is 6% per year and your money earns 3% interest, what's happening to your purchasing power? — A) It's growing by 3% B) It's shrinking by 3% C) It stays the same D) It doubles every year (Answer: B)
3. What does CPI stand for? — A) Central Price Index B) Consumer Price Index C) Cost Per Item D) Consumer Purchase Indicator (Answer: B)
4. Why is a small amount of inflation (2–4%) considered healthy? — A) It makes imports cheaper B) It signals a growing economy with spending and wage growth C) It reduces the national debt D) It makes the rupee stronger internationally (Answer: B)
5. Education costs in India have been rising at approximately what rate per year? — A) 2–3% B) 4–5% C) 8–10% D) 15–20% (Answer: C)
