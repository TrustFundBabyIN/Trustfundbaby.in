## Key Concepts

### What Is the Stock Market?
The stock market is a marketplace where people buy and sell small pieces of companies. Those small pieces are called shares or stocks. But let's back up — why does this market exist in the first place?

Imagine you start a chai business in your neighbourhood. It's going well, and you want to open 10 more outlets across the city. You need ₹50 lakh but don't have that kind of money. So you decide to sell small ownership pieces of your company to the public. Each piece (share) costs ₹100. If someone buys 1,000 shares, they've invested ₹1,00,000 and now own a tiny fraction of your chai empire. That money funds your expansion.

That's essentially what happens when a company "goes public" through an IPO (Initial Public Offering). Companies like Zomato, Nykaa, and Paytm raised thousands of crores by selling shares to the public for the first time. The stock market is where those shares are then traded — bought and sold between investors — every working day.

The stock market isn't a casino (though some people treat it like one). It's a way for companies to raise money for growth and for ordinary people to own a piece of that growth. When the company does well, the value of your share goes up. When it does poorly, the value goes down. Over long periods, the overall stock market has consistently grown faster than inflation, making it one of the best wealth-building tools available.

### BSE and NSE — India's Twin Exchanges
India has two major stock exchanges:

**BSE (Bombay Stock Exchange)**: Established in 1875, it's the oldest stock exchange in Asia. Located on Dalal Street in Mumbai, BSE lists over 5,000 companies. Its flagship index is the **Sensex**, which tracks 30 of the largest and most actively traded companies on BSE. When someone says "the Sensex went up 500 points today," they're talking about this index.

**NSE (National Stock Exchange)**: Founded in 1992, NSE brought electronic trading to India and revolutionised how stocks are bought and sold. Its flagship index is the **Nifty 50**, which tracks 50 large companies. NSE handles the majority of stock trading volume in India today.

An **index** like the Sensex or Nifty 50 is like a thermometer for the stock market. It gives you a single number that represents the overall direction of the market. If the Sensex goes from 70,000 to 73,500, the market has gone up 5%. It doesn't mean every stock went up 5% — some went up more, some went down — but the overall trend was positive.

You don't actually buy the Sensex or Nifty. You buy shares of individual companies. But the index tells you whether the overall market mood is positive (bullish) or negative (bearish). When the market is "bullish," investors are optimistic, buying more, and prices tend to rise. When it's "bearish," investors are worried, selling more, and prices tend to fall.

### SEBI — The Market's Traffic Police
**SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India)** is the regulator of India's stock markets. Think of them as the traffic police of Dalal Street — they make sure everyone follows the rules.

SEBI's job includes: preventing insider trading (when company insiders use secret information to make profits), stopping market manipulation (artificially pushing stock prices up or down), ensuring companies disclose truthful financial information, and protecting small investors from fraud.

Without SEBI, the stock market would be the Wild West. Big players with insider information would always win, and small investors would get cheated. SEBI levels the playing field. They can fine companies, ban individuals from trading, and even send people to jail for market offences.

As a young investor, you'll never directly interact with SEBI, but everything you do in the stock market — from opening a demat account to buying shares — happens within the framework SEBI has created to protect you.

## Real-World Example
On March 23, 2020, when India announced its first COVID lockdown, the Sensex crashed to around 26,000 — a fall of nearly 40% from its January high of 42,000. Panic selling was everywhere. But those who stayed invested (or even invested more during the crash) saw the Sensex recover to 42,000 by November 2020 and eventually cross 70,000 by late 2023. People who panicked and sold at 26,000 locked in their losses. People who held on or invested more during the panic saw their wealth more than double. This is why understanding the stock market matters — it helps you make calm, informed decisions instead of emotional ones.
