## Key Concepts

### Fixed Deposits — Lock It and Earn More
A Fixed Deposit (FD) is like making a deal with your bank: "I'll give you this money for a fixed period, and you pay me a higher interest rate than my savings account." It's one of the simplest and safest investment options in India.

Here's how it works. You walk into your bank (or do it online through your banking app) and say, "I want to deposit ₹50,000 for 1 year." The bank gives you an interest rate — say, 7% per year. For that entire year, you can't touch that ₹50,000 (well, you can, but you'll pay a penalty and lose some interest). At the end of the year, you get back your ₹50,000 plus ₹3,500 in interest = ₹53,500.

The key features of an FD: the interest rate is fixed when you start (it won't change even if the bank lowers rates later), the tenure can range from 7 days to 10 years, and your money is safe — FDs in India are covered by deposit insurance up to ₹5 lakh per bank by the DICGC (Deposit Insurance and Credit Guarantee Corporation). Even if the bank somehow goes bankrupt, the government protects your money up to ₹5 lakh.

FD interest rates are always higher than savings account rates. While a savings account might give you 2.5–4%, a 1-year FD could give you 6–7.5%. The trade-off is liquidity — you can't withdraw anytime without penalty.

### Recurring Deposits — Save a Little Every Month
A Recurring Deposit (RD) is like an FD's flexible cousin. Instead of depositing a lump sum, you commit to depositing a fixed amount every month for a set period. It's perfect for people who don't have a large amount sitting around but can save a small amount regularly.

Say you open an RD for ₹2,000 per month for 2 years at 7% interest. Every month, ₹2,000 gets deducted from your savings account automatically. After 2 years (24 deposits of ₹2,000 = ₹48,000 invested), you'll receive approximately ₹51,500 — that's ₹3,500 in interest. Not bad for a habit of saving ₹2,000 a month.

RDs are fantastic for building the savings habit. Since the amount is fixed and deducted automatically, you don't need willpower — the system does it for you. This is very similar to an SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) in mutual funds, which you learned about in Year 1. The difference? RDs give you guaranteed, fixed returns. SIPs invest in the market, so returns can be higher or lower — they're not guaranteed.

### FD vs RD vs Savings Account — When to Use What
Think of it as a spectrum of flexibility versus returns:

**Savings Account** — Most flexible. Withdraw anytime. Lowest interest (2.5–4%). Use for your everyday money — the cash you need for this month's expenses.

**Recurring Deposit** — Moderately flexible. You commit to monthly deposits. Better interest than savings (6–7.5%). Use when you can save a fixed amount every month but don't have a lump sum yet.

**Fixed Deposit** — Least flexible (money locked). Best interest among the three (6.5–7.5%). Use when you have a lump sum you won't need for a while.

A smart money strategy uses all three: savings for daily needs, RD for monthly saving goals, and FD for long-term lump sums. This layered approach ensures you always have access to some money while the rest earns higher returns.

## Real-World Example
In India, Post Office RDs are incredibly popular, especially in smaller towns and villages. The India Post recurring deposit scheme offers 6.7% interest (rates vary), and you can start with just ₹100 per month. Millions of Indian families — from farmers in Punjab to shopkeepers in Tamil Nadu — use Post Office RDs to save for their children's education or future needs. It's one of the most accessible savings tools in the country because you don't need a smartphone or internet — just walk to your nearest post office. Some families have been using Post Office RDs for generations.

## Quiz
1. What is the main advantage of a Fixed Deposit over a savings account? — A) You can withdraw anytime B) Higher interest rate C) No minimum deposit D) Free debit card (Answer: B)
2. In a Recurring Deposit, how do you make deposits? — A) One lump sum at the start B) A fixed amount every month C) Whatever amount you want, whenever D) Only at the end of the term (Answer: B)
3. What does DICGC protect? — A) Stock market investments B) Bank deposits up to ₹5 lakh C) Mutual fund returns D) Credit card fraud losses (Answer: B)
4. If you deposit ₹1,00,000 in a 1-year FD at 7% interest, how much will you receive at maturity? — A) ₹1,00,000 B) ₹1,07,000 C) ₹1,14,000 D) ₹1,00,700 (Answer: B)
5. Which savings option is most similar to an SIP in mutual funds? — A) Fixed Deposit B) Savings Account C) Recurring Deposit D) Current Account (Answer: C)
