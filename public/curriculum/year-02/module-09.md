# Module 9: What Are Shares?
**Year:** 2 (Age 14)
**Month:** 9
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what shares represent — ownership in a company
- Learn the difference between common shares and preferred shares
- Know how companies issue shares and why
- Understand what dividends are and how they work

## Key Concepts

### Shares = Ownership
When you buy a share of a company, you're buying a tiny piece of that company. You become a part-owner — a shareholder. If Infosys has 420 crore shares outstanding and you own 100 shares, you own approximately 0.0000024% of Infosys. That sounds tiny, but it means something real: you're entitled to a share of the company's profits, you can vote on major company decisions at annual meetings, and the value of your ownership grows if the company does well.

Think of it like this: imagine a pizza shop called "Pizza Palace" is worth ₹10,00,000. The owner decides to sell 1,000 shares at ₹100 each. If you buy 10 shares for ₹1,000, you own 1% of Pizza Palace. If the shop does well and its total value doubles to ₹20,00,000, your 10 shares are now worth ₹2,000. You didn't do any pizza-making — but your ownership stake grew because the company grew.

The key insight is that when you invest in shares, your wealth grows (or shrinks) based on how well the company performs. This is different from a fixed deposit, where your returns are fixed regardless of what happens in the economy. Shares carry more risk but historically deliver higher returns over long periods.

### How Shares Are Created and Traded
Companies issue shares when they need money. The first time they do this is called an **IPO (Initial Public Offering)**. Before the IPO, the company is privately owned — maybe by the founders, their family, and a few early investors. The IPO opens ownership to the public.

After the IPO, shares are listed on the stock exchange (BSE or NSE). From that point on, shares are traded between investors — not between the investor and the company. When you buy 10 shares of Reliance on the NSE, you're buying them from another investor who wants to sell, not from Reliance directly. The company doesn't get that money — the selling investor does. The company already got its money during the IPO (or later share issues).

This is an important distinction. The stock market is a secondary market — a marketplace for buying and selling existing shares. The company only raises money during its IPO or when it issues new shares later. All the daily trading on BSE and NSE is between investors.

To buy shares, you need a **demat account** (to hold shares electronically) and a **trading account** (to execute buy/sell orders). In India, these are opened through brokers like Zerodha, Groww, Upstox, or traditional brokers like ICICI Direct. You also need a PAN card. Since you're 14, your parents can open a demat account in your name (as a minor account), and it'll be converted to a regular account when you turn 18.

### Dividends — Your Share of the Profits
When a company makes profits, it has two choices: reinvest the money into the business (for expansion, R&D, paying off debt) or distribute some of it to shareholders as **dividends**.

A dividend is a cash payment per share. If TCS declares a dividend of ₹30 per share and you own 50 shares, you receive ₹1,500 directly in your bank account. Companies that pay regular dividends — like ITC, Infosys, HDFC Bank, and many PSUs (Public Sector Undertakings) — are popular with investors who want steady income.

Not all companies pay dividends. Fast-growing companies like Zomato or Nykaa often reinvest all their profits because they believe the growth will create more value for shareholders than a small dividend would. Whether a company pays dividends or reinvests depends on its stage of growth and strategy.

Dividends are nice, but they shouldn't be your primary reason for investing in shares. The main way you make money from shares is through **capital appreciation** — the share price going up over time. A company that grows its earnings consistently will see its share price rise, and your investment grows along with it.

## Real-World Example
Let's look at Reliance Industries. In 2002, one share of Reliance cost about ₹300. If you had bought 100 shares then for ₹30,000, today those shares would be worth over ₹2,50,000 (with the price around ₹2,500+). That's over 8x growth — and that's before counting all the dividends Reliance paid over those 20+ years. Meanwhile, Reliance grew from a petrochemicals company into India's largest conglomerate — launching Reliance Jio (which transformed Indian telecom), Reliance Retail, and more. Owning shares meant you participated in that entire growth story.

## Quiz
1. When you buy a share of a company, what do you become? — A) A creditor B) A part-owner (shareholder) C) An employee D) A customer (Answer: B)
2. What is an IPO? — A) Indian Purchase Order B) Initial Public Offering — the first time a company sells shares to the public C) International Price Option D) Internal Profit Organisation (Answer: B)
3. After an IPO, when shares are traded on the exchange, who receives the money? — A) The company B) The selling investor C) SEBI D) The stock exchange (Answer: B)
4. What is a dividend? — A) A loan from the company B) A share of the company's profits paid to shareholders C) A fee for owning shares D) A tax on stock market gains (Answer: B)
5. What do you need to buy shares in India? — A) Only cash B) A demat account and trading account C) A credit card D) A company's permission (Answer: B)
