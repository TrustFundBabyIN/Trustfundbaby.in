## Key Concepts

### What Is Interest?
Interest is the price of borrowing money — or the reward for lending it. Think of it as rent for money. When you deposit money in a bank, you're lending it to the bank, so they pay you interest. When you take a loan from the bank, you're borrowing their money, so you pay them interest.

The interest rate is expressed as a percentage per year. If your savings account gives you 3.5% interest per year and you have ₹10,000 in it, you'll earn ₹350 in interest after one year (assuming it's simple interest). Not bad for doing nothing — your money earns while you sleep.

Interest rates matter because they decide how expensive it is to borrow money and how rewarding it is to save. When rates are high, people are encouraged to save more (because they earn more) and borrow less (because loans cost more). When rates are low, borrowing is cheap, so people buy more things on credit. The RBI adjusts rates to keep the economy balanced.

### Simple Interest vs Compound Interest
**Simple interest** is straightforward. You earn interest only on your original deposit. If you put ₹10,000 at 5% simple interest for 3 years, you earn ₹500 each year — that's ₹1,500 total. Your money after 3 years: ₹11,500. The formula is simple: Interest = Principal × Rate × Time.

**Compound interest** is where the magic happens. Instead of earning interest only on your original amount, you earn interest on your interest too. Same ₹10,000 at 5% compounded annually: Year 1, you earn ₹500 (total: ₹10,500). Year 2, you earn 5% on ₹10,500 = ₹525 (total: ₹11,025). Year 3, you earn 5% on ₹11,025 = ₹551.25 (total: ₹11,576.25). You earned ₹76.25 more than simple interest — and that's just over 3 years. Over 20 or 30 years, compound interest creates enormous differences.

Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world." Whether he actually said it or not, the math backs it up. This is exactly why starting your Trust Fund Baby investment early is so powerful — more years of compounding means dramatically more money at the end.

### Repo Rate — The RBI's Remote Control
The **repo rate** is the interest rate at which the RBI lends money to commercial banks. It's the master switch that controls all other interest rates in the economy.

When the RBI increases the repo rate, banks have to pay more to borrow from the RBI. To cover this cost, they raise interest rates on loans (making home loans, car loans, and personal loans more expensive) and on deposits (giving you more interest on your savings). When the RBI cuts the repo rate, the opposite happens — loans get cheaper, but your savings earn less.

As of recent years, the RBI's repo rate has hovered around 6.5%. This number might seem small, but it ripples through every financial product in India — from your savings account interest to the EMI on your parents' home loan. Understanding the repo rate helps you understand why your FD interest rate went up or why your parents suddenly got a better deal on a loan.

## Real-World Example
Let's say you invest ₹10,000 today in a mutual fund that gives 12% annual returns (compounded). If you're 14 now and leave it untouched until you're 21 (7 years), here's what happens: Year 1: ₹11,200. Year 2: ₹12,544. Year 3: ₹14,049. Year 4: ₹15,735. Year 5: ₹17,623. Year 6: ₹19,738. Year 7: ₹22,107. Your ₹10,000 became ₹22,107 — more than double — without you doing anything. That's compound interest working for you. Now imagine if you added even ₹1,000 every month. The numbers get wild. This is the exact principle behind your Trust Fund Baby SIP.

## Quiz
1. What is interest best described as? — A) A tax on your salary B) The price of borrowing money C) A bank penalty D) A government fee (Answer: B)
2. You invest ₹5,000 at 10% simple interest for 2 years. How much interest do you earn? — A) ₹500 B) ₹1,000 C) ₹1,500 D) ₹2,000 (Answer: B)
3. What makes compound interest different from simple interest? — A) It's calculated monthly instead of yearly B) You earn interest on your interest C) It only applies to loans D) The bank charges extra for it (Answer: B)
4. What happens when the RBI increases the repo rate? — A) Loans become cheaper B) Savings accounts earn less C) Loan interest rates tend to increase D) Nothing changes for consumers (Answer: C)
5. If you invest ₹10,000 at 10% compound interest annually, how much will you have after 2 years? — A) ₹12,000 B) ₹12,100 C) ₹11,000 D) ₹12,500 (Answer: B)
