# Module 10: How Stock Prices Change
**Year:** 2 (Age 14)
**Month:** 10
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand the supply and demand dynamics that move stock prices
- Learn what causes stocks to go up or down in the short and long term
- Know the difference between market sentiment and company fundamentals
- Understand why stock prices fluctuate daily and why that's normal

## Key Concepts

### The Push and Pull of Supply and Demand
At its simplest, a stock price moves for one reason: more people want to buy it than sell it (price goes up), or more people want to sell it than buy it (price goes down). It's basic supply and demand, just like vegetables at a mandi.

When a company announces great quarterly profits, more investors want to own that company. Demand for its shares increases. But there are only so many shares available. Buyers start offering higher prices to convince sellers to part with their shares. The price rises. Conversely, if a company reports bad news — say, a regulatory investigation — shareholders get nervous and want to sell. Supply of shares on the market increases while buyers step back. The price falls.

This push and pull happens millions of times per day across thousands of stocks. Every single trade — every buy and sell order — contributes to setting the price. It's a continuous auction that runs from 9:15 AM to 3:30 PM on weekdays, with buyers and sellers constantly negotiating through the exchange's electronic system.

### What Moves Stock Prices?
Stock prices respond to two broad categories of factors:

**Company-specific factors (fundamentals)**: Quarterly earnings reports (did the company make more or less profit than expected?), new product launches, management changes, winning or losing a major contract, regulatory approvals or penalties, and expansion plans. If HDFC Bank reports a 20% jump in profits, its share price will likely rise. If a pharmaceutical company's drug fails an FDA trial, its price will likely fall.

**Market-wide factors (macro)**: RBI interest rate changes, inflation data, GDP growth numbers, global events (wars, oil price changes, US Federal Reserve decisions), government budget announcements, and elections. When COVID hit in March 2020, almost every stock crashed — not because every company had a problem, but because the entire market panicked. Similarly, when the government announces a policy favourable to a sector (say, EV subsidies), all electric vehicle-related stocks tend to rise together.

**Sentiment**: Sometimes prices move simply because of emotions. Fear and greed drive markets as much as fundamentals. During a bull run, people buy because everyone else is buying (FOMO — Fear Of Missing Out). During a crash, people sell because everyone else is selling (panic). Understanding sentiment helps you avoid making emotional decisions.

### Short-Term Noise vs Long-Term Growth
Here's the most important thing to understand about stock prices: **in the short term, the market is a voting machine. In the long term, it's a weighing machine.**

In the short term (days, weeks, months), stock prices bounce around based on news, rumours, quarterly results, and market mood. This is noise. It's unpredictable and mostly meaningless for long-term investors.

In the long term (years, decades), stock prices reflect a company's actual earnings growth. A company that consistently grows its revenue and profits will see its share price rise over time, regardless of short-term ups and downs. This is signal.

The Sensex was at about 100 points when it was created in 1986. Today, it's above 70,000. That's not because of short-term noise — it's because India's economy and its largest companies have grown enormously over four decades. Individual stocks can go to zero (if a company goes bankrupt), but the overall market has always trended upward over long periods.

This is why long-term investing beats short-term trading for most people. You don't need to predict what will happen tomorrow. You need to invest in good companies (or good mutual funds) and give them time. Time in the market beats timing the market.

## Real-World Example
Take Infosys, one of India's IT giants. In January 2008 (before the global financial crisis), Infosys was trading at about ₹1,800 per share. By October 2008, it had crashed to about ₹900 — a 50% drop. Panic sellers lost half their money. But if you held on (or better, bought more at ₹900), Infosys recovered. By 2024, it was trading at around ₹1,500–1,800 (adjusted for bonuses and splits, much higher in effective value). The short-term crash looked terrifying. The long-term trend rewarded patience. The lesson: crashes are temporary for good companies. Selling in panic is how you turn a temporary loss into a permanent one.

## Quiz
1. What is the basic reason a stock price rises? — A) The company's building gets bigger B) More people want to buy than sell C) The government increases its value D) The CEO orders a price increase (Answer: B)
2. Which of these is a company-specific factor that affects stock price? — A) A war breaking out in another country B) The company's quarterly earnings report C) Global oil prices D) The general election date (Answer: B)
3. What does "FOMO" stand for? — A) Fear Of Missing Out B) First Order Market Operation C) Financial Objectives Management Office D) Free Online Market Option (Answer: A)
4. What does the quote "In the short term, the market is a voting machine. In the long term, it's a weighing machine" mean? — A) Short-term prices reflect emotions; long-term prices reflect actual company value B) Markets only work in democracies C) You should vote on which stocks to buy D) Weight of investments matters more than votes (Answer: A)
5. What was the approximate level of the Sensex when it was created in 1986? — A) 1,000 B) 100 C) 10,000 D) 500 (Answer: B)
