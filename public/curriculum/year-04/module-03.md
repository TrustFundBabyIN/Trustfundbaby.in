# Module 3: Reading NAV & Calculating Returns
**Year:** 4 (Age 16)
**Month:** 3
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what NAV (Net Asset Value) means and how it's calculated
- Learn to calculate absolute returns on your mutual fund investments
- Understand the difference between absolute return and annualized return (CAGR)
- Apply these concepts to your own trust fund portfolio

## Key Concepts

### What Is NAV?
NAV stands for Net Asset Value. It's the price of one unit of a mutual fund. Think of it like the price per share of a company, but for mutual funds. Every mutual fund has a NAV that is calculated at the end of each trading day. If a fund's NAV is ₹50, that means one unit of that fund costs ₹50.

NAV is calculated by taking the total value of all the investments the fund holds, subtracting its expenses, and dividing by the total number of units investors hold. It sounds complicated, but the formula is simple: NAV = (Total Assets - Total Liabilities) ÷ Total Number of Units.

When you invest ₹5,000 in a fund with a NAV of ₹50, you get 100 units (₹5,000 ÷ ₹50 = 100 units). If the NAV rises to ₹55 later, your 100 units are now worth ₹5,500. That's a ₹500 gain — just from the NAV going up.

### Absolute Return vs CAGR
There are two ways to measure how well your investment is doing. **Absolute return** is the simplest — it just tells you the total percentage change from your purchase price to the current price. If you bought at ₹50 and it's now ₹55, your absolute return is 10%.

But absolute return doesn't tell you how long it took to get that return. That's where **CAGR (Compound Annual Growth Rate)** comes in. CAGR tells you the average annual return, smoothed out over time. A 30% absolute return over 3 years sounds great, but the CAGR is only about 9.1% per year. CAGR is more useful when comparing investments over different time periods.

### Why NAV Changes Every Day
A mutual fund invests in stocks (equity funds) or bonds (debt funds) or both. The prices of these stocks and bonds change every day based on market conditions. So the total value of the fund's portfolio changes every day, which means the NAV changes too. On good market days, NAV goes up. On bad days, it goes down. This is completely normal — it's like a roller coaster. What matters is the long-term trend, not daily ups and downs.

### How to Calculate Your Returns
Here's the simple formula: Return (%) = ((Current Value - Invested Amount) ÷ Invested Amount) × 100. For example, if you invested ₹20,000 and it's now worth ₹22,500, your return is ((22,500 - 20,000) ÷ 20,000) × 100 = 12.5%. That's exactly what happened with your HDFC Mid-Cap fund.

For SIPs, calculating returns is trickier because you invest different amounts at different times (each month's SIP buys at a different NAV). This is where tools like XIRR (Extended Internal Rate of Return) come in handy — they calculate your actual return considering the timing of each investment.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Let's apply this to your Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund, which is your best performer. Your trust invested ₹20,000 across multiple SIPs, and the current value is ₹23,100. The absolute return is ((23,100 - 20,000) ÷ 20,000) × 100 = 15.5%.

Now compare this with the ICICI Prudential Corporate Bond Fund. Invested ₹10,000, current value ₹10,800, return = 8.0%. Why the difference? The Parag Parikh fund invests in stocks (equity), which are riskier but can grow faster. The Corporate Bond fund invests in bonds (debt), which are safer but grow slower.

Your total portfolio tells the bigger story: ₹65,000 invested → ₹73,200 current value → 12.6% overall return. That ₹8,200 gain is real money that your trust earned by being invested wisely.
