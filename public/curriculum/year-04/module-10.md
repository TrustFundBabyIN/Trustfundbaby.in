# Module 10: Understanding Fund Switches
**Year:** 4 (Age 16)
**Month:** 10
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what a fund switch is and when it makes sense
- Learn the process of switching from one mutual fund to another
- Know the costs and tax implications of switching
- Understand when NOT to switch funds

## Key Concepts

### What Is a Fund Switch?
A fund switch means moving your money from one mutual fund to another within the same fund house (AMC). It's like transferring your savings from one type of account to another — but instead of going through your bank, you go through the mutual fund company.

For example, if your trust has money in HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund and the trustee decides to move it to HDFC Large Cap Fund, that's a switch. Technically, what happens is: your units in the first fund are redeemed (sold), and the money is immediately used to buy units in the second fund. Both transactions happen at the same NAV.

### When Does Switching Make Sense?
There are legitimate reasons to switch funds:

**Underperformance:** If a fund has been consistently underperforming its benchmark and peers for 2-3 years, it might be time to switch to a better fund.

**Change in risk profile:** As you get closer to your goal (turning 21), you might want to shift from aggressive equity funds to safer debt funds. This is called "de-risking."

**Fund manager change:** If the fund manager who was responsible for the fund's good performance leaves, the fund's future performance might be affected.

**Better opportunity:** If a new fund with a better strategy or track record becomes available, switching might make sense.

### The Costs of Switching
Switching isn't free. There are costs to consider:

**Exit Load:** Most funds charge an exit load if you sell (or switch out) within a certain period — usually 1% if you exit within 1 year. So if you switch ₹22,500 from HDFC Mid-Cap within 1 year, you'd pay ₹225 as exit load.

**Tax Implications:** When you switch, you're technically selling the old fund. This triggers a capital gains event. If you've held the fund for more than 1 year (for equity), the gain is considered long-term. LTCG up to ₹1 lakh per year is tax-free, but gains above that are taxed at 10%.

**Transaction Time:** Switches take 2-3 business days to complete. During this time, your money is "in transit" — it's not earning returns in either fund.

### When NOT to Switch
Don't switch just because the market dropped this month. Don't switch because your friend's fund did better last quarter. Don't switch because you saw an ad for a "hot new fund." Emotional switching is one of the biggest mistakes investors make. Studies show that investors who frequently switch funds actually earn LESS than those who stay invested.

The best strategy? Review your funds once a year. If they're still performing reasonably well (within 2-3% of their benchmark), stay put. Only switch if there's a clear, rational reason.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Let's say your trustee, Mr. Rajesh Mehra, reviews the portfolio in December 2025 and notices that the SBI Small Cap Fund (+12.0%) is slightly underperforming compared to a new small-cap fund from Axis that returned +18% this year. Should he switch?

Before switching, he should consider:
1. **Exit load:** If the SBI fund was purchased via SIP over 11 months, some units are less than 1 year old and would attract a 1% exit load.
2. **Tax:** The gain on SBI Small Cap is ₹1,800. Since this is within the ₹1 lakh LTCG exemption, no tax.
3. **Track record:** One year of underperformance isn't enough. SBI Small Cap might have a better 5-year track record.
4. **Fund manager stability:** Who manages the Axis fund? How long have they been there?

In most cases, the wise decision would be to stay put — especially with only 11 months of data. Your trust's current portfolio is well-diversified and performing well overall at +12.6%.

## Quiz
1. What is a fund switch? — A) Withdrawing money from a fund B) Moving money from one fund to another within the same AMC C) Buying more units of the same fund D) Changing your bank account (Answer: B)
2. What is an exit load? — A) A fee charged when you sell or switch out of a fund within a specified period B) A fee for opening a new fund C) Tax on your investment D) A penalty for late SIP payment (Answer: A)
3. If you switch ₹22,500 from a fund with a 1% exit load (held < 1 year), how much do you pay? — A) ₹0 B) ₹125 C) ₹225 D) ₹1,000 (Answer: C)
4. What is "de-risking" in the context of fund switching? — A) Taking more risk B) Moving from aggressive funds to safer funds as you approach your goal C) Closing your account D) Investing in cryptocurrency (Answer: B)
5. How often should you review your funds before considering a switch? — A) Daily B) Weekly C) Once a year D) Every 5 years (Answer: C)
