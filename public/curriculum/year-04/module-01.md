# Module 1: Understanding Your Trust Structure
**Year:** 4 (Age 16)
**Month:** 1
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what a trust is and why your parents created one for you
- Identify the key parts of your trust — name, trustee, bank account, and beneficiary
- Learn the difference between a trust and a regular savings account
- Know when and how you will receive your trust fund

## Key Concepts

### What Is a Trust?
A trust is like a locked money box that someone creates for you. Your parents put money into it, but they can't take it back — it's irrevocable, meaning once it's set up, it belongs to the future (that's you). Think of it this way: your parents decided to save money for your future, but instead of keeping it in a regular savings account where it might get spent on random things, they put it in a special legal structure. This structure has rules. The money can only be used for investing, and when you turn 21, it comes to you.

In India, trusts are governed by the Indian Trusts Act, 1882. There are two main types — private trusts and public trusts. Your trust is a private trust, meaning it's created for a specific person (you) by specific people (your parents). It's not open to the public like a charity trust.

### The Key Players: Trustee and Beneficiary
Every trust has at least two important roles. The **trustee** is the person who manages the trust — they make decisions about investing, handle the paperwork, and make sure everything runs by the rules. In most cases for Trust Fund Baby trusts, the trustee is a parent. The **beneficiary** is the person who eventually receives the money — that's you.

The trustee doesn't own the money. They're more like a manager. They have to act in your best interest, not their own. If they misuse the trust money, that's a serious legal problem. The law protects you as the beneficiary.

### Why a Trust and Not Just a Bank Account?
Your parents could have simply opened a fixed deposit or a savings account in your name. But a trust is more powerful for a few reasons. First, it's **irrevocable** — nobody can casually dip into it. Second, it allows **investment in mutual funds** through SIPs, which means the money can grow much faster than a savings account (which gives maybe 3-4% interest). Third, it has a **legal structure** that protects the money until you're mature enough to handle it. A bank account in a minor's name can be operated by the parent anytime — a trust has stricter rules.

### The Timeline: From Creation to Maturity
Your trust has a clear timeline. It gets created (usually when you're a child), money is invested through monthly SIPs for several years, and when you turn 21, the trust is dissolved and the money is transferred to you. For Trust Fund Baby trusts, this typically means 5-8 years of active investing. During this time, your money is growing through mutual fund investments — not sitting idle in a bank account.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Your trust is called the **Kabir Mehra Growth Trust**. It was created in January 2025, when you were 13 years old. Your father, Mr. Rajesh Mehra, is the trustee. The trust has its own PAN card (AACTK1234D) and its own bank account — an HDFC Trust Current Account. Every month, ₹5,000 goes into the trust via SIP (Systematic Investment Plan).

As of December 2025, your trust has ₹73,200. That's ₹65,000 that was actually invested, plus ₹8,200 in gains. The trust will continue running until you turn 21, which means there are still about 5 more years of investing ahead. That's a lot of time for your money to grow.
