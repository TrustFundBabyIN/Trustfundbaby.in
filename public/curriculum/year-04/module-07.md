# Module 7: The Role of Trustees
**Year:** 4 (Age 16)
**Month:** 7
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what a trustee does and their legal responsibilities
- Learn about trustee duties, powers, and limitations
- Know what happens if a trustee fails in their duties
- Understand the process of appointing a new trustee if needed

## Key Concepts

### Who Is a Trustee?
A trustee is the person appointed to manage the trust's affairs. They're like the captain of a ship — they don't own the ship, but they're responsible for steering it safely. In most Trust Fund Baby trusts, the trustee is a parent (usually the father or mother). Your trustee is Mr. Rajesh Mehra, your father.

Being a trustee is a legal responsibility, not just a title. The trustee must act with care, diligence, and loyalty. They must put the beneficiary's interests above their own. In legal terms, this is called "fiduciary duty" — the highest standard of care in law.

### Trustee Duties
A trustee has several key duties:

**Duty of Care:** The trustee must manage the trust's investments prudently. This means choosing reasonable investments (like diversified mutual funds), not gambling with the money on risky bets.

**Duty of Loyalty:** The trustee must always act in the beneficiary's best interest, not their own. They can't use trust money for personal benefit.

**Duty to Maintain Records:** The trustee must keep proper accounts of all trust transactions. Every SIP payment, every statement, every communication with fund houses — it must be documented.

**Duty to Invest:** The trustee can't just let the money sit idle. They must invest it according to the trust deed's instructions. In your case, this means investing in mutual funds via SIP.

**Duty to Distribute:** When the trust matures (when you turn 21), the trustee must distribute the funds to you as the beneficiary.

### Trustee Powers
Trustees also have powers — they can choose which mutual funds to invest in (within the trust deed's guidelines), they can switch between funds if one is underperforming, and they can decide the SIP amount (subject to the trust deed). They can also appoint professionals (like a financial advisor) to help manage the trust.

However, trustees cannot sell trust assets for personal use, change the beneficiary, or alter the trust's fundamental terms.

### What If a Trustee Can't Continue?
Life is unpredictable. What if your trustee (your father) can no longer manage the trust — due to health issues, relocation, or other reasons? The trust deed usually provides for appointing a successor trustee. This could be your mother, another family member, or a professional trustee (like a bank's trust department).

The process typically requires a written document, and in some cases, approval from the beneficiary (if they're old enough). The outgoing trustee must hand over all records, documents, and account access to the new trustee.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Your father, Mr. Rajesh Mehra, has been managing the Kabir Mehra Growth Trust since January 2025. In the past 11 months, he has:

- Set up the HDFC Trust Current Account
- Completed KYC for the trust (getting PAN AACTK1234D)
- Chosen four mutual funds across equity and debt categories
- Ensured ₹5,000 goes out every month as SIP
- Maintained records of all 11 SIP transactions

He's done all this while managing his own job and family responsibilities. That's what a good trustee does — they take on extra responsibility because they care about your future.

Your father made smart choices too — he diversified across four funds (mid-cap, small-cap, flexi cap, and corporate bond), balancing growth potential with safety. That ₹8,200 gain is partly because of his investment decisions.
