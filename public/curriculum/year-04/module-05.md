# Module 5: Understanding Your Account Statement
**Year:** 4 (Age 16)
**Month:** 5
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what a Consolidated Account Statement (CAS) is
- Learn to read every section of your trust's account statement
- Know the difference between a mutual fund statement and a bank account statement
- Be able to spot errors or unusual activity in your trust's statements

## Key Concepts

### What Is an Account Statement?
An account statement is a detailed record of all transactions in your trust's mutual fund account over a specific period. Think of it as a bank passbook, but for investments. Every time money goes in (SIP payment), every time units are bought or sold, every dividend received — it's all recorded here.

For mutual funds in India, you get two types of statements. The **Transaction Statement** shows individual buy/sell/dividend transactions. The **Holding Statement** shows what you currently own. The **Consolidated Account Statement (CAS)** combines everything across all your mutual fund investments into one document.

### The Key Sections Explained
Your account statement has these main sections:

**Investor Details:** This shows the trust's name, PAN, and KYC status. For your trust, it would show "Kabir Mehra Growth Trust," PAN "AACTK1234D," and KYC status as "Verified."

**Folio Details:** Each fund house assigns a folio number. If your trust invests in HDFC, SBI, and Parag Parikh funds, you'll have three different folio numbers — one from each AMC.

**Transaction History:** This is a chronological list of every transaction. It shows the date, type (Purchase/Redemption/Dividend), amount, NAV at the time of purchase, and units allotted. For SIPs, you'll see a monthly entry showing ₹5,000 being invested and the corresponding units received.

**Current Holdings:** This shows what you currently own — each fund, number of units, current NAV, and current market value. This is the snapshot of your portfolio right now.

**Capital Gains:** This section shows how much profit or loss you'd realize if you sold everything today. It's divided into short-term gains (held less than 1 year for equity, less than 3 years for debt) and long-term gains.

### Why You Should Review It Monthly
Reviewing your statement monthly takes 5 minutes but gives you enormous insight. You can spot if a SIP didn't go through (sometimes bank mandates fail), if there's a fund you didn't authorize, or if returns are unusually low. It's like checking your phone's battery — quick, easy, and prevents surprises.

### How to Read a Transaction Line
Here's how to read one line of a transaction: "15-Mar-2025 | Purchase (SIP) | ₹5,000 | NAV: ₹48.25 | Units: 103.63." This means on March 15, 2025, a SIP of ₹5,000 was processed. The NAV on that day was ₹48.25 per unit, so ₹5,000 ÷ ₹48.25 = 103.63 units were purchased. Each month, you're buying units at whatever the NAV is that day — this is called rupee cost averaging.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
Let's trace one month of your trust's HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund. On 15th March 2025, your SIP of ₹5,000 was debited from the HDFC Trust Current Account. The fund house received it and allotted units at that day's NAV of ₹48.25. You got 103.63 units. By December 2025, the NAV had risen to ₹54.28. Your 103.63 units are now worth ₹5,624. That's ₹624 gain on one month's SIP alone.

Now multiply this across 11 SIPs across 4 funds, and you get the full picture: ₹65,000 invested, ₹73,200 current value. Each SIP entry in your statement tells a piece of this growth story.

Your trust's bank statement from HDFC would show ₹5,000 debited every month — a simple, clean record of money flowing out to fund your investments.

## Quiz
1. What does CAS stand for? — A) Common Account Summary B) Consolidated Account Statement C) Combined Asset Sheet D) Central Accounting System (Answer: B)
2. How many folio numbers does Kabir's trust have if it invests in funds from 3 different AMCs? — A) 1 B) 2 C) 3 D) 4 (Answer: C)
3. If NAV is ₹48.25 and ₹5,000 is invested via SIP, how many units are allotted? — A) 98.45 B) 100.00 C) 103.63 D) 110.25 (Answer: C)
4. What is rupee cost averaging? — A) Buying units at the same NAV every month B) Buying more units when NAV is low and fewer when NAV is high, by investing a fixed amount monthly C) Averaging bank interest rates D) Calculating average returns (Answer: B)
5. Which section of your account statement shows what you currently own? — A) Transaction History B) Investor Details C) Current Holdings D) Capital Gains (Answer: C)
