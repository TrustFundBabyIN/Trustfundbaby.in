## Key Concepts

### The Indian Trusts Act, 1882
Trusts in India are governed by the Indian Trusts Act, 1882. This is a 140+ year old law, but it still forms the foundation of how trusts work. The Act defines what a trust is, who can create one, who can be a trustee, and what the trustee's duties are.

Under this Act, a trust is created when a person (the author) transfers property to another person (the trustee) for the benefit of a third person (the beneficiary). The trust must have a lawful purpose, and the beneficiary must be clearly identified.

For Trust Fund Baby trusts, the parents are the authors, one parent is usually the trustee, and the child is the beneficiary. The purpose is clear: invest in mutual funds for the child's financial future.

### The Trust Deed: Your Trust's Constitution
The trust deed is the legal document that creates the trust. Think of it as the constitution of your trust — it lays out all the rules. A typical trust deed includes:

- **Name of the trust** (Kabir Mehra Growth Trust)
- **Names of author(s) and trustee(s)** (your parents, with your father as trustee)
- **Name of the beneficiary** (you)
- **Purpose of the trust** (investing for the beneficiary's future)
- **Trust property** (what assets the trust holds)
- **Powers and duties of the trustee** (what the trustee can and cannot do)
- **Duration and termination** (when the trust ends — at your age 21)
- **Distribution rules** (how the money is given to you at maturity)

### Tax Rules for Trusts
Trusts in India are taxed differently from individuals. The income earned by a trust (like capital gains from mutual fund investments) is generally taxed at the maximum marginal rate unless specific conditions are met. However, if the trust is created by parents for a minor child, the income may be clubbed with the parent's income for tax purposes.

For equity mutual funds held for more than 1 year, long-term capital gains (LTCG) up to ₹1 lakh per year are tax-free. Gains above ₹1 lakh are taxed at 10%. For debt funds, the rules are different — gains are taxed based on your income tax slab.

Your trust's PAN (AACTK1234D) is used for all tax-related transactions. TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) may apply on certain transactions, and the trust must file an annual income tax return.

### SEBI Regulations for Mutual Funds
SEBI (Securities and Exchange Board of India) regulates mutual funds in India. SEBI rules protect investors like your trust by:

- Requiring mutual funds to disclose their portfolio every month
- Capping expense ratios (the fees fund houses charge)
- Mandating diversification rules (a fund can't put all its money in one stock)
- Requiring KYC (Know Your Customer) verification for all investors
- Ensuring fair NAV calculation practices

These rules mean your trust's investments are well-regulated and transparent. You can always see exactly where your money is invested.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)
The Kabir Mehra Growth Trust operates under all these rules. The trust deed specifies that Mr. Rajesh Mehra is the trustee, the purpose is mutual fund investment, and the trust matures when Kabir turns 21.

The trust's investments follow SEBI regulations — the HDFC Mid-Cap Opportunities Fund, for example, is regulated by SEBI and must disclose its portfolio monthly. The fund's expense ratio is capped by SEBI rules, ensuring the fund house doesn't charge excessive fees.

Your trust's PAN (AACTK1234D) is used for all transactions, and the trust files its own tax return. When your Parag Parikh Flexi Cap Fund earns a 15.5% return, any gains are tracked and taxed according to the rules — LTCG up to ₹1 lakh may be tax-free, with anything above taxed at 10%.
