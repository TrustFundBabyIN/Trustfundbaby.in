# Module 7: Estate Planning & Wills
**Year:** 9 (Age 21)
**Month:** 7
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand what estate planning is and why it matters even at 21
- Learn the basics of writing a will under Indian law
- Know the key legal instruments: will, nomination, power of attorney, and trust
- Take your first estate planning steps while your estate is simple

## Key Concepts

### What Is Estate Planning — And Why Start at 21?

Estate planning is deciding what happens to your assets, responsibilities, and digital life if you're no longer around or capable of making decisions. At 21, this feels morbid. But here's the reality: you now own assets. Your trust fund — ₹3 lakh invested, potentially growing to lakhs and crores over your lifetime — is yours. Your bank accounts, investments, possibly a vehicle, and increasingly, your digital assets (crypto, online accounts, intellectual property) all need a plan.

Estate planning isn't about death. It's about control. Without a plan, Indian succession law decides who gets what — and it may not match your wishes. With a plan, you decide. The earlier you start (when your estate is simple), the easier it is to build the habit. As your wealth grows, the stakes get higher and the planning gets harder. Start now, when a one-page will covers everything.

Consider this: if something happened to you tomorrow, would your parents know where your investments are? Which bank accounts you hold? What insurance policies exist? Your login credentials for financial platforms? Estate planning is also about making things easier for the people you care about.

### Writing a Will Under Indian Law

A will is a legal document stating how your assets should be distributed after your death. In India, wills are governed by the Indian Succession Act, 1925.

**Requirements for a valid will:**
- You must be of sound mind and at least 18 years old (you qualify)
- It must be in writing (handwritten or typed — both valid)
- It must be signed by you
- It should be attested by at least two witnesses (who sign in your presence)
- No stamp paper required — plain paper is fine

**What to include in your will:**
1. Your name, address, date of birth — clear identification
2. Declaration that this is your last will, revoking all previous wills
3. List of assets: investments (mutual fund folios, demat accounts), bank accounts, insurance policies, any property
4. Beneficiaries: who gets what, with specific amounts or percentages
5. An executor: the person who will carry out your will's instructions
6. Guardian for any dependents (if applicable)
7. Your signature, date, and two witness signatures

**Registration:** A will doesn't need to be registered to be valid, but registering it at the sub-registrar's office adds a layer of legal protection. It costs a nominal fee and prevents disputes about authenticity.

**Updating:** You can (and should) update your will as your life changes — marriage, children, significant asset changes. Each new will should explicitly revoke previous versions.

### Nomination — Your First Line of Defense

Before writing a will, ensure all your financial accounts have nominations. A nominee is the person who receives the asset upon your death, acting as a custodian. Nominations bypass the often-lengthy probate process.

**Where to add nominations:**
- Mutual fund folios (add nominee on the AMC website or app)
- Bank accounts (fill nomination form at your branch)
- Demat accounts (nominee details in account opening or via DP)
- Insurance policies (nomination is mandatory for life insurance)
- PPF and NPS accounts
- EPF (through your employer)

Important nuance: under current law, a nominee is a trustee, not the absolute owner. The nominee holds the asset for the legal heirs. This is why a will matters — it clarifies your actual intent beyond the nomination.

### Power of Attorney — For When You're Alive but Unable

A Power of Attorney (POA) authorizes someone to act on your behalf. This isn't about death — it's about incapacity or absence.

**Types:**
- **General POA:** Broad authority for the agent to handle most financial/legal matters
- **Special/Specific POA:** Limited to specific actions (e.g., selling a particular property, operating a specific bank account)

At 21, you might grant a limited POA to a trusted parent or sibling if you're traveling abroad, working in a different city, or simply want someone to handle financial paperwork on your behalf. Keep it specific and time-bound. Revocable POAs can be cancelled anytime — use these by default.

### Digital Estate Planning — The New Frontier

Your digital life has value. Email accounts, social media, cloud storage, cryptocurrency wallets, online subscriptions, domain names — these are assets and liabilities. India doesn't have comprehensive digital estate law yet, which makes proactive planning essential.

Create a secure document (stored with your will or in a password manager's emergency access feature) listing:
- All financial accounts and platforms
- Login credentials or instructions for access
- Digital assets with monetary value (crypto, domains, online businesses)
- Instructions for social media (memorialize vs delete)
- Trusted person to handle digital estate

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Kabir, freshly 21 with ₹3 lakh in investments and a bootcamp ahead, decides to write his first will. It takes 30 minutes.

**Kabir's will (simplified):**
"I, Kabir Mehra, aged 21, residing at [address], declare this to be my last will.

1. I revoke all previous wills.
2. My assets: Mutual fund folio [number] with ₹3,00,000 invested, savings account [bank/number] with ₹12,000, term life insurance policy [number] with ₹25 lakh cover.
3. I bequeath all my assets to my parents, Ravi Mehra and Sunita Mehra, in equal share.
4. I appoint my father, Ravi Mehra, as executor.
5. If my parents predecease me, assets pass to my sister, Ananya Mehra.

Signed: Kabir Mehra
Date: [date]
Witness 1: [name, signature]
Witness 2: [name, signature]"

Simple. One page. Covers everything. As his wealth grows, he'll update it. But right now, this ensures his ₹3 lakh doesn't get stuck in legal limbo for months.

He also adds his father as nominee on all mutual fund folios and bank accounts — a 10-minute online process that could save months of paperwork for his family.

## Quiz
1. What is the minimum age to write a valid will in India? — A) 16 B) 18 C) 21 D) 25 (Answer: B)

2. How many witnesses are required for a valid will in India? — A) None B) 1 C) 2 D) 3 (Answer: C)

3. Is registration of a will mandatory in India? — A) Yes, always B) No, but recommended C) Only for property D) Only if contested (Answer: B)

4. What is the legal status of a nominee in India? — A) Absolute owner of the asset B) Trustee/custodian for legal heirs C) Co-owner with the account holder D) Has no legal standing (Answer: B)

5. What type of POA gives broad authority to handle financial matters? — A) Special POA B) General POA C) Digital POA D) Limited POA (Answer: B)
