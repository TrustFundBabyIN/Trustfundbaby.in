# Module 8: Protecting Your Wealth — Insurance & Risk Management
**Year:** 9 (Age 21)
**Month:** 8
**Reading Time:** ~10 minutes

## Learning Objectives
- Understand insurance as wealth protection, not just an expense
- Learn the difference between term life insurance, health insurance, and general insurance
- Calculate how much life and health coverage you need at each life stage
- Avoid common insurance mistakes that waste money or leave you underinsured

## Key Concepts

### Insurance Is Wealth Protection — Not a Waste of Money

You've spent nine years building wealth through Trust Fund Baby. Now it's time to protect it. Insurance isn't about betting on disaster — it's about ensuring that a single bad event (hospitalization, accident, disability, death) doesn't wipe out everything you've built.

Think of your wealth as a house. Investments are the rooms you're building — each one adds value. Insurance is the wall around the property. Without it, one flood can destroy everything inside. You don't build walls because you expect floods. You build them because you can't afford not to.

At 21, insurance premiums are at their cheapest. A term life insurance policy that costs ₹500/month at 21 costs ₹1,500/month at 35 for the same coverage. Health insurance that's ₹8,000/year at 21 can be ₹25,000/year at 40. Lock in low premiums now.

### Term Life Insurance — The Only Life Insurance You Need

Let's kill a myth: endowment plans, money-back policies, and ULIPs are terrible investments disguised as insurance. They combine mediocre insurance with mediocre returns and charge you heavily for the privilege. Avoid them completely.

What you need is pure term life insurance. Term insurance pays a lump sum to your nominees if you die during the policy term. That's it. No maturity value, no returns, no complexity. And that's exactly why it's cheap — you're paying only for protection.

**How much coverage?** A common rule: 10–15 times your annual income. If you earn ₹5 lakh/year, get ₹50–75 lakh coverage. As your income grows, increase coverage. At 21, even a ₹50 lakh policy costs just ₹400–600/month. That's less than a Netflix + Spotify subscription.

**When do you need it?** Technically, you need term insurance when someone depends on your income. At 21, with no dependents, the urgency is lower. But here's the strategic play: buying at 21 locks in a low premium for 30–40 years. When you do have dependents (parents aging, future spouse/children), the policy is already in place at rates no 35-year-old can get.

**Where to buy:** Buy online directly from insurer websites (HDFC Life, ICICI Prudential, Max Life, Tata AIA). Online term plans are 30–40% cheaper than offline (agent-sold) plans. Compare on PolicyBazaar or Ditto (free advisory, no spam).

### Health Insurance — The Non-Negotiable

If you buy only one insurance product at 21, make it health insurance. A single hospitalization in India can cost ₹2–5 lakh for routine procedures and ₹15–30 lakh for serious conditions. Without health insurance, that money comes from your investments — potentially undoing years of wealth building.

**Employer health insurance is NOT enough.** Company policies cover you only while employed. They vanish when you switch jobs, get laid off, or take a break. And coverage is often inadequate (₹2–3 lakh when you need ₹10+ lakh). Buy a personal health insurance policy that stays with you regardless of employment status.

**How much coverage?** Minimum ₹10 lakh in a metro city, ₹5 lakh in a smaller city. Consider a super top-up plan for an additional ₹25–50 lakh at very low cost. Family floater plans cover your spouse and children under one policy.

**Key features to look for:**
- No room rent sub-limits (or at least single private room)
- No co-payment clause
- Short waiting period for pre-existing diseases (2–4 years)
- Cashless network hospitals in your city
- Restoration benefit (sum insured resets after a claim)
- No claim bonus (coverage increases for every claim-free year)

**Buy young, buy early.** A health insurance policy bought at 21 with no pre-existing conditions means you pass the waiting period before you're likely to need it. Wait until 30, and any health condition you develop becomes a "pre-existing disease" with a 2–4 year waiting period.

### Other Insurance — What's Worth It and What's Not

**Motor insurance:** Mandatory by law if you own a vehicle. Third-party insurance is the minimum; comprehensive is better. Buy online for better rates.

**Personal accident insurance:** Covers disability and accidental death. Very cheap (₹1,000–2,000/year for ₹25 lakh coverage). Worth adding to your portfolio.

**Home/renters insurance:** Covers your belongings against theft, fire, natural disasters. Costs ₹2,000–5,000/year. Worth it if you have expensive electronics or furniture.

**Travel insurance:** Essential for international travel. Covers medical emergencies, trip cancellation, lost luggage. Buy per-trip or annual multi-trip plans.

**Avoid:** Credit card insurance, loan protection plans, cancer-specific plans (comprehensive health insurance covers cancer), child insurance plans (invest in mutual funds instead).

### The Insurance + Investment Separation Rule

Insurance and investment are separate functions. Never mix them. "Investment-cum-insurance" products (ULIPs, endowments, money-back) give you poor insurance coverage AND poor investment returns while charging high fees.

The right approach: buy term insurance for protection, invest in mutual funds for wealth creation. ₹500/month term insurance + ₹5,000/month mutual fund SIP always beats a ₹5,500/month ULIP. Always.

## Real-World Example (From Your Trust)

Kabir, now 21, maps out his insurance portfolio:

**Term life insurance:** ₹50 lakh coverage, 40-year term. Premium: ₹550/month (bought online from HDFC Life). Nominee: parents. He doesn't need it yet (no dependents), but locks in the 21-year-old rate. By 35, the same policy would cost ₹1,400/month. He saves over ₹4 lakh in premiums over the policy's life just by buying early.

**Health insurance:** ₹10 lakh individual policy from Niva Bupa. Premium: ₹8,500/year. Includes cashless hospitalization, no room rent limit, 2-year waiting period for pre-existing diseases. He also adds a ₹25 lakh super top-up for ₹2,000/year. Total health coverage: ₹35 lakh for ₹10,500/year.

**Personal accident:** ₹25 lakh coverage for ₹1,500/year. Covers accidental death and permanent disability.

**Total insurance cost:** approximately ₹17,600/year — about ₹1,467/month. That's 3% of his expected ₹50,000/month post-bootcamp income. A small price for protecting a lifetime of wealth building.

His trust fund ₹3 lakh? Protected. His future earnings? Protected. His family's financial stability? Protected. That's insurance working as it should.

## Quiz
1. What type of life insurance provides the highest coverage for the lowest premium? — A) ULIP B) Endowment plan C) Term insurance D) Money-back policy (Answer: C)

2. What is a common rule for how much life insurance coverage you need? — A) 2x annual income B) 5x annual income C) 10–15x annual income D) 50x annual income (Answer: C)

3. Why should you NOT rely solely on employer health insurance? — A) It's too expensive B) It vanishes when you leave the job C) It covers too much D) It doesn't cover hospitals (Answer: B)

4. What is the advantage of buying health insurance at 21? — A) You're already sick B) Pre-existing disease waiting period passes before you likely need it C) It's free D) No claim is possible at 21 (Answer: B)

5. What is the "insurance + investment separation rule"? — A) Buy insurance from one company, invest with another B) Keep insurance for protection and mutual funds for investment — never mix C) Invest your insurance premiums D) Don't buy insurance, only invest (Answer: B)
